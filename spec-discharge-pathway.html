Evidence Base — Why This Pathway Works

Children's Hospital Colorado's Ventilator Care Program (VCP) demonstrated that a standardized discharge process significantly reduced length of stay while maintaining safety. Their model uses weekly interdisciplinary rounds, a Discharge Readiness Report, a Caregiver Skills Checklist, and mandatory high-fidelity simulation training. Children's Wisconsin uses a "Stepping Stones" model with defined phase-gate milestones. This SPEC pathway integrates both frameworks, adapted for our resources, distances, and patient population in West Texas and Eastern New Mexico.

0
Before Procedure
Decision-Making & Family Goals Conference
Timeline: Before trach/vent/G-tube placement
▾

This phase is the foundation of the entire journey. Families must understand what they are consenting to — not just the surgery, but the months of training and lifelong care responsibilities. A formal goals-of-care conference is required before proceeding.

📋 What Must Happen

  • Goals-of-care family conference completed with attending MD
  • Family understands indications, alternatives, and long-term implications
  • Comfort care option discussed openly and without judgment
  • At least 2 committed caregivers identified
  • SW assessment of home environment, support system, literacy
  • Insurance and DME coverage confirmed by case management
  • Family provided "What to Expect" education booklet
  • Consent obtained — tracheostomy, ventilator, G-tube as applicable

👥 Team Responsibilities

MD Lead family conference; explain medical necessity, prognosis, alternatives
RN Introduce family to trach/vent concept; provide written materials; assess family readiness
SW Full family assessment: social support, home environment, transportation, language barriers
CM Begin DME identification; confirm insurance for vent, trach supplies, G-tube supplies; identify home nursing agency
RT Introduce home ventilator concept; assess family questions about breathing support
🌵

West Texas Note: Many families drive 2–4 hours for appointments. Confirm family has a plan for how they will attend training sessions. Begin DME contact early — rural West Texas and Eastern NM have limited home nursing availability; delays in identifying PDN can extend hospitalization by weeks.

1
ICU Phase
Surgical Recovery & Medical Stabilization
Timeline: Post-op days 1–14 (PICU)
▾

The PICU phase focuses on medical stability. The first trach tube change by ENT typically occurs days 5–7. Education begins immediately — even at the bedside, observation is learning.

🏥 Medical Milestones (Gate Criteria)

  • Trach stoma healed, first tube change by ENT (days 5–7)
  • Hemodynamically stable, no continuous IV drips
  • FiO₂ ≤ 0.40 for ≥ 2 weeks (per CHCO criteria)
  • No significant hypoxemia episodes for ≥ 2 weeks
  • Ventilator settings acceptable for home device
  • G-tube stoma healed, feeds tolerated (if applicable)
  • Home ventilator trial initiated and tolerated

📖 Family Education Begins

  • Family present at bedside daily — observation encouraged
  • Anatomy reviewed: trachea, stoma, how trach tube works
  • Suctioning demonstrated by RN/RT — family watches
  • Ambu bag shown and explained — always at bedside
  • Family given SPEC education notebook
  • CPR class scheduled
🚦
Phase Gate: Ready to Progress to Step-Down?

Medical stability confirmed at weekly team rounds. All gate criteria above must be met. Transfer to step-down (or inpatient floor with dedicated trach/vent education space) where education moves into intensive mode.

2
Step-Down / Floor
Observation & Assisted Skill Building
Timeline: Weeks 1–3 of formal education
▾

Families transition from watching to doing — with full staff support at their side. The goal is comfort and repetition. No caregiver should feel rushed. Two caregivers must progress in parallel through all modules.

🫁 Tracheostomy Skills

  • Watch staff perform trach care × 3 encounters
  • Assisted suctioning — caregiver performs with staff present
  • Stoma cleaning and dressing change — demonstrated
  • Trach tie change — performed with 2-person technique
  • Inner cannula care (if applicable)
  • Ambu bag ventilation practiced on mannequin

💨 Ventilator & G-Tube Skills

  • Home vent orientation — parts, displays, settings overview
  • Circuit assembly practiced × 3 with RT supervision
  • Humidifier setup and water refill (distilled water only)
  • Common alarms reviewed — what each means and first response
  • G-tube care, feeding setup, rate and schedule reviewed (if applicable)
  • G-tube medication administration (if applicable)
⚠️
Teach-Back Required

After every skill, use teach-back: "Can you show me how you would do that?" or "What would you do if this alarm went off?" Document teach-back results in the caregiver education log. Proceed only when the family can explain and demonstrate correctly — not just when they say "I understand."

🌵

West Texas Note: If primary caregivers are taking turns driving to Lubbock, coordinate education schedules around their presence. Consider telehealth review sessions for skills they've already demonstrated, to reduce unnecessary travel. Both caregivers must achieve full competency before any discharge planning begins.

3
Active Education
Independent Skill Practice & Emergency Scenarios
Timeline: Weeks 3–7 of formal education
▾

Families now perform all routine care with staff observing — not helping. Emergency scenarios are introduced. CPR must be completed. This phase builds the confidence caregivers need to feel like their child's primary care expert.

🔁 Skills to Complete Independently

  • Trach suctioning — independently × 5 supervised sessions
  • Trach tube change (with 2nd caregiver) — supervised × 3
  • Complete trach care routine independently
  • Ventilator circuit setup — independently
  • Respond to 5 different ventilator alarms appropriately
  • Full G-tube feeding session independently (if applicable)
  • Medication administration via G-tube independently
  • CPR completed — both caregivers certified

🚨 Emergency Scenarios to Practice

  • Scenario 1: Accidental decannulation — replace trach tube
  • Scenario 2: Mucus plug / obstructed trach — recognize, suction, change tube if needed
  • Scenario 3: Vent malfunction — disconnect, manually ventilate with Ambu, troubleshoot
  • Scenario 4: Power outage — vent to battery, call 911 protocol
  • Scenario 5: Child in distress — full emergency response chain (check → suction → Ambu → 911)
  • Scenario 6: G-tube dislodgement — recognize, plug stoma, seek care
🚨
Simulation Training — Mandatory Per CHCO Model

CHCO's published research showed that high-fidelity simulation was the most helpful component of caregiver training — ranked above videos, handouts, and even bedside teaching. At minimum: two mandatory simulation sessions (plugged trach and ventilator malfunction). Caregivers must demonstrate correct emergency response, not just describe it. Debrief each simulation with the family.

5
Independent Care
24–48 Hour Independent Caregiver Stay (Rooming-In)
Timeline: Weeks 9–11 · Must precede discharge
▾

This is the most important phase before discharge. Caregivers assume full responsibility for all of their child's care — with the nursing team and RT available down the hall for true emergencies only. This is not observed practice. This is the real thing, in the safety of the hospital.

🛏️ During Independent Stay

  • Both caregivers present for the full 24–48 hours
  • Caregivers perform ALL routine care without prompting
  • Caregivers manage all vent alarms independently
  • Night monitoring performed by caregivers (not nursing)
  • Full feed schedule managed by caregivers (G-tube)
  • Medication schedule managed by caregivers
  • Any unresolved issues → documented and addressed before discharge

✅ Discharge Planning (Parallel Track)

  • DME company confirmed — vent, suction, humidifier, oximeter delivered to home
  • Home nursing (PDN) scheduled — first night coverage confirmed
  • 30-day supply of all trach, vent, and G-tube supplies delivered
  • Follow-up appointments booked: ENT (1–2 weeks), Pulm (2–4 weeks), Peds GI (if G-tube)
  • Local utility company notified (life-support dependent registration)
  • Local EMS / 911 notified of child's address and needs
  • Emergency contact card prepared (what to say when calling 911)
  • SPEC care coordinator number given to family
🌵

West Texas Critical Gap: Private duty nursing (PDN) availability is the #1 cause of discharge delay in our region. Begin PDN identification at Phase 0, not Phase 5. Have case management contact Lubbock-area agencies immediately upon trach placement. If PDN cannot be arranged before discharge, discuss with attending whether a planned discharge with careful family monitoring and telehealth support is appropriate, on a case-by-case basis.

6
Discharge & Follow-Up
Discharge Day & Post-Discharge Support Plan
Timeline: Discharge day + ongoing
▾

Discharge is not the end — it is the beginning of the home chapter. A robust post-discharge support structure is essential, especially for families far from Lubbock.

📦 Discharge Day Checklist

  • All discharge medications dispensed and explained
  • Written care plan (trach, vent, G-tube) given to family in both English and Spanish as needed
  • Emergency action card laminated and sent home
  • Backup trach tubes (current size + one size smaller) in travel bag
  • Portable Ambu bag in travel bag
  • DME company 24-hr contact number in family's phone
  • SPEC family portal / website shared with family
  • 48-hour post-discharge phone call scheduled by RN

📅 Follow-Up Schedule

  • 48 hrs post-discharge: Phone check-in from PICU/pulm team
  • 1–2 weeks: ENT trach clinic — tube check, stoma assessment
  • 2–4 weeks: Pulmonology — vent settings review, home nursing check
  • 4 weeks: Peds GI — G-tube check, feeding tolerance (if applicable)
  • Monthly × 3 months: Complex care/pulm follow-up
  • Ongoing: Telehealth available for families > 90 min from Lubbock
🏠
West Texas Telehealth Bridge

Families from Midland, Odessa, Amarillo, El Paso, and rural Eastern NM face 2–5 hour drives for follow-up. Establish a telehealth protocol at discharge: use video visits for routine follow-up, with in-person visits reserved for tube changes, scope exams, and any acute concerns. Coordinate with the child's local pediatrician to serve as the on-the-ground partner.

Regional Context

West Texas Adaptations to the CHCO Model

The CHCO model was built for a large urban academic center. We have adapted it for the realities of West Texas and Eastern New Mexico — vast distances, limited home nursing, and families who are driving hours to be at the bedside.

🚗 Distance & Travel

Many families drive 2–5 hours. Coordinate education sessions around family presence. Use telehealth for review sessions. Never require a family to return to Lubbock just for a routine check that can be done by video or with their local pediatrician.

👩‍⚕️ Private Duty Nursing Shortage

PDN availability is the #1 discharge delay in our region — delays can exceed 440% longer than urban centers. Begin PDN identification the day trach placement is decided. Consider enhanced family training as a bridge when PDN hours are limited.

🌵 Dry Climate & Humidity

West Texas is one of the driest climates in the US. Humidifier water chambers deplete faster. Families must check water every 4 hours. This must be explicitly taught — not assumed. Mucus plugging risk is higher here than in humid regions.

⚡ Power Outages

Dust storms and West Texas weather cause outages. Every family needs: a registered ventilator-dependent status with their utility company, an external battery backup, and a documented power outage plan. Register with the utility before discharge.

🗣️ Language & Health Literacy

A significant portion of our patient population speaks Spanish as their primary language. All written materials must be available in Spanish. Ensure a certified medical interpreter is used for all teaching sessions — not family members as interpreters.

🏥 Local EMS Preparation

Many families live in towns with limited EMS experience with pediatric trach/vent patients. Proactively notify local EMS with a child's home address, diagnosis, and emergency action plan. Some rural Texas EMS agencies welcome a brief phone orientation from the PICU team.

Your Family's Journey · Step by Step

Your Road to
Going Home

Learning to care for your child with a tracheostomy, ventilator, and feeding tube is a journey. You will not do it alone — your care team walks every step with you. Here is what the path looks like.

You are the most important member of your child's care team. The more time you spend learning in the hospital, the more confident and ready you will feel at home.

Your Journey: Step by Step

Each step must be completed before moving to the next. Take your time — there is no rush.

💬
Step 1
The Big Conversation
Before any procedure

Before anything happens, your family and your care team will sit down together. This is the time to ask every question, understand all the options — including comfort care — and decide together what is right for your child and your family. There is no wrong answer.

  • What a tracheostomy, ventilator, and G-tube each do
  • What life at home will look like
  • What other paths are available (including comfort care)
  • Your family's values and goals
🏥
Step 2
Surgery & Recovery in the PICU
Days 1–14 after surgery

After the procedure, your child will recover in the PICU. This is a time for healing — and also your first chance to start learning. Come to the bedside as much as you can. Watch the nurses and respiratory therapists care for your child. Start asking questions.

  • Your child's anatomy — how the trach tube and stoma work
  • What suctioning looks like and why it's important
  • Introduction to the Ambu bag (emergency breathing bag)
  • Beginning your education notebook
📖
Step 3
Learning by Watching, Then Doing
Weeks 1–3 of training

Now your real training begins. First you watch — then you try with your nurse or respiratory therapist right beside you. Every skill takes practice. You will do each one many times before you do it on your own. That is completely normal.

  • How to suction your child's trach — safely and confidently
  • How to clean the stoma and change trach ties
  • How to set up the ventilator circuit and humidifier
  • How to run G-tube feeds and give medications through the tube
🚨
Step 4
Emergency Practice
Weeks 3–7 of training

This is one of the most important parts of your training. You will practice what to do in an emergency — using a training mannequin in a safe, supportive environment. Practicing emergencies before they happen means you will be calm and ready if they ever do.

  • What to do if the trach tube falls out
  • What to do if there is a mucus blockage
  • What to do if the ventilator has a problem
  • CPR training — both caregivers must be certified
  • When to call 911 vs. when to call your care team
✅
Step 5
Your Skills Sign-Off
Weeks 7–9

Your care team will watch you perform each skill and officially sign off when you are ready. Both caregivers must complete this. If any skill needs more practice, that is okay — we just keep going until you feel confident and your team feels confident too.

  • All trach care skills performed correctly
  • All ventilator skills performed correctly
  • All G-tube skills performed correctly
  • All emergency scenarios handled correctly
  • CPR certification complete for both caregivers
🛏️
Step 6
You're In Charge — Rooming-In Night
24–48 hours (before discharge)

This is your trial run at home — but still in the safety of the hospital. For 24 to 48 hours, you and your co-caregiver take care of your child completely on your own. The nurses are available down the hall for a real emergency, but the day-to-day care is yours. Most parents say this is when everything clicks.

  • You manage all care — feeds, meds, trach care, vent monitoring
  • You handle the overnight monitoring
  • You respond to any alarms and handle any issues
  • You feel what home life will actually be like
🏠
Step 7
Going Home 🎉
Discharge day

You made it. Your equipment will already be at your home. Your supplies are packed. Your follow-up appointments are scheduled. Your emergency contact card is laminated and ready. You are not alone — your SPEC care team is just a phone call away, and your local pediatrician is your partner on the ground.

  • All equipment installed at home by your DME company
  • 30-day supply of all supplies waiting for you
  • Follow-up appointments already scheduled
  • Your SPEC care coordinator number saved in your phone
  • Emergency card in your wallet and on your fridge

Before You Leave the Hospital

📦

Equipment at Home

Your ventilator, suction machine, humidifier, pulse oximeter, and feeding pump will be delivered to your home before discharge. Your DME company will walk through each piece of equipment with you.

💊

Supplies for 30 Days

You will go home with at least 30 days of trach tubes, suction catheters, circuit tubing, humidifier chambers, G-tube supplies, and all medications. Keep track of your supply levels and order before you run out.

📅

Follow-Up Appointments

You'll have appointments within 2 weeks of going home: ENT to check your child's trach, Pulmonology to review vent settings, and Peds GI for the G-tube. These are already scheduled before you leave.

⚡

Register as Life-Support Dependent

Call your electric company and tell them your child is on a home ventilator. Most companies in Texas offer priority restoration during outages. This is free and could be life-saving.

🚒

Notify Local EMS

Let your local fire department and 911 know your child's address and that they use a trach and ventilator at home. Many EMS teams appreciate knowing this in advance so they can prepare.

📱

Your SPEC Care Team

Save your SPEC care coordinator number in your phone before you leave. We are available for questions, concerns, and guidance — you are never alone in caring for your child at home.

🚨 Emergency Response — Know This by Heart

If your child is struggling to breathe or something is wrong with the ventilator or trach, follow these steps immediately.

1Look at your child — are they breathing? What color are they? Are they distressed?
2Check the circuit — is it connected? Any visible problems? Check all connections.
3Suction the trach — a mucus plug can block the airway quickly.
4If trach fell out — replace immediately with the backup tube from your emergency bag.
5If vent is not working — disconnect and manually ventilate with your Ambu bag.
6If not resolved in 1–2 minutes — CALL 911 IMMEDIATELY while continuing to ventilate.

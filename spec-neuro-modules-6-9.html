Module 06 — Status Epilepticus

Learning Objectives — Module 6
By the end of this module, the learner will be able to:
  • Define convulsive and non-convulsive status epilepticus and explain why the 5-minute threshold matters
  • Execute the first-line, second-line, and third-line medication protocol for pediatric convulsive SE
  • Identify the most common etiologies of SE in children by age group
  • Recognize non-convulsive SE as a cause of unexplained altered mental status and describe the EEG criteria
  • Describe the role of continuous EEG monitoring in the PICU and identify high-risk populations
  • Apply the Neurocritical Care Society (NCS) pediatric SE treatment protocol at the bedside

Definitions & Time Thresholds

Status epilepticus (SE) is not simply a prolonged seizure — it is a medical emergency defined by failure of the normal seizure-termination mechanisms. The operational definitions are based on two time points: when ongoing seizure activity becomes pathologic (t1) and when irreversible injury risk begins (t2).

DefinitionDurationClinical Implication
Impending SE≥5 minutes of continuous seizure or ≥2 seizures without return to baselineTreat now — do not wait to see if it stops spontaneously. The probability of self-termination falls sharply after 5 minutes.
Established SE≥30 minutesFirst-line benzodiazepines have likely already been given; move to second-line therapy
Refractory SESE that continues after adequate first-line + second-line therapyRequires continuous infusion anesthesia; ICU admission; continuous EEG monitoring; urgent etiology workup
Super-refractory SESE that continues or recurs ≥24h after onset of anesthetic therapyHighest mortality risk; requires multidisciplinary team; consider immunotherapy, ketogenic diet, or surgical evaluation
Non-convulsive SE (NCSE)EEG-confirmed SE without prominent motor activityClinically silent or subtle; altered mental status alone may be the only sign; requires EEG to diagnose
Clinical Pearl — The 5-Minute Rule
"A seizure that has lasted 5 minutes is unlikely to stop on its own and is causing ongoing neuronal injury. Do not wait for 'one more minute.' The delay between seizure onset and first benzodiazepine administration is the single most modifiable factor in SE outcome."

Tiered Treatment Protocol (NCS / Pediatric SE Guidelines)

0
Stabilization — Simultaneous with Seizure Treatment
ABCs • Oxygen (target SpO&sub2; ≥95%) • IV or IO access • Bedside glucose (treat hypoglycemia immediately: D10W 2 mL/kg IV in neonates; D25W 2 mL/kg or D50W 1 mL/kg in older children) • 12-lead ECG if cause unknown • Send: BMP, Ca, Mg, antiepileptic drug levels, toxicology, CBC, blood culture if febrile
1
First-Line: Benzodiazepines (≤5 minutes from arrival)
IV/IO lorazepam: 0.1 mg/kg IV/IO (max 4 mg); may repeat once after 5 minutes
IM midazolam: 0.2 mg/kg IM (max 10 mg) — preferred when no IV access; non-inferior to IV lorazepam (RAMPART trial)
Intranasal midazolam: 0.2 mg/kg (max 10 mg) via MAD device; effective prehospital option
Rectal diazepam: 0.5 mg/kg (max 20 mg); now largely replaced by intranasal/IM routes
If seizure persists after two benzodiazepine doses → proceed to second-line immediately. Do not give a third benzo without escalating.
2
Second-Line: Antiseizure Medications (≤20 minutes)
Give one of the following (no head-to-head superiority demonstrated; ESETT trial showed equivalence for levetiracetam, fosphenytoin, and valproate):

Levetiracetam (Keppra): 60 mg/kg IV (max 4,500 mg) over 5–10 min — preferred at most centers; no cardiac monitoring required; minimal drug interactions
Fosphenytoin: 20 mg PE/kg IV (max 1,500 mg PE) at ≤3 mg PE/kg/min; requires cardiac monitoring for QTc and hypotension; avoid in sodium channelopathy
Sodium valproate: 40 mg/kg IV (max 3,000 mg) over 5–10 min; avoid if suspected mitochondrial disorder, liver disease, or pregnancy
Phenobarbital: 20 mg/kg IV (max 1,000 mg) at 1 mg/kg/min; first choice in neonates; monitor for respiratory depression
3
Third-Line: Refractory SE (≤40–60 minutes) — ICU Required
Continuous IV anesthetic infusion — titrate to EEG burst suppression:
Midazolam infusion: 0.05–2 mg/kg/h; first choice at most centers; rapid titration; relatively short context-sensitive half-life
Pentobarbital: load 5–15 mg/kg; infusion 0.5–5 mg/kg/h; deepest suppression; associated with ileus, hypotension, immune suppression
Ketamine infusion: 0.5–3 mg/kg/h; NMDA antagonist; bronchodilator; increasing evidence for refractory SE; maintains airway reflexes better than barbiturates
Propofol: use with extreme caution in children — propofol infusion syndrome (PRIS) risk with prolonged high-dose use; limit to <48h at <4 mg/kg/h
Continuous EEG (cEEG) monitoring is mandatory for all patients on anesthetic infusions.

Non-Convulsive SE: The Hidden Diagnosis

NCSE must be considered in any child with unexplained altered mental status, subtle motor activity (eye deviation, rhythmic eye blinking, facial twitching), or failure to return to baseline after an apparent seizure. It cannot be diagnosed at the bedside — EEG is required.

  • High-risk populations for NCSE: comatose PICU patients, post-cardiac arrest, post-convulsive SE, TBI, CNS infection, metabolic encephalopathy, known epilepsy with AMS
  • Up to 30–50% of PICU patients with unexplained altered consciousness have electrographic seizures on cEEG — most are non-convulsive
  • Subtle signs to watch for: gaze deviation, nystagmus, rhythmic facial twitching, autonomic changes (tachycardia, hypertension), lip smacking without other movement
  • A child who does not return to baseline 30 minutes after a convulsive seizure should have urgent EEG placed to rule out NCSE
  • Treating empirically with benzodiazepines without EEG is reasonable in high-suspicion cases but does not replace monitoring
Case Vignette — Status Epilepticus
5-Year-Old with Febrile Status Epilepticus
A 5-year-old with no prior seizure history arrives by EMS actively seizing. Parents report the child had a fever of 39.2°C for 12 hours before the seizure started. EMS gave no medications. Time from seizure onset is approximately 12 minutes. He is convulsing with generalized tonic-clonic activity. SpO&sub2; 94% on 10L mask. No IV access.

Questions: What is your immediate treatment? What route? When do you move to second-line? What workup do you initiate and when?

Immediate treatment: IM midazolam 0.2 mg/kg (max 10 mg) to the lateral thigh — fastest route when no IV access. Simultaneously attempt IV/IO access.

While IM midazolam takes effect: Oxygen to maintain SpO&sub2; ≥95%. Fingerstick glucose (treat if <60 mg/dL). Do not delay glucose check. Alert team to prepare for intubation if airway not maintained.

If still seizing at 5 minutes after IM midazolam: Give IV lorazepam 0.1 mg/kg if IV now established. This is the second benzodiazepine dose.

If still seizing after two benzodiazepine doses (~20 minutes in): Move immediately to second-line. Give levetiracetam 60 mg/kg IV (max 4,500 mg) over 5–10 minutes. Notify ICU team.

Workup in parallel: BMP, Ca, Mg, glucose, CBC, blood culture (febrile). LP should be performed after seizure control and clinical stabilization — not during active convulsion. In a 5-year-old with first febrile seizure, LP is indicated if: age <12 months, or >12 months without full vaccination, or meningeal signs present, or complex features. This child — 5 years, no prior seizure, febrile, >5 minutes = febrile SE, not simple febrile seizure. LP is indicated after stabilization; empiric antibiotics + acyclovir if any concern for meningitis/HSV.

A 3-year-old is admitted with febrile SE, received two doses of lorazepam and one dose of levetiracetam, and is now seizure-free but remains obtunded 45 minutes after last seizure. The most appropriate next step is:
  • A. Observe for 30 more minutes and reassess
  • B. Urgent EEG to rule out non-convulsive SE
  • C. Give a third benzodiazepine dose empirically
  • D. Intubate electively for airway protection
B is correct. Failure to return to baseline 30–45 minutes after convulsive seizure cessation should prompt urgent EEG to rule out NCSE. Up to 30–50% of PICU patients with unexplained AMS have electrographic seizures. A third benzodiazepine (C) without confirmed ongoing seizure activity is inappropriate and increases respiratory depression risk. Observation alone (A) is unsafe at 45 minutes post-ictally. Intubation (D) may ultimately be needed but the diagnostic priority is EEG.

Module 07 — Pediatric Stroke

Learning Objectives — Module 7
By the end of this module, the learner will be able to:
  • Recognize the clinical presentation of ischemic and hemorrhagic stroke in children across age groups including neonates
  • Explain why pediatric stroke is frequently misdiagnosed and delayed, and identify the most common mimics
  • Initiate the appropriate diagnostic workup including urgent neuroimaging for suspected acute pediatric stroke
  • Describe the major risk factors for pediatric arterial ischemic stroke and cerebral sinovenous thrombosis
  • Outline acute management principles including the role of thrombolysis and thrombectomy in pediatric stroke
  • Apply trauma-specific stroke considerations including traumatic dissection and post-traumatic vasospasm
⚠️
Pediatric stroke is systematically under-recognized. The median delay from symptom onset to diagnosis in children is 24–28 hours — compared to <3 hours in adults. The most common reason: the diagnosis was not considered. Children do have strokes. Stroke must be on the differential for any child with acute focal neurological deficit, sudden severe headache, or unexplained altered mental status.

Epidemiology & Why Children Are Different

  • Incidence: approximately 2–13 per 100,000 children per year for arterial ischemic stroke (AIS); higher in neonates (1 in 2,300–5,000 live births)
  • Pediatric stroke has different risk factor profiles from adult stroke: congenital heart disease, sickle cell disease, arteriopathies, hypercoagulable states, infection-triggered, and trauma are dominant in children vs. atherosclerosis and hypertension in adults
  • Neonatal stroke is most commonly perinatal arterial ischemic stroke — often presenting as neonatal seizures, not focal deficits, because neonatal brains don’t manifest focal signs the way older children’s do
  • Childhood stroke has a recurrence rate of 15–20% — identifying the etiology and starting secondary prevention is critical

Top Risk Factors by Category

  • Cardiac: Congenital heart disease (especially cyanotic), arrhythmia, cardiomyopathy, endocarditis, post-cardiac surgery
  • Hematologic: Sickle cell disease (highest stroke risk of any pediatric condition — 10% lifetime risk without prophylaxis), thrombophilia (Factor V Leiden, protein C/S deficiency), iron deficiency anemia
  • Arteriopathy: Focal cerebral arteriopathy (most common in previously healthy children), moyamoya disease, dissection (traumatic or spontaneous), vasculitis
  • Infection-triggered: VZV arteriopathy (stroke weeks after chickenpox), meningitis, COVID-19-associated coagulopathy
  • Trauma: Cervical arterial dissection (often without obvious external trauma), carotid dissection after minor neck hyperextension

Clinical Presentation: FAST Is Not Enough

The adult FAST (Face, Arm, Speech, Time) mnemonic misses many pediatric stroke presentations. Children may present with:

Focal Motor
Hemiplegia, facial droop, arm drift, monoplegia. May be subtle in infants — prefer use of one hand, asymmetric crawling.
Speech / Language
Aphasia, dysarthria, sudden inability to speak or understand. Often misidentified as confusion.
Sudden Severe Headache
"Worst headache of life" = subarachnoid hemorrhage until proven otherwise. Thunder-clap onset is the key feature.
Vision Changes
Hemianopia, diplopia, sudden monocular vision loss (amaurosis fugax = TIA).
Altered Consciousness
GCS decline, drowsiness, or coma can be the dominant presentation in posterior circulation stroke or sinovenous thrombosis.
Neonatal Seizures
Focal clonic seizures in a neonate = perinatal stroke until MRI proves otherwise. Focal deficits appear later as preferential handedness.

Acute Management

1
Activate Stroke Protocol & Urgent Neuroimaging
CT brain without contrast: First-line to rule out hemorrhage (fast, available 24/7). CT does not reliably show acute ischemia <6h but is the gate-keeper before thrombolysis.
MRI brain with DWI: Gold standard for ischemic stroke — DWI positive within minutes of ischemia. If clinical suspicion high, go directly to MRI with DWI if immediately available.
CT angiography or MRA: Identifies large vessel occlusion (LVO) amenable to thrombectomy, arteriopathy, dissection.
2
Supportive Care — Prevent Secondary Injury
Maintain SpO&sub2; ≥95% • Avoid hyperthermia (temperature ≥37.5°C worsens ischemia — treat actively) • Target normoglycemia (both hypo- and hyperglycemia worsen outcome) • Maintain age-appropriate BP — avoid aggressive hypotension acutely in ischemic stroke (permissive hypertension in acute phase) • Avoid hypovolemia • Seizure management per Module 6 protocol
3
Antithrombotic Therapy
Aspirin 3–5 mg/kg/day (max 81–100 mg): recommended as initial antithrombotic for pediatric AIS in most etiologies; start after hemorrhage excluded by neuroimaging
Anticoagulation (heparin or LMWH): preferred for cardioembolism, cervical arterial dissection, and cerebral sinovenous thrombosis (CSVT) — including when hemorrhagic transformation present in CSVT
Sickle cell disease: Exchange transfusion to rapidly lower HbS to <30% is the most urgent intervention; NOT thrombolytics
4
IV tPA & Mechanical Thrombectomy — Know the Evidence
IV alteplase (tPA): Not FDA-approved for pediatric AIS; limited case series data; considered case-by-case at experienced stroke centers for children with significant LVO within 4.5h window after hemorrhage excluded and with no absolute contraindications. Call pediatric neurology and neurosurgery immediately.
Mechanical thrombectomy: Growing evidence supports use for pediatric LVO with accessible anatomy; consult neurointerventional radiology urgently for suspected LVO; no strict age lower limit in experienced hands.
Both decisions require immediate consultation — do not delay by waiting for a complete workup.
Trauma Pearl — Cervical Arterial Dissection
"Traumatic cervical arterial dissection can follow minor neck trauma — a chiropractic manipulation, a sports injury, or even vigorous vomiting. The stroke may present hours to days after the inciting event. In any child or adolescent with acute ischemic stroke, ask specifically about recent neck trauma, even trivial. Ipsilateral neck pain, Horner syndrome, and pulsatile tinnitus are clues. Diagnosis requires CT angiography or MRA of the neck."
A 9-year-old with sickle cell disease (HbSS) presents with sudden left-sided hemiplegia and aphasia for 45 minutes. CT brain is negative for hemorrhage. The most urgent next intervention is:
  • A. IV alteplase 0.9 mg/kg
  • B. Aspirin 81 mg orally
  • C. Emergency exchange transfusion to reduce HbS to <30%
  • D. Heparin infusion at full anticoagulation doses
C is correct. In sickle cell disease, acute stroke is driven by HbS polymerization and sickling-related vaso-occlusion. Emergency exchange transfusion to reduce HbS to <30% is the cornerstone of acute treatment — it reduces sickle cell concentration while maintaining hemoglobin level. Simple transfusion alone risks hyperviscosity. IV alteplase (A) is not indicated and may increase hemorrhagic risk in SCD. Aspirin alone (B) is insufficient for the acute phase. Heparin (D) is not first-line for SCD stroke.

🚫
Do not delay antibiotics for neuroimaging in suspected bacterial meningitis. If the child is febrile, ill-appearing, has meningismus, or has a petechial rash — give antibiotics (and dexamethasone) immediately. LP can follow if neuroimaging clears herniation risk. Blood cultures before antibiotics if obtainable within 10 minutes without delaying treatment, but do NOT delay treatment to obtain cultures.

LP Safety: When to Image First

Perform CT before LP if any of:

  • Focal neurological deficit
  • Papilledema on fundoscopy
  • GCS ≤12 or rapidly deteriorating
  • New onset seizure in the setting of suspected meningitis
  • Immunocompromised state
  • History of CNS disease (mass, hydrocephalus, prior neurosurgery)

Safe to LP without CT if ALL of:

  • No focal deficit
  • No papilledema
  • GCS ≥13 and stable
  • No immunocompromise
  • No prior CNS disease
  • Normal CT does not guarantee safe LP — herniation can occur with normal imaging

Anti-NMDAR Encephalitis: The Teenage Psychiatric Mimic

Anti-NMDAR encephalitis is the most common autoimmune encephalitis and is frequently misdiagnosed as a primary psychiatric disorder. The clinical course is distinctive and recognition is essential because it is highly treatable.

1
Prodrome (days 1–5)
Flu-like illness, fever, headache. Often attributed to viral illness and missed.
2
Psychiatric Phase (days 5–14)
Behavioral change, agitation, paranoia, hallucinations, insomnia. Most patients are admitted to psychiatry first. Antipsychotics have limited effect and may worsen dyskinesias.
3
Neurological Phase (weeks 2–4)
Seizures, movement disorders (orofacial dyskinesias, choreoathetosis), decreased level of consciousness, autonomic instability (temperature dysregulation, hemodynamic lability, hypersalivation). May require ICU admission.
4
Recovery (weeks to months with treatment)
Gradual with immunotherapy. Recovery can be prolonged. Relapse occurs in 12–25% — requires maintenance therapy. In teenage girls: always order pelvic ultrasound for ovarian teratoma.
Clinical Pearl — HSV Encephalitis
"Give acyclovir first, ask questions later. A child with fever + behavioral change + seizures + temporal lobe signal on MRI has HSV encephalitis until proven otherwise. Delay in acyclovir treatment is directly associated with worse neurological outcome. The drug is safe; withholding it is not. Start IV acyclovir 20 mg/kg q8h (neonates) or 10–20 mg/kg q8h (>3 months) immediately when HSV encephalitis is on the differential."
A 16-year-old girl is admitted from psychiatry after 2 weeks of progressive behavioral change, insomnia, and auditory hallucinations unresponsive to antipsychotics. She is now having orofacial movements and had one generalized seizure. CSF shows 20 lymphocytes, normal glucose, and normal protein. MRI brain is normal. The most important next diagnostic step is:
  • A. Repeat MRI in 48 hours
  • B. Psychiatric evaluation and medication adjustment
  • C. Serum and CSF anti-NMDAR antibody panel; pelvic ultrasound
  • D. Empiric antiepileptic medication only
C is correct. This is the classic presentation of anti-NMDAR encephalitis: subacute psychiatric prodrome in a teenage girl, followed by seizures, orofacial dyskinesias, and autonomic instability, with a CSF pleocytosis. Normal MRI does not exclude the diagnosis — MRI is normal in approximately 50% of cases. Serum AND CSF NMDAR antibodies should be sent (CSF is more sensitive). Pelvic ultrasound is mandatory in teenage girls to identify ovarian teratoma (present in ~50%), which must be removed as part of treatment. Starting immunotherapy (IVIG + steroids) while awaiting results is appropriate in high-suspicion cases.

Definition & Rapid GCS Assessment

Altered mental status (AMS) is any change from a child's baseline level of consciousness, cognition, behavior, or responsiveness. It is a symptom, not a diagnosis. The goal of the initial evaluation is simultaneous stabilization and identification of the life-threatening reversible causes.

First 15 Minutes: Simultaneous Stabilization & Workup

0–2 min
Airway, Breathing, Circulation
GCS ≤8 → prepare for intubation. O&sub2; to maintain SpO&sub2; ≥95%. IV/IO access. Cardiac monitor. Pulse oximetry. Capnography if intubated.
2–5 min
Bedside Glucose — Non-Negotiable
Check glucose immediately. If <60 mg/dL (neonates <45): treat NOW — D10W 2 mL/kg IV (neonates); D25W 2 mL/kg or D50W 1 mL/kg (older children). Recheck in 15 minutes. Do not delay this step for any other part of the workup.
5–10 min
Empiric Treatments for Reversible Causes
Opioid toxidrome (miosis, bradypnea, AMS): Naloxone 0.01 mg/kg IV/IM/IN (max 0.4 mg); repeat every 2–3 min if needed; shorter half-life than most opioids — patient may re-narcotize
Seizure — if any suspicion of ongoing SE: Lorazepam 0.1 mg/kg IV per SE protocol (Module 6)
Benzodiazepine toxicity: Flumazenil with extreme caution — may precipitate seizures in BZD-dependent patients; generally avoid
TCA overdose: Sodium bicarbonate 1–2 mEq/kg if wide QRS or hypotension
10–15 min
Rapid History, Targeted Exam & Directed Workup
History: time of onset, progression, preceding events, medications at home, possible ingestion, fever, trauma, prior seizures, prior neurological diagnoses, VP shunt
Exam: pupil size and reactivity (miosis = opioid/pontine; mydriasis = anticholinergic/herniation; unequal = herniation until proven otherwise), fundoscopy (papilledema, retinal hemorrhages in AHT), meningismus, lateralizing signs, posturing
Workup: BMP + Ca + Mg, LFTs, ammonia, CBC, blood culture if febrile, toxicology screen (urine + serum), ABG/VBG, cortisol if adrenal crisis possible; CT brain if trauma/focal signs/shunt; urgent EEG if NCSE suspected

AMS in the Trauma Patient

AMS in a trauma patient demands a simultaneous primary survey and neuro assessment. Do not anchor on the obvious injury and miss a concurrent head injury — or vice versa.

  • GCS trend matters more than a single value: A GCS of 13 that is falling is more concerning than a GCS of 10 that is improving. Document and trend serially every 15 minutes in the first hour.
  • Lucid interval = epidural hematoma until proven otherwise: A child who is initially alert after head trauma and then deteriorates has an expanding epidural hematoma until CT proves otherwise. Call neurosurgery immediately.
  • AHT (Abusive Head Trauma): Presentation may be subtle — vomiting, poor feeding, irritability, or AMS without any history of trauma. Retinal hemorrhages, metaphyseal fractures, and posterior subdural hematoma are clues. Low threshold for ophthalmology consultation and skeletal survey.
  • Post-traumatic seizures: Occur in 10–35% of severe TBI in children. Prophylactic levetiracetam is used at many centers for the first 7 days after severe TBI. Do not confuse a post-ictal state with primary TBI-related AMS without EEG confirmation.
  • Herniation signs = immediate action: Unequal pupils, Cushing’s triad (HTN + bradycardia + irregular breathing), extensor posturing → hyperventilate, mannitol or HTS bolus, elevate HOB 30°, emergent CT and neurosurgery (see TBI Module 4).
Case Vignette — AMS
2-Year-Old Found Unresponsive
A 2-year-old is brought in by EMS after being found unresponsive at home. Parents state she was “fine” one hour ago. No known medical history. No medications. Temperature 36.8°C. HR 58, BP 138/90. RR 8 and irregular. GCS: E1 V1 M2 = 4. Pupils: right 6mm minimally reactive, left 3mm reactive. No external injuries visible.

Question: What is your 60-second assessment and immediate action? What does the pupil exam tell you?

60-second assessment: GCS 4 with abnormal posturing → immediate airway management. The combination of bradycardia (HR 58) + hypertension (BP 138/90) + irregular respirations = Cushing’s triad = herniation until proven otherwise. This is a neurological emergency.

Pupil interpretation: Unequal pupils (anisocoria) with a dilated, minimally reactive right pupil = right third nerve (CN III) compression → right-sided uncal herniation. The herniation is ipsilateral to the dilated pupil. This means there is likely a right-sided expanding intracranial lesion compressing the right CN III against the tentorium.

Immediate actions (simultaneous): Call airway team + neurosurgery NOW. Intubate. Mannitol 1 g/kg IV bolus OR hypertonic saline 3% 5 mL/kg if no IV/IO. Hyperventilate to PaCO&sub2; 30–35 mmHg temporarily to buy time. Emergent CT brain. HOB 30 degrees.

On CT: Bilateral subdural hematomas with midline shift — in this age and presentation, with no history of trauma, this is AHT until proven otherwise. Call child protective services, ophthalmology (retinal hemorrhages), and social work. Skeletal survey.

The “no injuries visible” clue: AHT frequently has no external signs of trauma. The absence of bruising does not rule out non-accidental injury in a child with bilateral subdurals and herniation.

A 6-year-old with a known VP shunt for post-infectious hydrocephalus presents with 3 days of worsening headache and vomiting, now somnolent (GCS 12). The single most important next step is:
  • A. LP to rule out meningitis
  • B. Anti-emetics and IV fluids
  • C. CT brain and shunt series; immediate neurosurgery notification
  • D. MRI brain (preferred over CT for shunted patients)
C is correct. A VP-shunted child with progressive headache, vomiting, and declining GCS has shunt malfunction until proven otherwise. CT brain (fast, available) identifies ventricular enlargement or shunt hardware issues. Shunt series (plain X-rays of the entire shunt tract) identifies disconnection or kinking. Neurosurgery must be notified immediately — shunt revision may be needed urgently. LP (A) is contraindicated in the setting of possible shunt malfunction and elevated ICP. MRI (D) provides more detail but is slower and less immediately available; CT is appropriate first. Anti-emetics alone (B) in a shunted child with AMS is dangerous anchoring.

For Families: Courageous Parents Network

Neurocritical illness in children frequently leads to conversations about prognosis, goals of care, and — in the most serious cases — end-of-life decision-making. These conversations are among the most challenging in pediatrics, and families need more than a single bedside conversation. They need guides, resources, and a community that understands their experience.

Resource for Families & Clinicians
Courageous Parents Network
The Courageous Parents Network (CPN) is a nonprofit organization that provides free, expert-reviewed video guides, written resources, and community support for parents of children with serious illness. Their resources cover: navigating the ICU, understanding a child’s prognosis, goals-of-care conversations, making decisions about life-sustaining treatment, palliative care and hospice, sibling support, grief, and legacy-building. Resources are available in English and Spanish.
CPN resources are appropriate to share with families at any stage of serious illness — not only at end of life. Early introduction of palliative support resources improves family coping, reduces moral distress, and does not shorten life.

You Are Not Alone
If your child is in the ICU with a serious neurological illness, the Courageous Parents Network offers free video guides, written resources, and a community of other parents who understand this experience. Visit courageousparentsnetwork.org or ask your care team for printed materials. Resources are available in English and Spanish, and all content is reviewed by medical and palliative care experts.

Palliative Care Integration in Pediatric Neurocritical Illness

  • Palliative care is not the same as end-of-life care. It is specialized support for patients and families facing serious illness at any stage — including patients who are expected to survive and recover.
  • Early palliative involvement improves outcomes: Reduces parental anxiety, improves family-clinician communication, reduces moral distress in nursing staff, and does not shorten length of life.
  • Goals-of-care conversations should happen proactively, not only in crisis. A family that understands the range of possible outcomes is better prepared to make decisions aligned with their child’s values.
  • Avoid language that forecloses hope while also avoiding false certainty. “We will do everything possible while also making sure your child is comfortable and your family is supported” is not a contradiction — it is good medicine.
  • Document all goals-of-care conversations in the medical record, including who was present, what was discussed, and the family’s stated values and preferences.
⚡ Rapid Recall — Modules 6–9

12 Must-Know Points

1
Treat SE at 5 minutes
Seizure ≥5 min = status epilepticus. Give benzodiazepine immediately. Do not wait to see if it stops. IM midazolam when no IV access.
2
ESETT: three second-line agents are equivalent
Levetiracetam, fosphenytoin, and valproate showed no significant difference in the ESETT trial. Levetiracetam is preferred at most centers (no monitoring required).
3
NCSE: EEG is mandatory
Any child not returning to baseline 30 minutes post-ictally needs urgent EEG. NCSE cannot be diagnosed clinically.
4
Pediatric stroke: delay is the enemy
Median time to diagnosis in children is 24–28 hours. Stroke must be on the differential for any child with acute focal deficit. Todd’s paralysis is the most common mimic.
5
SCD stroke: exchange transfusion, not tPA
Emergency exchange transfusion to reduce HbS <30% is the primary treatment for ischemic stroke in sickle cell disease.
6
Traumatic dissection: minor trauma counts
Cervical arterial dissection can follow trivial neck trauma. Stroke hours to days later. Always ask about recent neck injury in pediatric stroke.
7
Antibiotics before LP in bacterial meningitis
If a child is ill-appearing with suspected bacterial meningitis, give antibiotics + dexamethasone FIRST. LP follows imaging safety check. Do not delay treatment.
8
Give acyclovir first, ask questions later
Fever + AMS + temporal lobe features = HSV encephalitis until proven otherwise. Start IV acyclovir immediately. Delay worsens outcome.
9
Anti-NMDAR: psychiatric mimic in teenage girls
Subacute behavioral change + seizures + orofacial dyskinesias = anti-NMDAR encephalitis. Send serum + CSF antibodies. Pelvic ultrasound for teratoma. Start immunotherapy.
10
Glucose first in any AMS
Fingerstick glucose is the first intervention in any child with AMS. Treat hypoglycemia immediately. Do not delay for any other workup.
11
Unequal pupils = herniation until proven otherwise
Anisocoria with a dilated, poorly reactive pupil = ipsilateral uncal herniation. Treat immediately: mannitol or HTS, hyperventilate, emergent CT, call neurosurgery.
12
CPN: introduce early, not only at end of life
Courageous Parents Network resources help families navigate serious illness at any stage. Early introduction reduces anxiety and improves shared decision-making.

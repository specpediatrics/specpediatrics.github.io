Sofia, age 3
Subglottic stenosis · Tracheostomy only
✓ Tracheostomy No Ventilator No G-Tube
🌿
Sofia's Mom speaks

Sofia was born breathing just fine, but when she was one, we noticed she always sounded like she was whistling when she breathed. The doctors found her airway had narrowed as she grew — a condition called subglottic stenosis. Her lungs work perfectly, her heart is strong, and she eats everything we put in front of her. She just needed a little opening to help air get through. The tracheostomy gave her that opening. She runs around the backyard in Lubbock, chases our dog, goes to preschool three days a week. The trach is just part of Sofia — like glasses are for some kids. Her surgeon says that as she grows, her airway may grow enough that we can close it one day. Until then, she's thriving.

Diagnosis
Subglottic stenosis
Technology
Tracheostomy tube only
Quality of Life
Attends preschool, plays normally
🍼
Marcus, age 18 months
Esophageal atresia repair · G-tube only
No Tracheostomy No Ventilator ✓ G-Tube
🌻
Marcus's Dad speaks

Marcus was born with his food pipe and breathing pipe not connected the way they should be — the doctors called it esophageal atresia. After his surgery, he breathes perfectly on his own. But eating? That's always been the hard part. He gets tired before he takes enough by mouth, and sometimes food goes the wrong way. The G-tube — the little button in his belly — means we can make sure he gets all his nutrition and his medications safely. He does still eat by mouth for practice and pleasure, but the G-tube carries the load. Marcus is the happiest baby you'll ever meet. He babbles, he laughs, he climbs everything in sight. The tube doesn't slow him down one bit.

Diagnosis
Esophageal atresia (repaired)
Technology
G-tube (MIC-KEY button)
Quality of Life
Active, social, oral feeding practice ongoing

Lily, age 6
SMA Type 2 · Tracheostomy + Home Ventilator
✓ Tracheostomy ✓ Ventilator No G-Tube
📚
Lily speaks for herself

My name is Lily and I am six years old. I have something called SMA — it makes my muscles weaker than other kids. My arms and legs don't work the way I want them to, but my brain is totally fine. My mom says I am the smartest person in our house, and I think she's right. I have a trach in my throat to help air get in, and I have a ventilator — we call it my breathing buddy — that helps me breathe, especially at night and when I'm sick. I go to first grade. I love math and reading chapter books. My ventilator comes with me on my wheelchair and we go everywhere together. My classmates all know about my trach and nobody thinks it's weird anymore. I want to be a doctor when I grow up, like the ones who help me.

Diagnosis
Spinal Muscular Atrophy Type 2
Technology
Trach + Home Ventilator (V-Home)
Quality of Life
Attends 1st grade, loves reading & math

Noah, age 4
Congenital central hypoventilation · Trach + Vent + G-tube
✓ Tracheostomy ✓ Ventilator ✓ G-Tube
❤️
Noah's Parents speak

When Noah was born, we were terrified. His brain doesn't send the right signals to breathe on its own — the doctors called it congenital central hypoventilation syndrome, or CCHS. Without support, Noah would stop breathing in his sleep. We had a lot of conversations with our team about what life could look like. We chose all three: a tracheostomy so the ventilator could connect directly, the ventilator to breathe for him at night and nap times, and a G-tube because eating safely was a challenge early on. We drove from Odessa to Lubbock for every appointment those first years. Was it hard? Incredibly. But watch Noah for five minutes and you'll see exactly why we chose this path. He is ferociously alive. He dances, he makes up songs, he tells jokes that are actually funny. The equipment is heavy. Our boy is heavier. He's worth every mile.

Diagnosis
CCHS (Ondine's Curse)
Technology
Trach + Ventilator + G-tube
Quality of Life
Dances, sings, tells jokes — fully engaged in life

Emma, 8 weeks old
Trisomy 18 · Comfort-focused care
Comfort Care Family-centered Palliative support
🕊️
Emma's parents speak

Emma came into this world with Trisomy 18. When we learned what that meant — that her body faced challenges that no surgery or machine could fully fix — we had the hardest conversations of our lives with our care team. We asked every question. We cried. We prayed. We talked to the palliative care team, to the chaplain, to other parents. And in the end, we chose to focus on what we could give Emma: every moment of love, warmth, and peace that we had. No ventilator. No tracheostomy. Not because we didn't love her enough — but because we loved her so completely that we wanted her life, however long, to be held in our arms rather than in a hospital room. Emma knew our voices. She knew our touch. She passed quietly eight weeks after she was born, in our home, surrounded by everyone who loved her. She was perfect. She still is. Choosing comfort care was not giving up — it was the bravest, most loving thing we ever did.

🤲 What comfort care means

Prioritizing your child's comfort, dignity, and quality of life. Focusing on pain relief, warmth, and family presence rather than life-prolonging technology.

💙 What it is NOT

It is not giving up. It is not loving less. It is not a failure. It is a courageous, considered, loving choice — one that many families make with deep intentionality.

👨‍👩‍👧 Who supports you

Palliative care teams, hospice nurses, chaplains, social workers, and child life specialists — all focused on supporting the whole family through this journey.

🌙 No right or wrong

Families who choose comfort care and families who choose full technology both love their children completely. Different paths, the same love.

There is no wrong answer.

Every family in these stories made their choice with love, information, and the guidance of their care team. The child with every technology and the child with none — both were held in the same fierce, complete love.

If you are in the middle of these conversations right now, you are not alone. These decisions are among the hardest any parent will ever face. Take your time. Ask every question. Bring your family, your faith, your values into the room.

Your care team is here to walk with you — not to decide for you.

Introduction

What Is a Gastrostomy Tube?

A gastrostomy tube — called a G-tube — is a small, flexible tube that goes through the belly directly into the stomach. It allows your child to receive nutrition, fluids, and medications safely when eating by mouth is difficult or impossible.

The G-tube passes through a small opening in the abdominal wall called a stoma. The tube is held in place inside the stomach by a small water-filled balloon or a bumper. On the outside, it sits flush or slightly raised against the skin.

G-tubes do not replace the mouth — children who have G-tubes can often still eat by mouth for pleasure and practice, in whatever amounts are safe and comfortable for them. The G-tube simply ensures they receive everything they need nutritionally, even on difficult days.

Anatomy — Where the G-Tube Sits

Why Children Need a G-Tube

Children may need a G-tube for many different reasons. Common conditions include:

  • Prematurity — babies born early who cannot yet coordinate suck-swallow-breathe safely
  • Neurological conditions — such as cerebral palsy, stroke, or brain injury that affect the swallowing reflex
  • Congenital anomalies — such as esophageal atresia, tracheoesophageal fistula, or cleft palate repair
  • Swallowing disorders — including silent aspiration (food going into the lungs without obvious coughing)
  • Failure to thrive — not gaining weight despite eating, due to high caloric needs or poor intake
  • Complex cardiac disease — infants who tire out during feeding before taking adequate volume
  • Cancer or other conditions requiring prolonged nutrition support

What to Expect — The Big Picture

A G-tube is placed surgically or endoscopically (called a PEG) in the operating room or procedure suite. Most children go home within 1–3 days of placement. The stoma — the opening in the skin — will be healed enough for routine care within 1–2 weeks.

💛
A Message for Families

Learning to use a G-tube can feel overwhelming at first. Every parent who does this today was once exactly where you are — scared, unsure, wondering if they could really do it. They all did. You will too. Your care team will teach you every skill before you leave the hospital, and you will not go home until you feel confident and ready.

Types of G-Tubes

Which Type Does My Child Have?

There are several types of gastrostomy tubes. The type your child has was chosen by their surgeon or gastroenterologist based on age, size, and clinical situation. Knowing your child's exact tube type is important for troubleshooting and replacement.

MIC-KEY Button

Also called: Low-profile button · AMT Mini ONE

The most common type for children going home. A small, round button that sits nearly flat against the skin. It has a balloon inside the stomach that holds it in place. A separate extension tube clicks in when feeding or giving meds, and is removed between uses.

✓ Most common for home use in children

PEG Tube

Percutaneous Endoscopic Gastrostomy

A longer, dangling tube placed endoscopically. Has an internal bumper (not a balloon) that holds it in the stomach. Often the initial tube placed — many children are later converted to a low-profile button after 6–12 weeks when the tract is mature.

Often temporary — ask about conversion to button

Balloon vs. Non-Balloon

Internal retention mechanism

Balloon buttons (like MIC-KEY) have a small balloon filled with sterile water that inflates inside the stomach. The balloon must be checked weekly. Non-balloon buttons use a mushroom-shaped bumper — more secure but require a special remover tool to change.

Ask your team: balloon or non-balloon?
📝
Write Down Your Child's Tube Details

Before leaving the hospital, write down: tube brand (e.g., MIC-KEY), French size (e.g., 14 Fr or 16 Fr), and balloon volume (e.g., 5 mL). Keep this in your phone and in your emergency bag. You will need this information if the tube comes out unexpectedly and you go to your local ER.

Know Your Equipment

Parts of the G-Tube System

The G-tube is a simple system but every part has a purpose. Understanding each component helps you troubleshoot problems and care for the tube confidently.

G-Tube Button System — Component Guide
Button (External Dome)
The round disc that sits on your child's skin. Low-profile and designed to stay flat. Some have a safety disc on top that must be opened before connecting the extension.
Internal Balloon
A small balloon filled with sterile water (typically 3–7 mL) that inflates inside the stomach to prevent the tube from coming out. Must be checked weekly.
Extension Tube
A separate tube that clicks or twists into the button for feeding and meds. Comes in different types: right-angle (for feeding pump) and straight (for bolus). Remove and clean after each use.
Feeding Port & Med Port
On most extension tubes there is a large port (feeding) and a smaller port (medications). The med port allows you to give meds without interrupting a continuous feed.
Clamp / Slide Clamp
Always close the clamp before disconnecting the extension from the button. An open clamp means stomach contents can drain out — and air can go in, causing discomfort.
Balloon Fill Port
A small side port on the button used to add or remove sterile water from the internal balloon. Check weekly with a syringe — remove water, measure it, then refill to the prescribed amount.
Daily Care

Stoma Care

The stoma is the opening in your child's skin where the G-tube passes through. Keeping it clean and dry is the single most important daily task. A well-cared-for stoma has less infection, less leaking, and less skin irritation.

Daily Stoma Cleaning

Clean the stoma once or twice daily — more often if there is leaking or moisture. Use clean technique: wash your hands thoroughly first.

1

Gather Your Supplies

You need: clean gauze pads or soft cloths, mild soap (unscented) or saline wound wash, warm water, clean cotton-tipped swabs, and a fresh gauze split pad (if using one under the tube).

2

Look at the Stoma First

Before cleaning, look closely at the site. Normal: skin is pink, slightly firm at the edges, no odor, small amount of clear or whitish crust. Concerning: redness spreading outward, bright red bumpy tissue growing out (granulation), foul smell, yellow/green discharge, or bleeding. Document and report any concerning signs.

3

Clean Around the Tube

Using a damp gauze or cotton swab with mild soap and water, clean in a circle starting at the tube and moving outward. Clean under the external disc of the button — gently lift the disc and clean beneath it. Remove any crusting or dried secretions gently. Do not scrub.

💡 Saline-soaked gauze works very well for removing dried crust without irritating the skin.
4

Dry Completely

Pat the area dry with a clean gauze pad. Moisture trapped under the button is one of the main causes of skin breakdown and granulation tissue. Let the site air dry for a minute before replacing any gauze pad under the tube.

🚫 Never use alcohol, hydrogen peroxide, or iodine around the stoma — these damage healing skin tissue.
5

Gauze Pad — To Use or Not?

A split gauze pad under the button helps absorb small amounts of moisture or leakage. However, once the stoma is well-healed (4–6 weeks post-placement), many children do better without a gauze pad — the skin stays drier. Ask your GI team what they recommend for your child.

6

Check Balloon Water Weekly

Once a week, use a syringe to check the balloon. Remove all the water, measure how much came out, then refill with the same prescribed amount of sterile water. If significantly less water came out than expected, the balloon may have a leak and the tube may need replacement soon. Call your GI team.

💡 Write the balloon water amount on a sticky note and keep it on your supplies bag so you never forget how much to refill.

Granulation Tissue — What Is That Pink Bump?

Granulation tissue is one of the most common G-tube problems. It looks like a small, pink or red, moist bump growing at or around the stoma. It is not cancer, not dangerous — but it can bleed, ooze, and be uncomfortable.

⚠️
What Causes Granulation Tissue?

It forms when the body senses friction or moisture at the tube site and responds by growing extra tissue. Common causes: tube moving too much (too long a tube), moisture trapped under the button, skin irritation from leaking formula. Keeping the tube secure and the site dry helps prevent it. Treatment options include silver nitrate cauterization by your care team, steroid cream, or Maalox/Mylanta applied topically — ask your GI team before treating at home.

Nutrition

Giving Feeds Through the G-Tube

G-tube feeds deliver nutrition, fluids, and calories directly to the stomach. Your child's feeding schedule — what formula, how much, and how fast — is prescribed by your dietitian and GI team. Never change the amount or rate without checking first.

Bolus Feeding (Syringe or Gravity)

Bolus feeding means giving a set amount of formula over 20–30 minutes, several times a day — similar to how a typical meal works. It is the most common method for children at home.

1

Wash Hands & Prepare Formula

Wash hands thoroughly. Prepare the prescribed amount of formula at room temperature or warmed — never microwave (creates hot spots). Shake the formula well before pouring.

2

Position Your Child Upright

Position your child at a 30–45 degree angle (sitting, or with head of bed elevated) during and for at least 30 minutes after feeding. This reduces reflux and aspiration risk. Never feed flat on their back.

3

Vent the Stomach First

Connect the extension tube to the button. Open the clamp. Hold the extension upright and wait — if air or stomach contents come back up freely, that is normal venting. This releases gas in the stomach before the feed. See the venting section for full details.

4

Give the Feed by Gravity (Preferred) or Syringe

Gravity method: Pour formula into a feeding syringe or bag held above the stomach. Let it flow in slowly by gravity over 20–30 minutes. Adjust the height of the syringe to control the flow rate — higher = faster.

Syringe push: Gently push the formula using the plunger. Never force it — if you feel resistance, stop and troubleshoot.

🚫 Never push forcefully if you feel resistance — this can cause pain, vomiting, or tube damage.
5

Flush With Water After the Feed

After every feed, flush the extension tube and button with 10–30 mL of water (amount prescribed by your team). This prevents formula from clogging the tube and helps with hydration. Use cooled boiled water or sterile water — not tap water for children under 1 year or those with immunocompromise.

6

Close Clamp, Remove Extension, Clean

Close the clamp on the extension before disconnecting to avoid stomach contents draining. Remove the extension and rinse it with warm soapy water. Let it air dry. Extension tubes can be reused — wash after every feed.

Continuous Feeding (Pump)

Some children need formula delivered slowly and continuously over many hours — often overnight or 24 hours a day. This uses an enteral feeding pump (such as a Kangaroo or INFINITY pump). Your home nursing company will deliver the pump and train you on the specific model.

  • Prime the feeding bag and tubing before starting — fill the line with formula to remove all air
  • Connect to the extension tube, open the clamp, and start the pump at the prescribed rate (mL/hr)
  • Check every 4 hours: is the pump running, is the bag nearly empty, is the tube in the right position?
  • Flush with water before and after the pump feed and before/after medications
  • Change feeding bag and tubing every 24 hours — do not reuse overnight bags

Venting (Burping) the Stomach

Children with G-tubes often swallow air or have more gas than usual. Venting means releasing that air through the tube before a feed — preventing discomfort, bloating, and reflux. It is one of the most helpful skills you will learn.

💨
How to Vent

Connect the extension tube to the button and open the clamp. Hold the extension tube vertically above your child's stomach. Do NOT push — just wait. Air will bubble up and out, sometimes with a little formula. Keep holding until bubbling stops (usually 1–5 minutes). You can also attach a large syringe without the plunger and let it act as a funnel — air and small amounts of formula drain back up. Vent before every bolus feed and whenever your child seems gassy, uncomfortable, or retching.

Formula and Nutrition

Your child's formula type, caloric density, and daily volume are prescribed by your registered dietitian. Common formulas used in children include:

Elemental / Amino Acid Formulas

Proteins broken down to the smallest components — easiest to absorb. Used for children with allergies, malabsorption, or gut inflammation.

Examples: Neocate, EleCare, Puramino, Vivonex Pediatric

Semi-Elemental (Peptide-Based)

Partially broken-down proteins. A middle ground between whole protein and elemental formulas. Good for children with moderate gut issues.

Examples: Pediasure Peptide, Peptamen Junior, Vital 1.0

Polymeric (Whole Protein)

Standard formulas with intact proteins. For children with normal digestive function who just need volume or caloric support.

Examples: Pediasure, Nutren Junior, Kate Farms, Compleat Pediatric

Blenderized / Real Food Diets

Blended whole foods given through the tube. Growing in popularity. Requires careful food safety practices and GI team guidance.

Commercial options: Real Food Blends, Nourish. Or homemade with dietitian guidance.
⚠️
Never Change Formula or Amounts Without Guidance

Formula changes can cause significant GI upset, diarrhea, or poor nutrition if not done carefully. Always contact your dietitian or GI team before switching formulas, changing the concentration, or adjusting volumes. In West Texas, if your regular formula is unavailable at your local pharmacy, call your care team — many formulas can be shipped directly to your home through specialty pharmacies.

Medications

Giving Medications Through the G-Tube

Most oral medications can be given through the G-tube — but not all. Always confirm with your pharmacist which medications are safe to crush or use in liquid form through a feeding tube.

1

Check Each Medication With the Pharmacist

Before going home, ask your pharmacist: "Can this be given through a G-tube?" Some medications are extended-release and cannot be crushed. Some are enteric-coated and will not work if crushed. Some medications interact with feeding formula — ask if the feed needs to be held before or after.

2

Use Liquid Formulations When Available

Liquid medications are easiest. If a liquid form exists, use it. If not, many tablets can be crushed into a fine powder and dissolved in a small amount of warm water (5–10 mL). Never crush tablets into formula — this can clog the tube and change how the medication works.

3

Give Medications One at a Time

Never mix multiple medications together — they can interact with each other or the formula. Give each medication separately with a small water flush (3–5 mL) between each one. This prevents clogging and ensures each medication is fully delivered.

4

Flush Before and After All Medications

Always flush the tube with the prescribed amount of water before the first medication and after the last medication. This ensures medications are fully delivered into the stomach and the tube is clear for the next feed.

💡 Keep a written medication log — especially if multiple caregivers give medications at different times. This prevents double-dosing.
🚫
Medications That Should NEVER Be Crushed for G-Tube Use

Extended-release, enteric-coated, and sublingual medications must never be crushed. Common examples: Singulair sprinkles can be used as-is but should not be crushed into tablet form; many seizure medications have specific formulations — confirm with neurology. When in doubt, always call the pharmacist first.

Problem Solving

Emergencies

What to Do in an Emergency

Two emergencies require immediate action: tube dislodgement (tube falls out) and a completely blocked tube that is causing distress. Know what to do before it happens.

🚨 Call 911 Immediately

  • Tube fell out AND you cannot replace it AND stoma is closing fast
  • Child is vomiting blood or has bloody stools after a tube issue
  • Signs of peritonitis: rigid, hard, painful belly; high fever; child very ill
  • Child is unresponsive or in severe pain
  • Tube appears to have gone into the wrong place (not the stomach)

📞 Call Your Care Team

  • Tube fell out and you successfully replaced it — team should still know
  • Tube is leaking significantly for more than 24 hours
  • Signs of infection at the stoma: spreading redness, fever, pus
  • Balloon will not refill properly
  • Persistent vomiting through multiple feeds
  • Child refusing feeds or showing significant GI distress

🚨 Tube Fell Out — Act Immediately

⏱️
The Stoma Can Close Within 2–4 Hours

A gastrostomy stoma is a surgically created opening that the body wants to close. In young children and new stomas, the tract can close within hours — sometimes faster. If the tube comes out and cannot be replaced, you must get to an ER immediately. Do not wait to see if it stays open.

🚨 If the G-Tube Falls Out

1
Stay calm — do not panic

This is frightening but manageable. Your child is not bleeding internally. Focus on the steps.

2
Cover the stoma immediately

Place a clean damp cloth, gauze, or even a clean finger over the stoma to keep it open while you prepare for replacement.

3
Get the emergency backup tube

You should always have a backup G-tube (same size and brand) at home. Deflate the balloon of the backup tube completely. Lubricate the tip with water-soluble lubricant (like KY Jelly) — never petroleum jelly.

4
Insert gently into the stoma

Hold the skin around the stoma steady with one hand. With the other hand, gently insert the lubricated tube into the stoma using a slight rotating motion. It should slide in with minimal resistance. Never force it.

5
Inflate the balloon

Once inserted, inflate the balloon with the prescribed amount of sterile water. Gently tug the tube — it should stop with resistance when the balloon is in the stomach.

6
Confirm position — then call your care team

Check position: attach a syringe and aspirate — you should get stomach contents back. You can also inject a small amount of air and listen over the stomach for a gurgle. Call your GI team to let them know, even if replacement was successful. Go to ER if you cannot replace the tube within 10–15 minutes.

Blocked Tube That Will Not Clear

If the tube is fully blocked and does not clear with warm water flushing, try: carbonated water or cranberry juice (let sit 15–20 minutes, then flush again). If still blocked after 30 minutes, call your care team. A completely blocked tube that cannot be cleared at home requires a tube replacement at the clinic or ER.

Home Preparedness

Supply Checklist for Home

Keep at least a 30-day supply buffer. In West Texas and Eastern New Mexico, specialty G-tube supplies are not available at most local pharmacies — plan ahead and order before you run out.

🍼 Tube & Feeding Supplies

Backup G-tube (same brand, size, balloon volume) — minimum 2
Extension tubes (right-angle for pump; straight for bolus) — 10+
60 mL catheter-tip syringes for bolus feeding
10 mL syringes for balloon checks and medications
Feeding bags (24-hr bags for pump feeds) — 30+ day supply
Enteral feeding pump (provided by DME)
Formula — 30-day supply, stored correctly
Water-soluble lubricant (KY Jelly) for tube replacement

🧼 Stoma Care Supplies

Split gauze pads (if using at stoma site)
Non-woven gauze pads for cleaning
Cotton-tipped swabs
Mild unscented soap or saline wound wash
Sterile water (for balloon refills — not tap water)
Barrier cream (if prescribed for skin protection)
Medical tape (for securing extension during feeds)
Gloves (clean, non-sterile)
🌵
West Texas Supply Planning Tip

Major pharmacy chains in Lubbock, Midland, and Odessa may stock basic supplies, but specialty items like exact-size MIC-KEY buttons often require ordering. Work with your DME company to establish monthly automatic shipment to your home. Keep the DME 24-hour phone number in your phone — you will need it eventually. Many families also keep a "go bag" with 2 backup tubes, 3 extensions, and a syringe kit packed and ready for travel or emergencies.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

Questions we hear most from families in the hospital and at home. If your question is not here, please ask your care team or reach out to SPEC.

Can my child still eat by mouth if they have a G-tube? ▾
Yes — in most cases. The G-tube does not replace the mouth. Many children with G-tubes eat by mouth for pleasure, taste experiences, and to practice their feeding skills. The tube handles the nutrition burden so that oral eating can be relaxed and enjoyable rather than stressful. Your occupational therapist or speech-language pathologist will guide exactly what oral feeds are safe and appropriate for your child.
Can my child swim or bathe with a G-tube? ▾
Yes — once the stoma is fully healed (typically 4–6 weeks after placement). Bath water and pool water will not damage the tube. After swimming, dry the stoma site thoroughly. Avoid hot tubs and pools with high chlorine concentrations until cleared by your GI team. Most children with well-healed G-tubes have no restrictions on normal water activities.
How often does the G-tube need to be replaced? ▾
Balloon-type buttons (like MIC-KEY) typically need replacement every 3–6 months — the balloon gradually loses water and can fail. Non-balloon buttons can last longer. Your GI team will schedule routine changes. Some families learn to change the tube at home; others prefer clinic changes. Regardless, always keep at least one backup tube at home in case the tube comes out unexpectedly before your scheduled change.
My child's tube site looks red and crusty every morning. Is that normal? ▾
A small amount of clear or whitish crust at the stoma edge is normal — this is dried secretions and is easy to remove with a damp swab. What is not normal is spreading redness that goes outward from the stoma, warmth, swelling, yellow or green discharge with a bad smell, or fever. If you see those signs, contact your care team within 24 hours. Normal crusting just needs gentle daily cleaning.
The formula smells different and my child seems gassy. What should I do? ▾
First check the formula expiration date and storage — formula left at room temperature for more than 4 hours (opened) or 2 hours (mixed) should be discarded. Gas is very common and usually relieved by venting the stomach before feeds and ensuring your child is upright during and after feeds. If gassiness is severe and persistent, contact your dietitian — a formula change or rate adjustment may help.
Will my child have the G-tube forever? ▾
Not necessarily. Many children with G-tubes eventually progress to full oral feeds and have their tube removed — this is called tube weaning. The timeline depends entirely on the underlying condition and how feeding skills develop. Children with neurological conditions may need the tube long-term; children who needed temporary support (prematurity, post-surgical recovery) may be weaned within months to years. Your GI team and feeding therapist will track your child's progress and help guide the weaning process when appropriate.
I live far from Lubbock. What do I do in an emergency at night? ▾
First: for tube dislodgement, your closest ER can replace the tube — call ahead and tell them your child's tube brand, French size, and balloon volume so they can prepare. Keep this information written in your phone and in your emergency bag. For most other G-tube problems (leaking, blockage, skin issues), call your SPEC care coordinator or the GI on-call line — many issues can be managed by phone or telehealth. For true emergencies where your child is very ill, always call 911. Do not drive long distances with an acutely ill child.

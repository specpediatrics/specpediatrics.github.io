Learning Objectives — Module 5
By the end of this module, the learner will be able to:
  • Explain why inhaled corticosteroids are the cornerstone of asthma controller therapy and describe their mechanisms of benefit
  • Identify clinical and prescription-record signs of harmful SABA overuse and explain the associated risks
  • Describe the two GINA 2025 treatment tracks and select the appropriate reliever strategy for a given patient
  • Apply GINA 2025 step therapy for children 6–11 years and for adolescents (≥12 years) across Steps 1–5
  • Outline NAEPP 2020 step therapy for comparison and identify key points of divergence from GINA, including the role of budesonide-formoterol as reliever
  • Describe the FDA neuropsychiatric black-box warning for leukotriene receptor antagonists and its clinical implications

ICS as the Cornerstone

Asthma is fundamentally an inflammatory airway disease. Regardless of severity, controller therapy must address the underlying eosinophilic inflammation driving bronchial hyperresponsiveness, mucus hypersecretion, and airway remodeling. Inhaled corticosteroids (ICS) remain the most effective anti-inflammatory agents available for the vast majority of patients with persistent asthma — a position supported by decades of randomized trial data and endorsed by both GINA 2025 and NAEPP 2020.

ICS act primarily by binding glucocorticoid receptors in airway epithelial and immune cells, suppressing the transcription of pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-4, IL-5, IL-13, TSLP). The result is reduced eosinophil recruitment, decreased mast cell activation, decreased mucus production, and restoration of β2-adrenoceptor responsiveness — which explains why regular ICS use also reduces reliance on bronchodilators over time.

What ICS Treatment Achieves

  • Reduces symptom frequency — daytime and nighttime symptoms decrease within 2–4 weeks of regular use
  • Reduces exacerbation risk — daily low-dose ICS reduces the risk of severe exacerbations requiring systemic steroids by approximately 50–60%
  • Reduces asthma mortality — regular ICS use is associated with a significant reduction in asthma deaths; SABA-only treatment is associated with increased risk
  • Improves lung function — FEV&sub1; and FEV&sub1;/FVC improve over weeks to months of treatment
  • Partially prevents airway remodeling — though some structural changes may be irreversible if inflammation persists for years untreated

ICS Safety in Children

Parents frequently ask about steroid side effects. The critical distinction is inhaled versus systemic. At low-to-medium doses, ICS are designed to act locally in the airway with minimal systemic absorption. GINA 2025 and NAEPP 2020 both state that the benefits of ICS in reducing asthma morbidity and mortality far outweigh the risks of low-to-medium dose use in children.

The primary safety consideration at higher doses is a modest effect on linear growth — meta-analyses suggest approximately 0.5 cm reduction in first-year growth velocity with low-dose ICS in prepubertal children, without a consistent effect on adult height. Oropharyngeal candidiasis and dysphonia are local adverse effects largely prevented by spacer use and mouth rinsing after each dose.

Clinical Pearl
When a parent says “I don’t want my child on steroids,” distinguish — gently and clearly — between systemic and inhaled steroids. “The medicine in this inhaler goes straight to the airways. The amount that gets into the bloodstream is tiny compared to a steroid tablet. We only use tablets for flare-ups. This inhaler is how we prevent them.”

The SABA Problem

Short-acting beta2-agonists (SABAs, principally albuterol) provide rapid bronchodilation within 1–3 minutes. But GINA 2025 makes a pivotal paradigm shift: SABA-only treatment is no longer considered safe or appropriate for any age group, and overuse is a serious safety signal that demands clinical action.

Why SABA Alone Is Insufficient

  • SABAs relieve bronchoconstriction but do not treat underlying airway inflammation — symptoms return as the effect wears off
  • Regular SABA use without ICS leaves the inflammatory milieu untreated, worsening bronchial hyperresponsiveness over time
  • Patients who rely on SABA as their primary agent tend to underestimate severity, delaying appropriate controller escalation
  • β2-adrenoceptor downregulation with chronic SABA use may reduce bronchodilator responsiveness over time
⚠️ Safety Warning — SABA Overuse Thresholds
GINA 2025 defines SABA overuse as using 3 or more 200-dose SABA canisters per year. Dispensing of 12 or more canisters per year is associated with markedly increased risk of asthma death. NAEPP 2020 similarly identifies SABA use on more than 2 days per week for symptom relief as indicating inadequate control. When pharmacy records show SABA overuse, escalate controller therapy — do not simply prescribe more SABA.

From SABA to ICS-Formoterol: A Paradigm Shift

GINA 2025 Track 1 introduces low-dose budesonide-formoterol (ICS-LABA) as the preferred reliever for patients aged 12 years and older. This is called AIR (anti-inflammatory reliever) therapy when used as needed only, or MART (Maintenance and Reliever Therapy) when the same inhaler serves both maintenance and relief. The rationale: every time a patient needs bronchodilation, they also receive a dose of ICS — coupling relief with anti-inflammatory therapy and addressing the core problem of SABA-only regimens.

For Families
Why We’re Concerned About the Blue Inhaler
“The blue inhaler helps right away — it opens up the airways quickly. But it doesn’t treat the reason the airways keep tightening in the first place. If your child is using the blue inhaler more than twice a week, that’s a sign we need to do more to treat the underlying inflammation. Using it too often — without a controller — can actually make asthma harder to control over time.”

GINA 2025 Treatment Tracks

GINA 2025 provides a two-track framework for patients aged 12 years and older. Both tracks use ICS-containing therapy at every step; the key difference is the reliever agent.

Clinical Pearl — MART vs. AIR
MART means the same budesonide-formoterol inhaler is used both daily (maintenance) and as needed (relief). AIR means as-needed ICS-formoterol only, without daily maintenance — for patients with less frequent symptoms at Steps 1–2. Both reduce exacerbations compared to SABA-based approaches.

Step Therapy — Children 6–11 Years

For children aged 6–11 years, Track 2 is standard. Track 1 with as-needed ICS-formoterol reliever applies to patients 12 years and older due to US approval constraints.

Key Considerations for Children 6–11 Years

  • Step 1 preferred option: Whenever a SABA is taken, a low-dose ICS should be taken concomitantly per GINA 2025 — this captures ICS benefit even for infrequent symptoms without requiring daily use
  • Step 2 ICS doses: Budesonide 100–200 mcg/day or fluticasone propionate 100–200 mcg/day via pMDI + spacer are appropriate starting doses
  • ICS-LABA approval: Several combinations are approved for children ≥6 years in the US (e.g. budesonide-formoterol pMDI, fluticasone-salmeterol — age cutoffs vary by product)
  • Stepping up: Before stepping up, always confirm adherence, inhaler technique, and trigger avoidance. A 3-month trial at a given step is generally appropriate before escalation
  • Stepping down: If well-controlled for 3+ months, consider stepping down to the lowest step that maintains control

Step Therapy — Adolescents ≥12 Years

Adolescents follow the same framework as adults in GINA 2025 and are eligible for Track 1. This is particularly relevant given high rates of non-adherence to daily controllers in this age group — an as-needed ICS-formoterol regimen removes the barrier of remembering a separate daily medication.

Clinical Pearl — Adolescents and Track 1
In a teenager who consistently forgets their daily ICS, switching to as-needed budesonide-formoterol (Track 1 AIR strategy) is not a step-down — it is a pragmatic approach that provides ICS with every bronchodilator use. The SYGMA 2 trial showed non-inferiority to daily ICS plus SABA for symptom control, with markedly fewer severe exacerbations than SABA alone.

Step Therapy — Children ≤5 Years

Treatment in children 5 years and younger is complicated by diagnostic uncertainty, limited drug approvals, and high spontaneous resolution rates for preschool wheeze. The goal is to treat the child in front of you while reassessing the diagnosis regularly.

StepPreferred AgentNotes
Step 1SABA as neededFor infrequent episodic wheeze with low asthma probability
Step 2Daily low-dose ICS (pMDI + spacer + mask)If SABA needed >2×/week on average or ≥3 exacerbations/year requiring OCS
Step 3Medium-dose ICS or add LTRA (with discussion of black-box warning)Refer to pediatric pulmonologist or allergist
Step 4+ReferRe-evaluate diagnosis before further escalation

NAEPP 2020 conditionally recommends intermittent high-dose ICS (e.g. budesonide 1 mg nebulized twice daily for 7 days) at the onset of viral illness for API-positive children under 5 with ≥3 moderate-to-severe episodes per year. GINA 2025 considers this an option for selected high-risk patients; not a broad recommendation.

⚠️ Device Note for ≤5 Years
Nebulizers are NOT preferred as the routine delivery method for young children per GINA 2025. A pMDI with valved holding chamber and fitted face mask is preferred for children who cannot form a reliable mouthpiece seal. See Module 6 for full device selection guidance.

Leukotriene Receptor Antagonists — FDA Warning

LTRAs, principally montelukast (Singulair), have been used as alternative controllers and as add-on therapy, particularly in patients with concurrent allergic rhinitis. However, their use is now constrained by an important safety concern from the FDA.

⚠️ FDA Black-Box Warning — Montelukast (March 2020)
The FDA added a black-box warning to montelukast for serious neuropsychiatric adverse events including: agitation, aggression, anxiousness, dream abnormalities and hallucinations, depression, insomnia, irritability, restlessness, suicidal thinking and behavior (including suicide), and tremor. These events can occur in patients with or without a prior psychiatric history. The FDA states: “The benefits of montelukast may not outweigh the risks in some patients, particularly in those with mild disease.” Reserve montelukast for patients with an inadequate response or intolerance to other asthma therapies.

Clinical Implications

  • GINA 2025 lists LTRA as an alternative (not preferred) at Steps 2–3 for children 6–11 years, and as an alternative at Step 2 for children ≤5 years
  • NAEPP 2020 conditionally recommends montelukast as an alternative to daily ICS at Step 2 but acknowledges the FDA warning
  • Before prescribing, discuss the neuropsychiatric risk explicitly with the patient and family, document this conversation, and have a low threshold to discontinue if behavioral changes occur
  • Montelukast retains a role in patients with concurrent allergic rhinitis, exercise-induced bronchoconstriction prophylaxis, or families unable to use inhaler devices
For Families
What to Watch for if Your Child Takes Montelukast
Montelukast (Singulair) can, in some children, cause changes in mood, behavior, sleep, or thinking. Watch for unusual irritability, aggression, sadness, sleep problems, unusual dreams, or — in older children — thoughts of self-harm. If you notice any of these, stop the medication and call your doctor. These effects can start weeks or months after beginning the medication.

NAEPP 2020 vs. GINA 2025 — Key Differences

US trainees need to be comfortable with both frameworks. Most US centers use NAEPP as their institutional guideline; GINA 2025 is used for academic teaching and represents the current evidence consensus internationally. The Chipps et al. 2022 review provides a structured comparison.

Chipps 2022 Synthesis
Chipps et al. conclude that GINA and NAEPP are largely concordant at Steps 2–5 but diverge significantly at Step 1 and in reliever strategy. The central message for US clinicians: NAEPP 2020’s most important updates are replacing SABA-alone at Step 1 with ICS-containing options, the conditional recommendation for intermittent high-dose ICS in preschool wheeze, and acknowledging that ICS-formoterol as reliever represents the international evidence direction.

Biologics — An Overview

Biologic therapies target specific molecular pathways in Type 2 asthma inflammation. They are reserved for patients with severe uncontrolled asthma (Step 5) who remain symptomatic despite high-dose ICS-LABA with confirmed adherence and addressed modifiable factors. Case Vignette

Case Vignette
Twelve Years Old, Barely Controlled
Mia is a 12-year-old with a 3-year history of asthma, currently on budesonide 100 mcg twice daily via pMDI + spacer. She reports daytime symptoms 3–4 days per week, wakes 1–2 nights per week with cough, and uses her albuterol about 4 times per week. Pharmacy records show 7 albuterol refills in the past 8 months. She says she does take her ICS daily “most of the time.” Spirometry: FEV&sub1; 78% predicted, post-BD improvement 15%.

Question: What is your assessment of control, and what is the most appropriate next treatment step for Mia?
Assessment & Recommendation

Control: Uncontrolled (3–4 of 4 GINA criteria met: daytime >2×/week, nighttime waking, SABA >2×/week). Seven albuterol refills in 8 months is a serious overuse signal per GINA 2025.

Before stepping up: Verify adherence; watch her demonstrate inhaler technique; assess triggers; check for comorbidities (rhinitis, GERD). A higher-step regimen taken inconsistently will not outperform a lower step taken reliably.

Optimal step-up (Track 1, GINA 2025, ≥12y): Switch to budesonide-formoterol 160/4.5 pMDI as MART — 1 puff twice daily as maintenance plus as needed for relief. This couples ICS with every bronchodilator use and directly addresses SABA overuse. Provides Step 3 coverage on Track 1.

Alternatively (Track 2 / NAEPP): Step up to low-dose ICS-LABA maintenance (e.g. fluticasone-salmeterol 100/50 twice daily) with SABA as needed — also guideline-concordant. Follow up in 4–6 weeks.

Self-Assessment

A 9-year-old with newly diagnosed asthma has daytime symptoms 3×/week, one night waking, and uses SABA 3×/week. Per GINA 2025, the most appropriate initial treatment is:
  • A. SABA as needed only (Step 1)
  • B. Daily low-dose ICS + as-needed SABA (Step 2)
  • C. Low-dose ICS-LABA (Step 3)
  • D. Montelukast daily — preferred first-line controller
B is correct. This child’s symptom frequency (daytime >2×/week, nighttime waking, SABA >2×/week) classifies him as partly controlled to uncontrolled on no therapy — appropriate for Step 2. Daily low-dose ICS plus as-needed SABA is GINA 2025 Track 2 Step 2 for children 6–11 years. Track 1 with ICS-formoterol reliever is recommended for ≥12 years only. Montelukast (D) is an alternative, not preferred, and carries an FDA black-box warning.
A 14-year-old is poorly adherent with daily fluticasone and uses albuterol 4–5 times per week. Which change best addresses both adherence and SABA overuse simultaneously?
  • A. Increase fluticasone to medium dose
  • B. Add montelukast as a second controller
  • C. Switch to as-needed budesonide-formoterol (Track 1 AIR strategy)
  • D. Continue current regimen and schedule monthly adherence check-ins
C is correct. As-needed ICS-formoterol (Track 1 AIR strategy) addresses both issues: every bronchodilator use delivers ICS (resolving the adherence gap), and the patient transitions away from SABA-only relief. GINA 2025 trials show this approach reduces severe exacerbations even in patients with low average inhaler use. Option A fails to address adherence. Option D maintains the status quo at continued risk.
A parent wants montelukast instead of an ICS for their 7-year-old “because the steroid worries me.” Which statement is most accurate?
  • A. Montelukast is the preferred first-line controller for children under 12
  • B. Low-dose ICS and montelukast are equivalent in efficacy and safety
  • C. Low-dose ICS is the preferred controller at Step 2; montelukast is an alternative with an FDA black-box warning for neuropsychiatric effects that must be discussed
  • D. The FDA has approved montelukast as first-line therapy
C is correct. Both GINA 2025 and NAEPP 2020 list low-dose ICS as the preferred Step 2 controller — montelukast is an alternative. The FDA black-box warning (March 2020) for serious neuropsychiatric adverse effects must be discussed explicitly with families before prescribing. Addressing the steroid concern is appropriate — inhaled steroids at low doses carry a very different risk profile from systemic steroids, and the risk-benefit profile strongly favors ICS over leaving asthma uncontrolled.

Learning Objectives — Module 9
By the end of this module, the learner will be able to:
  • Distinguish between difficult-to-treat asthma and true severe asthma using the GINA 2025 framework
  • Apply a systematic checklist to identify and address modifiable factors before escalating therapy in a child with apparently uncontrolled asthma
  • Identify the major comorbidities that contribute to difficult-to-treat asthma in children and adolescents
  • Explain the basic concepts of Type 2 vs. non-Type 2 asthma endotypes and how biomarkers guide biologic selection
  • Describe the five approved biologics for severe pediatric asthma, their targets, age cutoffs, and key biomarker indications
  • Identify the clinical thresholds that should prompt referral to a pediatric pulmonologist or allergist/immunologist

Difficult-to-Treat vs. True Severe Asthma

The most important concept in this module: not all uncontrolled asthma at high treatment steps is severe asthma. GINA 2025 draws a precise distinction that has major clinical implications for management.

Clinical Pearl — The GINA Figure 2 Algorithm
GINA 2025 Figure 2 “Investigating uncontrolled asthma” begins with a single instruction: watch the patient use their inhaler. Before ordering biologics, before stepping up to Step 5, before referring to subspecialty — watch the technique. A substantial fraction of patients referred as severe asthma are delivering little or no drug to their airways due to device errors.

Systematic Assessment of Modifiable Factors

GINA 2025 provides a structured checklist for assessing modifiable factors in patients with apparently uncontrolled asthma. All of the following must be evaluated before concluding that a patient has true severe asthma. In practice, multiple modifiable factors often coexist.

The Modifiable Factors Checklist

  • Confirm the diagnosis of asthma. Is there objective evidence of variable airflow limitation? Up to 25–35% of patients labeled as having asthma in primary care may not have the condition. Spirometry with bronchodilator response, methacholine challenge, or FeNO measurement should be reviewed.
  • Check inhaler technique. Watch the patient use every device they are prescribed. Technique errors are present in 50–80% of patients across studies — for every device type.
  • Assess adherence. Ask non-judgmentally: “In the last month, on how many days per week did you take the brown inhaler?” Consider pharmacy dispensing records. Poor adherence is the single most common modifiable factor in difficult-to-treat asthma.
  • Identify and reduce triggers. Allergen sensitization + ongoing exposure (pets, dust mite, mold, cockroach); occupational exposures in adolescents; air pollution; tobacco smoke exposure (including second-hand); NSAID sensitivity (aspirin-exacerbated respiratory disease in older adolescents).
  • Screen for comorbidities (see below).
  • Assess for psychosocial barriers. Anxiety, depression, and dysfunctional breathing are common in adolescents with poorly controlled asthma and may mimic or worsen asthma symptoms. Family dysfunction, food insecurity, and housing instability are social determinants that affect medication access and adherence.
⚠️ Escalating Without Checking These Factors Is Guideline-Discordant
Per GINA 2025, adding a biologic or stepping to Step 5 therapy without a systematic assessment of modifiable factors is an error. A thorough evaluation may reveal that the patient’s uncontrolled asthma is entirely explained by poor technique and ongoing cat allergen exposure — neither of which is addressed by omalizumab.

Comorbidities in Difficult-to-Treat Asthma

Comorbidities contribute to apparent treatment refractoriness in two ways: they share symptoms with asthma (and may be the actual cause of symptoms) or they worsen asthma control through biological or mechanical mechanisms. Identifying and treating comorbidities often improves asthma control without any medication change.

ComorbidityHow It Affects AsthmaAssessment & Management

Inducible laryngeal obstruction (ILO / VCD)Paradoxical vocal cord adduction mimics asthma; inspiratory stridor may be misidentified as wheeze; does not respond to bronchodilatorsConsider when spirometry is normal during symptoms, symptoms resolve rapidly, or symptoms are predominantly inspiratory; refer to ENT or SLP for laryngoscopy and breathing retraining

Phenotypes, Endotypes & Biomarkers

In severe asthma, treatment selection is guided by inflammatory phenotype. The dominant paradigm in contemporary asthma biology is the distinction between Type 2 (T2) high and Type 2 low inflammation, which predicts response to currently available biologics.

Type 2 High Asthma

Type 2 inflammation is driven by ILC2 cells, Th2 lymphocytes, and epithelial-derived cytokines (IL-4, IL-5, IL-13, TSLP). It is characterized by eosinophilic airway inflammation, elevated serum IgE, and often coexists with allergic rhinitis, eczema, and food allergy. All five currently approved biologics for severe asthma target T2 pathways.

Type 2 Low Asthma

A minority of patients with severe asthma have predominantly neutrophilic or paucigranulocytic airway inflammation with low eosinophils, low IgE, and low FeNO. This phenotype is less responsive to ICS and has no approved biologic therapies as of 2025. Management focuses on optimizing ICS-LABA, addressing comorbidities (obesity, smoking, reflux), and investigating alternative diagnoses.

Biologic Therapy in Severe Pediatric Asthma

Biologic therapies are monoclonal antibodies or receptor blockers targeting specific cytokines or receptors in the T2 inflammatory cascade. They are reserved for Step 5 — patients with severe uncontrolled asthma despite high-dose ICS-LABA with confirmed adherence and addressed modifiable factors. All five currently approved biologics reduce exacerbations by 40–70% in appropriately selected patients.

Omalizumab (Xolair)
Anti-IgE  ·  ≥6 years
Mechanism: Binds free serum IgE, preventing mast cell and basophil activation. Reduces IgE receptor expression over time. Indication: Moderate-to-severe allergic asthma with sensitization to at least one perennial aeroallergen and total IgE 30–1500 IU/mL. Dosing: Subcutaneous every 2 or 4 weeks; dose table based on body weight and baseline IgE. Key point: The oldest and most widely studied biologic in children with asthma; FDA approval ≥6 years.
Mepolizumab (Nucala)
Anti-IL-5  ·  ≥6 years
Mechanism: Monoclonal antibody against IL-5, reducing eosinophil survival and production. Indication: Severe eosinophilic asthma; blood eosinophils ≥150 cells/µL at initiation (higher counts predict greater benefit). Dosing: Subcutaneous every 4 weeks; fixed dose by age/weight group. Key point: FDA approval ≥6 years; reduces exacerbations by ~50% and allows OCS reduction in OCS-dependent patients.
Benralizumab (Fasenra)
Anti-IL-5Rα  ·  ≥12 years
Mechanism: Binds IL-5 receptor alpha on eosinophils and basophils; induces ADCC (antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity), causing near-complete eosinophil depletion. Indication: Severe eosinophilic asthma; blood eosinophils ≥300 cells/µL for best response. Dosing: Subcutaneous every 4 weeks ×3, then every 8 weeks. Key point: FDA approval ≥12 years; convenient every-8-week dosing after loading. Rapid, near-complete eosinophil depletion is distinctive.
Dupilumab (Dupixent)
Anti-IL-4Rα  ·  ≥6 years
Mechanism: Blocks IL-4 receptor alpha, inhibiting both IL-4 and IL-13 signaling — the two key Th2 cytokines driving T2 inflammation. Indication: Moderate-to-severe eosinophilic asthma or OCS-dependent asthma. Also approved for atopic dermatitis (≥6 months) and CRSwNP — useful for patients with multiple T2 conditions. Dosing: Subcutaneous every 2 weeks; weight-based dosing in children. Key point: FDA approval ≥6 years for asthma; the broadest T2 indication of any current biologic; may be preferred when comorbid eczema or CRSwNP is present.
Tezepelumab (Tezspire)
Anti-TSLP  ·  ≥12 years
Mechanism: Blocks thymic stromal lymphopoietin (TSLP), an epithelial-derived alarmin that initiates and amplifies T2 inflammation upstream of IL-4/IL-5/IL-13. Indication: Severe uncontrolled asthma regardless of eosinophil count — the broadest phenotypic indication of any approved biologic; may be effective in T2-low patients. Dosing: Subcutaneous every 4 weeks; fixed dose (210 mg). Key point: FDA approval ≥12 years; NAVIGATOR trial showed ~70% exacerbation reduction across all eosinophil count subgroups; valuable for patients without a clearly elevated eosinophil phenotype.
Clinical Pearl — Biologic Selection in Practice
When blood eosinophils are elevated (≥300 cells/µL) and IgE is elevated with confirmed sensitization, both omalizumab and an anti-eosinophil agent are plausible. In an adolescent with concomitant severe eczema and elevated eosinophils, dupilumab may address both conditions. In a patient with uncontrolled asthma but low eosinophils and low IgE, tezepelumab is the only biologic with demonstrated efficacy across eosinophil strata. Always consult with or refer to allergy/immunology or pediatric pulmonology for biologic initiation.

Referral Thresholds

Knowing when to refer is as important as knowing how to manage. GINA 2025 and NAEPP 2020 both provide guidance on when specialist involvement is appropriate. The following indications apply to pediatric patients.

Refer to Pediatric Pulmonology

  • Asthma uncontrolled at Step 3 or higher despite confirmed adherence and correct technique
  • Diagnosis is uncertain or another condition is suspected (e.g. CF, PCD, tracheomalacia, ILO)
  • Two or more systemic corticosteroid courses in the past year, or OCS dependency
  • Prior intubation or ICU admission for asthma
  • Significant exercise limitation attributed to asthma despite treatment
  • Abnormal spirometry pattern inconsistent with typical obstructive asthma (e.g. fixed obstruction, restrictive pattern)

Refer to Allergy / Immunology

  • Suspected allergic triggers contributing to asthma (confirmed sensitization + ongoing exposure)
  • Consideration of allergen immunotherapy (SCIT or SLIT) — NAEPP 2020 conditionally recommends SCIT for mild-to-moderate allergic asthma
  • Concurrent allergic rhinitis, eczema, or food allergy requiring coordinated management
  • Consideration of biologic therapy for severe asthma
  • Suspected NSAID-exacerbated respiratory disease in adolescents
For Families
What to Expect from a Severe Asthma Referral
If your child’s doctor refers you to a lung specialist or allergy specialist, it’s because asthma that is harder to control sometimes needs additional tests and treatments not available at a general pediatric visit. The specialist will likely repeat lung function tests, check for triggers, and review every medication your child takes. This process takes time — but it often identifies something that was being missed, and opens the door to treatments that can make a real difference.

Case Vignette

Case Vignette
Referred as “Severe Asthma” — Is It?
Noah is a 13-year-old referred by his pediatrician as “severe asthma not responding to treatment.” He is on fluticasone-salmeterol 250/50 pMDI twice daily plus as-needed albuterol. He has had 3 courses of oral prednisolone in the past year. His blood eosinophil count is 480 cells/µL and total IgE is 320 IU/mL. His pediatrician is considering starting omalizumab.

You see Noah in your clinic for the first time. Before doing anything else, you watch him use his fluticasone-salmeterol pMDI. He actuates it without shaking, inhales rapidly, and does not use a spacer. He says he “forgets” the morning dose about half the time. He lives with an indoor dog. He also reports feeling anxious frequently and sometimes panics when he gets short of breath.

Question: Is this severe asthma? What do you do next?
Clinical Reasoning

Is this severe asthma? Not yet confirmed. Per GINA 2025, severe asthma is defined as asthma that remains uncontrolled despite good adherence to high-dose ICS-LABA after modifiable factors have been addressed. Noah has at least four unaddressed modifiable factors: (1) incorrect inhaler technique for both pMDI devices; (2) approximately 50% adherence to the morning ICS-LABA dose; (3) ongoing dog allergen exposure; (4) anxiety with dysfunctional breathing pattern.

What to do next — in order:

1. Correct technique: Teach correct pMDI technique — shake, spacer use, slow coordinated inhalation, breath-hold, mouth rinse. Watch Noah demonstrate return demonstration. Document.

2. Address adherence: Explore barriers to morning dosing non-judgmentally. Consider switching from twice-daily to once-daily ICS-LABA (if appropriate agent) to reduce dose burden. Discuss MART approach using budesonide-formoterol as the ICS-formoterol reliever (≥12 years eligible) — this may improve de facto adherence.

3. Address allergen exposure: Dog allergen is a potent sensitizer and continuous exposure significantly worsens asthma in sensitized patients. Counsel the family about dog removal or strict dog exclusion from bedroom; confirm sensitization with skin prick test or specific IgE to dog. Refer to allergy/immunology for consideration of dog allergen immunotherapy if removal is not possible.

4. Address anxiety: Screen with GAD-7; refer for breathing retraining and psychological support. Dysfunctional breathing may be contributing significantly to his symptomatic burden and SABA use.

Hold on omalizumab for now. Initiating omalizumab before these factors are addressed would be premature and guideline-discordant. Reassess in 3 months after modifiable factors are corrected. If still uncontrolled at that point — with confirmed technique and adherence — then biologic evaluation (with his eosinophil and IgE profile, both omalizumab and mepolizumab/dupilumab could be candidates) would be appropriate.

Self-Assessment

A 10-year-old on medium-dose ICS-LABA continues to have daily symptoms and two exacerbations in the past year. Before considering biologic therapy, which of the following must be addressed FIRST per GINA 2025?
  • A. Obtain blood eosinophil count and FeNO to determine the appropriate biologic
  • B. Systematically assess and correct inhaler technique, adherence, ongoing triggers, comorbidities, and confirm the asthma diagnosis
  • C. Begin a trial of subcutaneous omalizumab as first-line biologic for uncontrolled asthma
  • D. Add tiotropium to her current regimen before escalating further
B is correct. GINA 2025 makes a critical distinction: difficult-to-treat asthma (uncontrolled because of modifiable factors) is not the same as severe asthma (uncontrolled despite high-dose ICS-LABA with addressed modifiable factors). The GINA Figure 2 algorithm begins with watching the patient use their inhaler and assessing adherence — before any medication escalation. Jumping to biologics (C) without this assessment is guideline-discordant. Ordering biomarkers (A) is premature if technique and adherence have not yet been assessed.
Tezepelumab differs from other approved biologics for severe asthma primarily because:
  • A. It is the only subcutaneous biologic available for asthma
  • B. It is approved for children as young as 6 years
  • C. It targets TSLP upstream of the T2 cascade and demonstrates efficacy regardless of blood eosinophil count
  • D. It is the only biologic that also treats atopic dermatitis and CRSwNP
C is correct. Tezepelumab targets TSLP (thymic stromal lymphopoietin), an epithelial alarmin that initiates T2 inflammation upstream of IL-4, IL-5, and IL-13. By blocking this upstream signal, tezepelumab reduces exacerbations across all eosinophil count subgroups in the NAVIGATOR trial — including patients with low or normal eosinophils. This makes it the only biologic with demonstrated efficacy in patients who do not fit a classical T2-high eosinophilic phenotype. FDA approval is ≥12 years. Dupilumab (not tezepelumab) is approved for atopic dermatitis and CRSwNP (D). Multiple biologics are subcutaneous (A). Omalizumab, mepolizumab, and dupilumab have ≥6 year approvals (B).
Which comorbidity, when present in a child with asthma, is a specific risk factor for fatal food-allergic anaphylaxis and should prompt verification of an epinephrine auto-injector prescription?
  • A. Allergic rhinitis
  • B. GERD
  • C. Food allergy
  • D. Obesity
C is correct. Asthma is the leading risk factor for fatal food anaphylaxis — the combination of food allergy and asthma significantly elevates the risk of a severe or fatal anaphylactic reaction compared to food allergy without asthma. At every visit, children with both asthma and food allergy should have their epinephrine auto-injector prescription confirmed as current, and the family should be counseled on when to use it. Allergic rhinitis (A) worsens asthma control but is not independently associated with fatal anaphylaxis. GERD (B) and obesity (D) are important asthma comorbidities but do not carry this specific fatality risk.

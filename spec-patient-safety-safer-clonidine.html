Why Patient Safety

A Curriculum Built Around Real Problems

Most preventable harm in pediatrics is not caused by lack of knowledge. It is caused by cognitive shortcuts under pressure, systems that enable error, communication that breaks down, and teams that do not speak up. This curriculum addresses those gaps directly.

🔒
Educational privacy note: Cases on this page are composite educational scenarios derived from common pediatric safety patterns. They are intentionally modified to avoid representing any single identifiable patient, family, clinician, unit, or institution.
💡

Module 1 — Medication Safety

Medication Reconciliation & Dosing Safety

Medication errors are among the most common preventable adverse events in pediatrics. Decimal points, verbal history, and EMR entry can create a perfect storm of risk even before any medication is given. This module uses a representative composite case to teach recognition, root cause analysis, and system-level prevention.

Composite Case Root Cause Analysis EMR Pitfalls M&M Ready QI Project
Anchor Case — Composite Educational Scenario
Pediatric Medication Reconciliation Near-Miss: A 10-Fold Clonidine Discrepancy
A pediatric patient in early childhood with developmental conditions presents for admission. During medication reconciliation, a caregiver reports risperidone 2.5 mg BID. The dose is entered into the EMR based on verbal history alone. On subsequent review, the home medication source confirms the intended dose was 0.05 mg at bedtime, revealing a potential 10-fold dosing discrepancy.

Was this an error or a near-miss? A near-miss if caught before administration; a medication error if any dose was given. Both require full RCA and documentation.

Human factors: Reliance on verbal caregiver report alone • Repeated verification creating false reassurance (confirmation bias) • Anchoring bias: “we already confirmed it” • Decimal point confusion (for example, 0.25 vs 2.5 mg) • Off-hours cognitive load and fatigue

System factors: No hard stop or dose-range alert for pediatric risperidone • Medication reconciliation workflow allowed entry without source verification • EMR converted unverified verbal report into authoritative-looking record • No forcing function requiring “show me the bottle” for high-risk psychiatric medications

What went right: Subsequent reassessment • Family engagement with home medication source verification • Clinical monitoring in place • Near-miss recognized before serious harm

Bottom-line cause: Failure of robust medication reconciliation combined with cognitive bias under uncertainty, amplified by EMR-induced false confidence.

Subtopics

Medication reconciliation is the process of comparing a patient’s medication orders to all medications the patient has been taking. In pediatrics, this requires source verification — not just asking, but confirming with a primary source.

  • “Show me the bottle” — the single most powerful low-cost intervention for high-risk home medications
  • Cross-reference with pharmacy records for any psychiatric, anticoagulant, insulin, or chemotherapy agent
  • For admission from another facility: request medication administration record, not verbal handoff alone
  • Verbal caregiver report is a starting point, not a final source — always confirm when dose seems unusual
  • Do not allow EMR entry to substitute for source verification — once entered, the record looks “real”
Teaching Pearl
“Repeated confirmation of the same unverified source does not increase accuracy — it increases anchoring. If the first report was wrong, asking again produces the same wrong answer with more confidence.”

Certain medication classes require extra reconciliation rigor due to their narrow therapeutic index, high overdose potential, or complex dosing by weight or age.

Medication ClassKey RiskReconciliation Priority
Antipsychotics (risperidone, aripiprazole, quetiapine)Decimal errors; weight-based dosing variation; sedation riskAlways verify bottle or pharmacy record
Anticoagulants (warfarin, enoxaparin, apixaban)Narrow therapeutic index; weight-based; lot-specific dosingRequire lab confirmation before dosing; verify with hematology if applicable
Insulin10-fold errors common; pen vs vial; unit abbreviation confusionNever abbreviate “U”; always write “units”; verify type and dose separately
OpioidsWeight-based; different concentrations for infants vs older childrenVerify concentration AND dose; double-check by second nurse/pharmacist
Seizure medicationsMultiple agents; narrow windows; brand vs generic confusionConfirm generic name, brand name, and formulation; verify with neurology if complex regimen

Decimal point errors are among the most common and dangerous dosing mistakes in pediatrics. A misplaced decimal can create a 10-fold underdose or overdose.

  • Always use a leading zero: write 0.25 mg, never .25 mg
  • Never use trailing zeros: write 5 mg, never 5.0 mg
  • When a pediatric dose looks “adult-sized” for a young child, pause and recalculate before proceeding
  • Verbal communication of decimal doses should always be followed by written confirmation — “zero point two five milligrams” not “point two five”
  • EMR fields do not distinguish between 0.25 and 2.5 without dose-range checking — do not rely on the system to catch this
⚠️
The 10-fold rule: Any time a pediatric medication dose appears to differ from expectation by a factor of 10, stop. Independently recalculate using weight. Do not assume the caregiver or the prior record is correct.

This case is ideal for M&M because it is a near-miss (no harm), the error mechanism is universal (not individual), and it opens a rich systems discussion. Use psychologically safe language throughout.

  • Opening: “This is a really common situation — we all rely on caregiver history. I’m curious how you would approach this.”
  • Cognitive reflection: “What made the 0.5 mg dose seem reasonable at the time?”
  • Systems thinking: “What simple step could we add that would reliably prevent this in the future?”
  • EMR insight: “How did the EMR influence our confidence in the medication dose?”
  • Closing: “This wasn’t a knowledge problem — it was a system and cognitive problem. That’s why it can happen to any of us.”
  • Avoid: “Why didn’t you check the bottle?” / “Who made the mistake?” / “This should have been obvious”
  • Mandatory “source verified” field for high-risk medications: “Bottle / Pharmacy record / Caregiver only”
  • Pediatric-specific dose-range alerts in EMR that flag doses exceeding expected weight-based ranges
  • Decimal safety formatting: enforce leading zeros; highlight unusual decimal placement
  • Pharmacist verification for high-risk home medications such as clonidine, insulin, anticoagulants, opioids, and complex neurologic or psychiatric regimens before first administration
  • Closed-loop read-back for any verbal medication order
  • Alert fatigue is real — system alerts should be targeted (high-risk meds only), not global, to preserve their signal value
Module 2 — Human Factors

Cognitive Errors & Human Factors

Human factors science studies how people interact with complex systems under conditions of stress, fatigue, and cognitive overload. Understanding why smart people make predictable errors is the first step to building defenses against them.

The Four Core Cognitive Biases in Clinical Care

  • Anchoring bias: Over-relying on the first piece of information received. In the risperidone case: the initial verbal report of 2.5 mg became the anchor that all subsequent confirmations were measured against — not against the truth.
  • Confirmation bias: Seeking information that confirms what we already believe. “We already confirmed it three times” — each confirmation was of the same unverified source.
  • Premature closure: Stopping the diagnostic or verification process too early. Accepting the first plausible answer without considering alternatives.
  • Normalization of deviance: Gradually accepting non-standard practice as normal. “We always just ask the family” becomes the default without critical evaluation.

Situational Factors That Increase Error Risk

  • Night shift and handoff: Reduced vigilance, incomplete information transfer, cognitive fatigue — especially between 2 and 6 AM
  • High task load: Multiple simultaneous demands reduce working memory available for verification tasks
  • Interruptions: Each interruption during a high-risk task (medication order entry, handoff) resets attentional focus and increases omission error risk
  • Time pressure: Real or perceived urgency shortens verification steps — especially for medications given “before rounds”
  • Hierarchy and social dynamics: Junior trainees less likely to question a senior’s medication order, even when uncertain
Core Teaching Point
“Cognitive biases are not character flaws — they are normal features of how human brains process information under load. Building systems that interrupt these biases (checklists, forcing functions, mandatory verification steps) is more reliable than asking individuals to simply ‘be more careful.’”

Defenses You Can Build Today

Cognitive RiskBedside DefenseTeaching Cue

Confirmation biasSeek disconfirming evidence — ask “what would make me wrong?”“Show me the bottle”

Module 3 — EMR & Systems Safety

The EMR: Power Tool & Hidden Risk

The electronic medical record is the most powerful clinical tool in modern medicine — and one of the most underappreciated sources of patient harm. Understanding its limitations is a core safety competency.

💻
The central EMR safety insight: “The EMR converts uncertain information into authoritative-looking data.” Once a dose is entered, it appears identical whether it was verified with a pharmacy record or copied from an unverified verbal report. The record does not know the difference. The clinician must.
  • Structured data fields give information a clean, official appearance regardless of source quality — a typed dose looks the same whether it came from a pharmacy record or an anxious parent’s memory
  • Prior encounters carry forward medication lists that may be outdated, never verified, or based on a previous error that was never corrected
  • Copy-forward functionality propagates errors across visits and admissions without re-verification
  • Seeing it in the chart makes it feel confirmed — this is the EMR amplifying confirmation bias, not preventing it
  • Mitigation: explicitly document source of medication information (bottle / pharmacy / caregiver verbal) and flag unverified entries

Modern EMR systems generate hundreds of alerts per physician per day. Studies show that clinicians override 90–95% of drug interaction and dose alerts. The result: when a genuinely important alert appears, it looks exactly like the 200 alerts that preceded it.

  • Alert fatigue is not laziness — it is a rational response to a poorly calibrated signal system
  • Overriding a legitimate high-risk alert because it looks like the routine low-risk alerts that dominate your day is a predictable consequence of over-alerting
  • Ideal alert systems are targeted (high-risk medications only), tiered (severity-based), and interruptive only for true clinical urgency
  • When you override an alert: pause and ask “is this one of the 5% that actually matters?”
⚠️
Teaching question: “How do we avoid alert fatigue while still catching high-risk errors like this one?” The answer is system design, not individual vigilance — fewer, better-calibrated alerts preserve their signal value.

EMR downtime — planned or unplanned — exposes every workflow that has become dependent on the record. When the system goes down, clinicians who cannot function without it are a safety risk. This is an underappreciated training gap.

  • Know your downtime procedures before you need them — not during the event
  • Medication reconciliation becomes entirely manual during downtime — paper-based verification workflows must be practiced, not improvised
  • The medications that were never properly verified in the EMR become invisible during downtime — amplifying pre-existing reconciliation gaps
  • Core skill: can you safely manage your patients for 2 hours without EMR access?
Clinical Pearl — EMR Safety
“The EMR didn’t fail here — it was not designed to catch this type of error. The system cannot distinguish between verified and unverified information. That distinction requires a human clinician who understands the limitation.”
Module 4 — Communication

Difficult Conversations & the 5 A’s

Difficult conversations are not rare in pediatrics. They happen daily — when families are scared, when information is uncertain, when an error has occurred, and when the team disagrees. The goal is never to “win” the conversation. The goal is to maintain trust while keeping the patient safe.

The 5 A’s Framework

A structured approach to difficult conversations that slows the interaction, reduces escalation, and centers both empathy and safety. Most communication failures occur because clinicians skip steps 2 and 3 and jump from Acknowledge directly to Fix.

1
Acknowledge
Name the emotion or tension early. Lower intensity before adding information.
“I can see this is really frustrating and confusing.”
2
Allow
Let them speak without interruption. Most people calm after 30–60 seconds of being heard.
“Tell me what’s been going on from your perspective.”
3
Agree
Align on what the problem actually is before attempting to solve it.
“So the main concern is the medication dose — did I get that right?”
4
Affirm
Give control back. Tell them what CAN be done, not only what cannot.
“What we can do right now is review the medication together.”
5
Assure
Close the loop. Build reliability. Prevent future distrust.
“I’ll confirm this today and update you once it’s verified.”
💬
Applied to the medication case: “I can see this is confusing (Acknowledge). Let’s go through this together — can you walk me through how you’ve been giving it? (Allow). So the question is whether the dose is 0.25 or 2.5 mg (Agree). What we can do now is check the bottle and confirm step by step (Affirm). I’ll verify this today and update your medication list (Assure).”

When Tensions Rise: Practical Bedside Skills

  • Stay calm first. Your tone sets the room. Lowering your own voice lowers the temperature of the conversation.
  • Show empathy early. Acknowledge emotion before introducing facts. “I can see this is really worrying” before “the test result showed…”
  • Listen actively. Reflect back what you hear. “What I’m hearing is that your main concern is… is that right?”
  • Use positive framing. “What we can do is…” rather than “I can’t…”
  • Seek to understand before correcting. Residents often jump to correction without fully grasping the family’s concern. The wrong correction, delivered confidently, escalates.
  • Set clear, calm limits. If a conversation becomes unsafe, it’s appropriate to say: “I want to help, but I need us to speak respectfully so we can focus on your child.”
  • Avoid escalating language: “no,” “you’re wrong,” “that’s not possible.”
  • Know when to call for backup — charge nurse, social work, attending. Safety always supersedes communication.
Bedside Script — Medication Verification Under Uncertainty
“Sometimes medication labels can be confusing — would you mind showing me the bottle so we can double-check together? We want to make absolutely sure we get this exactly right.”
Bedside Script — Disclosing a Near-Miss
“I want to be transparent with you about something we caught before it caused any harm. We identified that the dose we had recorded was different from what the bottle showed. We caught it in time, and I want to walk you through exactly what happened and what we’ve done to make sure it doesn’t happen again.”

Resident Teaching Questions (Nice & Safe)

💬
Use these in M&M or teaching rounds — designed for psychological safety:

🟢 “Which of the 5 A’s do you think is hardest to do when you’re tired or stressed?”
🟢 “Where in this case could one of the A’s have changed the outcome?”
🟢 “Do we tend to jump to fixing before we fully understand the problem?”
🟢 “What is one phrase you can start using on your next shift?”
🟡 “How might the parent have experienced this interaction?”
🔴 Avoid: “Why didn’t you just…” / “This should have been obvious.”
Module 5 — Handoff & Transitions

Handoff & Transitions of Care

📋
Coming Soon — Full module in development. This module will cover I-PASS handoff structure, the highest-risk moments in pediatric sign-out, night float vulnerabilities, ICU-to-floor transition failures, closed-loop communication at shift change, and a simulation-based handoff scenario.

What You Need to Know Now

  • Handoffs are the highest-risk moments in inpatient care. Most serious adverse events in teaching hospitals are associated with communication failures at handoff — not during the primary care encounter.
  • I-PASS framework: Illness severity • Patient summary • Action list • Situation awareness and contingency planning • Synthesis by receiver
  • The “synthesis by receiver” step is the most commonly omitted — and the most important. Asking the incoming team to repeat back their understanding catches gaps in transfer
  • Medications changed within the last 2 hours of a shift are particularly high-risk at handoff — the outgoing team knows; the incoming team may not
  • For every patient at handoff, ask: “What is the one thing most likely to go wrong overnight, and what should they do when it does?”

Psychological Safety & Just Culture

Core Concepts to Know

Why Near-Misses Go Unreported

  • Fear of blame, punishment, or public shame
  • Uncertainty about what qualifies as reportable
  • The perception that reporting is burdensome with no visible benefit
  • Normalization: “This happens all the time — why report this one?”
  • Near-miss reporting is the only source of data on near-misses. What isn’t reported cannot be fixed.

Just Culture vs. Blame Culture

  • Just culture: Holds individuals accountable for reckless behavior while recognizing that most errors are system failures — not individual character failures
  • Creates an environment where reporting is safe, expected, and valued
  • Blame culture: Punishes individuals for errors regardless of system contribution — drives near-misses underground
  • Produces compliance-based safety (cover your tracks) rather than learning-based safety (fix the system)

10 Safety Principles for Every Shift

If you carry these ten principles into every clinical encounter, you will catch errors that systems miss.

1
Show me the bottle
For any unusual or high-risk home medication, verbal confirmation is not enough. Source verification is the standard.
2
Repeated confirmation ≠ increased accuracy
Confirming an unverified source three times gives you the same wrong answer with more confidence.
3
The EMR converts uncertainty into authority
An entry looks the same whether it was verified with a pharmacy record or copied from a verbal report. You must know the difference.
4
Leading zero, no trailing zero
Write 0.25 mg, never .25 mg. Write 5 mg, never 5.0 mg. Decimal errors create 10-fold dosing discrepancies.
5
Emotion first, information second
The 5 A’s: Acknowledge before you Agree, and Agree before you Affirm. Most communication failures come from skipping steps 2 and 3.
6
Cognitive bias is not a character flaw
Anchoring and confirmation bias are normal brain functions under cognitive load. Build checklists and forcing functions, not just “be more careful.”
7
Handoff is the highest-risk moment
Most serious inpatient adverse events are linked to handoff failures. I-PASS with synthesis by receiver. Always specify: “What is the one thing most likely to go wrong?”
8
Near-miss = safety signal
A near-miss is not a non-event. It reveals a system vulnerability that will produce real harm if the next sequence goes slightly differently. Report it.
9
Alert fatigue is a system problem
Overriding alerts is a rational response to poor alert calibration. Pause once per shift to ask: “Is this alert one of the 5% that actually matters?”
10
Slow is smooth, smooth is safe
The sense of urgency that causes you to skip verification steps is often manufactured, not real. Most high-risk tasks can afford 60 more seconds of deliberate care.

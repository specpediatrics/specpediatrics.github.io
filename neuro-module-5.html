Learning Objectives — Module 5
By the end of this module, the learner will be able to:
  • Differentiate upper motor neuron (UMN) from lower motor neuron (LMN) weakness patterns and localize lesions along the neuraxis
  • Recognize spinal muscular atrophy types and current treatment options including gene therapy and SMN2 splicing modifiers
  • Manage Guillain-Barré syndrome including respiratory monitoring thresholds and treatment selection
  • Understand DMD/BMD presentation, diagnosis, and multidisciplinary care including cardiac and pulmonary surveillance
  • Interpret basic NCS/EMG findings and distinguish demyelinating from axonal and myopathic patterns

Overview — Neuromuscular Disorders in Children

Neuromuscular disorders (NMDs) encompass a broad spectrum of conditions affecting the motor unit — from the anterior horn cell in the spinal cord, through the peripheral nerve and neuromuscular junction, to the muscle fiber itself. In pediatrics, these disorders range from devastating neonatal-onset conditions like SMA type 1 to slowly progressive myopathies that may not be diagnosed until adolescence.

The clinical hallmark of NMD is weakness. The pattern, distribution, tempo, and associated findings of that weakness — combined with a systematic localization framework — allow the clinician to generate a focused differential diagnosis and select the appropriate investigations. The past decade has transformed this field: conditions once considered uniformly fatal now have disease-modifying and even curative therapies, making early recognition and timely referral more important than ever.

Why This Matters to You as a Resident
You will encounter the floppy infant, the toddler who falls frequently, the child who cannot keep up with peers, and the adolescent with acute ascending weakness. Your ability to localize the problem — is this UMN or LMN? Nerve or muscle? Junction or anterior horn cell? — determines whether the right workup happens in hours or is delayed by weeks. In GBS, that distinction can be the difference between timely intubation and respiratory arrest.

Approach to Weakness

The first and most important step in evaluating a child with weakness is to localize the lesion. The motor pathway extends from the cerebral cortex through the brainstem, spinal cord, anterior horn cell, peripheral nerve, neuromuscular junction, and finally the muscle. Each level produces a characteristic pattern of weakness with distinct associated findings.

UMN vs. LMN Signs

Upper Motor Neuron
UMN Signs
Tone: Spasticity (velocity-dependent increased tone)
Reflexes: Hyperreflexia, clonus
Plantar response: Upgoing (Babinski positive)
Atrophy: Absent early; may develop late from disuse
Fasciculations: Absent
Pattern: Often affects extensors in upper limbs, flexors in lower limbs
Lower Motor Neuron
LMN Signs
Tone: Flaccidity (hypotonia)
Reflexes: Hyporeflexia or areflexia
Plantar response: Downgoing or absent (no Babinski)
Atrophy: Early and prominent
Fasciculations: Present (especially anterior horn cell disease)
Pattern: Follows myotomal or nerve distribution

Localization by Pattern

Lesion Level Pattern of Weakness Key Associated Findings
Motor cortex / internal capsule Contralateral hemiparesis UMN signs, possible aphasia, seizures; face involved
Spinal cord Bilateral weakness below the level of the lesion UMN signs below the level, LMN signs at the level; sensory level; bowel/bladder dysfunction
Anterior horn cell LMN weakness, often asymmetric Fasciculations, atrophy, no sensory loss; examples: SMA, poliomyelitis
Peripheral nerve Follows specific nerve or polyneuropathy distribution Sensory + motor involvement, hyporeflexia; distal > proximal in polyneuropathy
Neuromuscular junction Fatigable weakness — worse with repetitive use No sensory loss, no reflex changes (early), ptosis, diplopia, bulbar symptoms
Muscle Proximal > distal weakness No sensory loss, reflexes preserved or decreased late; elevated CK; Gowers sign
Resident Pearl — The Localization Reflex
Before ordering any test, ask yourself: "Where in the motor unit is this problem?" The answer dictates everything — a CK is useless in GBS, an LP is useless in DMD, and an MRI brain is useless in a myopathy. Localize first, then investigate. The physical examination is your most powerful diagnostic tool in neuromuscular disease.

Spinal Muscular Atrophy

Spinal muscular atrophy (SMA) is an autosomal recessive disorder caused by homozygous deletion or mutation of the SMN1 gene on chromosome 5q13. Loss of SMN1 leads to degeneration of anterior horn cells in the spinal cord, resulting in progressive denervation and muscle weakness. A paralogous gene, SMN2, produces a small amount of functional SMN protein — the copy number of SMN2 is the primary modifier of disease severity.

Clinical Classification

Type Onset Motor Milestone Natural History
Type 1 (Werdnig-Hoffmann) <6 months Never sits independently Severe hypotonia, tongue fasciculations, respiratory failure; historically fatal by age 2 without intervention
Type 2 6–18 months Sits but never walks independently Progressive weakness, scoliosis, respiratory compromise; survival into adulthood common
Type 3 (Kugelberg-Welander) >18 months Achieves independent walking May lose ambulation over time; proximal weakness, Gowers sign; near-normal lifespan
Type 4 Adult onset Normal childhood milestones Mild proximal weakness; ambulatory; normal lifespan

Diagnosis

SMN1 gene testing is the first-line diagnostic study. Homozygous deletion of SMN1 (both copies) confirms the diagnosis in approximately 95% of cases. SMN2 copy number should be determined simultaneously as it guides prognosis and treatment eligibility. Most states now include SMA in newborn screening panels, enabling presymptomatic diagnosis and early treatment initiation.

Treatment — A Transformed Landscape

SMA has undergone one of the most remarkable therapeutic transformations in pediatric neurology. Three disease-modifying therapies are now available:

  • Nusinersen (Spinraza) — Intrathecal antisense oligonucleotide that modifies SMN2 pre-mRNA splicing to increase functional SMN protein production. Administered by lumbar puncture on a maintenance schedule. Approved for all SMA types.
  • Onasemnogene abeparvovec (Zolgensma) — Adeno-associated virus (AAV9) gene replacement therapy delivering a functional copy of SMN1. Single intravenous infusion. Approved for children under 2 years of age. Requires hepatic monitoring due to risk of transaminitis and thrombotic microangiopathy.
  • Risdiplam (Evrysdi) — Oral small molecule SMN2 splicing modifier that increases full-length SMN protein. Daily oral administration. Approved for patients ≥2 months of age with all SMA types.
Resident Pearl — Time Is Motor Neurons
In SMA, motor neuron loss begins before birth and accelerates after delivery. Every week of delay in treatment means irreversible loss of anterior horn cells. Newborn screening has changed the paradigm — presymptomatic treatment produces dramatically better outcomes than treatment initiated after symptom onset. If you suspect SMA, order genetic testing immediately and refer urgently.
Newborn Screening
SMA is now included in the Recommended Uniform Screening Panel (RUSP) for newborn screening in the United States. A positive screen detects homozygous SMN1 deletion and should prompt immediate referral to pediatric neurology for confirmatory testing, SMN2 copy number determination, and treatment initiation — ideally within the first weeks of life.

Duchenne & Becker Muscular Dystrophy

Duchenne muscular dystrophy (DMD) and Becker muscular dystrophy (BMD) are X-linked recessive disorders caused by mutations in the dystrophin gene — the largest known human gene. The dystrophin protein stabilizes the muscle cell membrane during contraction. Its absence (DMD) or reduction (BMD) leads to progressive muscle fiber damage, necrosis, and replacement by fibrofatty tissue.

Clinical Presentation — DMD

  • Onset typically between ages 2–5 years with delayed motor milestones, frequent falls, difficulty climbing stairs
  • Gowers sign — the child uses hands to "walk up" the thighs when rising from the floor, reflecting proximal lower extremity weakness
  • Pseudohypertrophy of the calves — firm, enlarged calves due to fibrofatty infiltration replacing muscle
  • Waddling gait, toe-walking, lumbar lordosis
  • CK markedly elevated — often 10,000–50,000+ IU/L (normal <200)
  • Progressive loss of ambulation by approximately age 12
  • Cardiac involvement: Dilated cardiomyopathy develops in virtually all DMD patients — cardiac screening with echocardiography should begin at diagnosis
  • Respiratory decline: Restrictive lung disease from respiratory muscle weakness — pulmonary function testing should begin when the child can cooperate (typically around age 5–6)
  • Cognitive involvement: ~30% have intellectual disability; all should be screened for learning difficulties

Clinical Presentation — BMD

Becker muscular dystrophy presents with a similar but milder phenotype. Dystrophin protein is reduced in quantity or abnormal in structure but present. Onset is later (typically after age 5, often in adolescence), ambulation is preserved longer, and cardiac involvement — particularly dilated cardiomyopathy — may be the predominant clinical feature. CK is elevated but typically less dramatically than in DMD.

Diagnostic Approach

  • Step 1: CK — Markedly elevated CK is the initial red flag. Any boy with unexplained CK elevation should be evaluated for dystrophinopathy.
  • Step 2: Dystrophin gene testing — Multiplex ligation-dependent probe amplification (MLPA) or next-generation sequencing to identify deletions, duplications, or point mutations in the dystrophin gene.
  • Step 3: Muscle biopsy — Reserved for cases where genetic testing is inconclusive. Immunohistochemistry demonstrates absent (DMD) or reduced (BMD) dystrophin staining.

Management — Multidisciplinary Care

Domain Intervention Details
Corticosteroids Deflazacort or prednisone Prolongs ambulation by 2–5 years, preserves pulmonary and cardiac function; initiated at age 4–5 when motor plateau or decline is observed
Cardiac Echocardiogram, cardiac MRI, ACE inhibitor or ARB Annual cardiac screening from diagnosis; prophylactic ACE-I/ARB initiated by age 10 or at first sign of dysfunction
Pulmonary PFTs, cough assist, nocturnal BiPAP Monitor FVC; initiate nocturnal ventilation when FVC falls below 50% predicted or when symptomatic nocturnal hypoventilation develops
Rehabilitation PT/OT, stretching, bracing Focus on contracture prevention, maintaining function; avoid eccentric/high-resistance exercise
Orthopedic Scoliosis surveillance, fracture risk Scoliosis develops in most non-ambulatory patients; spinal fusion may be required; steroid-induced osteoporosis requires monitoring
Clinical Tip
Do Not Miss the Incidental CK
DMD is sometimes first suspected when a CK is drawn for other reasons — a liver panel showing elevated AST and ALT (which are also present in muscle), pre-surgical bloodwork, or evaluation for motor delay. If you see an unexplained CK of several thousand in a young boy, do not repeat it and wait — refer to neurology and order dystrophin gene testing. Early diagnosis allows earlier steroid initiation and better outcomes.

Guillain-Barré Syndrome & CIDP

Guillain-Barré syndrome (GBS), or acute inflammatory demyelinating polyradiculoneuropathy (AIDP), is the most common cause of acute flaccid paralysis in children after the near-eradication of poliomyelitis. It is an immune-mediated disorder in which autoantibodies attack peripheral nerve myelin (or axons in axonal variants), typically following a preceding infection.

Clinical Presentation

  • Preceding illness (viral URI, gastroenteritis — especially Campylobacter jejuni) in 50–70% of cases, typically 1–4 weeks prior
  • Symmetric ascending weakness — begins in the legs, progresses to arms, and may involve respiratory and cranial nerve muscles
  • Paresthesias and pain — often the first symptom, particularly in the back and legs
  • Hyporeflexia or areflexia — a hallmark finding; deep tendon reflexes are diminished or absent
  • Cranial nerve involvement in ~50% — facial weakness (bilateral), oropharyngeal weakness
  • Autonomic dysfunction in ~50% — labile blood pressure, tachycardia/bradycardia, urinary retention, ileus
  • Peak severity typically reached within 2–4 weeks (monophasic course)

Variants

  • Miller Fisher syndrome — triad of ataxia, areflexia, and ophthalmoplegia; associated with anti-GQ1b antibodies
  • Acute motor axonal neuropathy (AMAN) — pure motor, axonal pattern; more common after Campylobacter infection
  • Acute motor-sensory axonal neuropathy (AMSAN) — axonal with sensory involvement; poorer prognosis
  • Pharyngeal-cervical-brachial variant — weakness predominantly affects oropharynx, neck, and upper extremities
  • Pan-dysautonomia — isolated autonomic failure without significant motor involvement

Diagnostic Workup

  • CSF: Albuminocytologic dissociation — elevated protein with normal cell count. This classic finding may be absent in the first week; if clinical suspicion is high, do not wait for the LP result to initiate treatment.
  • Anti-GQ1b antibodies: Obtain when Miller Fisher variant is suspected
  • NCS/EMG: Confirms the diagnosis and distinguishes demyelinating (AIDP) from axonal variants. May be normal very early — serial studies can be helpful.
  • MRI spine with and without contrast: May show ventral nerve root enhancement — supports the diagnosis but is not required

Respiratory Monitoring — The Critical Task

Respiratory failure is the most dangerous complication of GBS. Approximately 15–20% of children with GBS require mechanical ventilation. Bedside respiratory monitoring must be performed serially — do not rely on oxygen saturation alone, as it is a late indicator of ventilatory failure.

Respiratory Thresholds — Know These
Negative Inspiratory Force (NIF): < −30 cmH₂O (less negative than −30) indicates significant inspiratory muscle weakness and approaching intubation threshold.

Forced Vital Capacity (FVC): FVC <20 mL/kg or declining trend is concerning. Values approaching 30% predicted warrant ICU monitoring and preparation for intubation.

Clinical signs: Tachypnea, use of accessory muscles, paradoxical breathing, inability to count to 20 in a single breath, weak cough — any of these should prompt escalation.

Monitor NIF and FVC every 4–6 hours in the acute phase. Trend matters as much as absolute values — a rapidly declining FVC may warrant intubation before reaching the threshold.

Treatment

  • IVIG (0.4 g/kg/day for 5 days) or plasma exchange (PLEX) — equivalent efficacy; IVIG is more commonly used in pediatrics due to ease of administration
  • IV corticosteroids have NOT shown benefit in GBS and are not recommended — this is a frequent board question and a common clinical error
  • Pain management — neuropathic pain is common and may require gabapentin, pregabalin, or opioids
  • DVT prophylaxis in immobilized patients
  • Bladder dysfunction — rare but possible; monitor urinary output
  • Cardiopulmonary monitoring for dysautonomia — continuous telemetry in the acute phase
Resident Pearl — Steroids Do Not Work in GBS
This is one of the most commonly tested facts in neurology: intravenous corticosteroids have no benefit in Guillain-Barré syndrome. Multiple randomized controlled trials have confirmed this. Do not confuse GBS with CIDP — steroids are effective in CIDP but not in acute GBS. When in doubt, treat with IVIG.

CIDP — Chronic Inflammatory Demyelinating Polyradiculoneuropathy

CIDP is defined by the same demyelinating, immune-mediated pathophysiology as AIDP but with a chronic or relapsing course — symptoms that progress or relapse for >2 months (in contrast to the monophasic course of GBS, which reaches nadir within 4 weeks).

  • Symmetric proximal and distal weakness, sensory involvement, areflexia
  • CSF shows elevated protein (as in AIDP); NCS shows demyelinating features
  • Treatment: Pulse monthly corticosteroids + IVIG; alternatives include rituximab every 6 months or chronic PLEX
  • Unlike GBS, CIDP responds to corticosteroids — this is a key distinguishing therapeutic feature
Clinical Tip
GBS vs. CIDP — The 2-Month Rule
If a patient initially diagnosed with GBS continues to worsen or relapses beyond 2 months from onset, reconsider the diagnosis as CIDP. This distinction is critical because CIDP responds to corticosteroids and requires long-term immunomodulatory therapy, whereas GBS is treated with a single course of IVIG or PLEX and is expected to be monophasic.

Myasthenia Gravis

Myasthenia gravis (MG) is an autoimmune disorder in which antibodies target components of the postsynaptic neuromuscular junction — most commonly the acetylcholine receptor (AChR), less commonly muscle-specific kinase (MuSK). The result is impaired neuromuscular transmission leading to the clinical hallmark: fatigable weakness.

Clinical Features

  • Fatigable weakness — symptoms worsen with repeated or sustained use and improve with rest; this is pathognomonic
  • Ocular symptoms — ptosis and diplopia are often the presenting complaint; ptosis may be asymmetric and worsens through the day
  • Bulbar weakness — dysarthria, dysphagia, difficulty chewing; voice becomes nasal or weak with sustained speech
  • Generalized weakness — proximal limb weakness, neck flexor weakness, respiratory muscle involvement
  • Myasthenic crisis — respiratory failure requiring mechanical ventilation; may be triggered by infection, medication changes, or surgery
  • Reflexes and sensation are normal — this distinguishes NMJ disorders from neuropathies and myopathies

Diagnosis

  • AChR antibodies — positive in ~80–85% of generalized MG; less sensitive in purely ocular disease
  • Anti-MuSK antibodies — check in AChR-seronegative patients; MuSK-positive MG has distinct clinical features (bulbar predominance, muscle atrophy)
  • Ice test — application of ice to the eyelid for 2 minutes improves ptosis by reducing acetylcholinesterase activity at the cooled junction; simple, safe bedside test
  • Repetitive nerve stimulation (RNS) — ≥10% decrement in compound muscle action potential amplitude at 2–3 Hz stimulation is diagnostic
  • Single fiber EMG — the most sensitive electrodiagnostic test; shows increased jitter and blocking
  • CT chest — evaluate for thymoma (more common in adult-onset MG; rare in childhood but should be checked)

Treatment

Therapy Mechanism Role
Pyridostigmine Cholinesterase inhibitor — increases ACh availability at the NMJ First-line symptomatic treatment; provides rapid but temporary improvement
Corticosteroids Immunosuppression Often used for initial immunotherapy; watch for transient worsening in the first 1–2 weeks
Steroid-sparing agents Azathioprine, mycophenolate mofetil Long-term immunosuppression to reduce steroid dependence; slow onset (months)
IVIG / PLEX Rapid removal or neutralization of pathogenic antibodies Used for myasthenic crisis, preoperative optimization, or severe exacerbations
Thymectomy Removal of thymus — reduces autoantibody production Recommended for thymoma; also beneficial in non-thymomatous AChR-positive generalized MG
Medications to Avoid in Myasthenia Gravis
Several commonly used medications can worsen neuromuscular transmission and precipitate myasthenic crisis. Key drugs to avoid or use with extreme caution include: aminoglycosides, fluoroquinolones, macrolides, beta-blockers, magnesium (especially IV), neuromuscular blocking agents (prolonged effect), and certain anticonvulsants. Always check before prescribing any new medication to a patient with MG.

Congenital Myopathies

Congenital myopathies are a group of genetic muscle disorders characterized by structural abnormalities in the muscle fiber visible on biopsy. Unlike the dystrophies (where muscle is progressively destroyed), congenital myopathies feature abnormal but relatively stable muscle architecture. They typically present at or near birth with hypotonia and weakness, and their course is often static or slowly progressive.

Major Subtypes

Type Biopsy Finding Key Features
Central core disease Central cores devoid of mitochondria and oxidative enzyme activity Associated with RYR1 mutations; risk of malignant hyperthermia with certain anesthetics; typically milder course
Nemaline myopathy Nemaline (rod) bodies within muscle fibers Variable severity from severe neonatal to mild childhood-onset forms; respiratory involvement is the main determinant of morbidity; facial weakness common
Centronuclear (myotubular) myopathy Centrally placed nuclei in muscle fibers X-linked form (MTM1 mutations) is severe with neonatal respiratory failure; autosomal forms milder; ophthalmoplegia may be present

General Features

  • Onset at birth or in early infancy with hypotonia ("floppy infant"), weakness, feeding difficulties
  • Proximal weakness, often with facial involvement; may have high-arched palate and elongated face
  • CK is normal or mildly elevated — in contrast to the markedly elevated CK in dystrophies
  • Respiratory involvement is the leading cause of morbidity and mortality — monitor early
  • Diagnosis requires muscle biopsy with specialized stains (oxidative enzyme stains, electron microscopy) and/or genetic testing
  • Management is supportive: respiratory support, PT/OT, nutritional optimization, orthopedic management of scoliosis and contractures
Resident Pearl — The Floppy Infant Differential
When evaluating a hypotonic infant, the first distinction is central (brain) vs. peripheral (motor unit) hypotonia. A centrally hypotonic infant is typically alert but floppy — think chromosomal disorders, brain malformations, metabolic encephalopathy. A peripherally hypotonic infant has weakness in addition to low tone — think SMA, congenital myopathy, congenital myotonic dystrophy, congenital myasthenia. Absent reflexes, tongue fasciculations, and respiratory distress all point to a peripheral cause.

NCS/EMG Interpretation Basics

Nerve conduction studies (NCS) and electromyography (EMG) are the core electrodiagnostic tools for evaluating neuromuscular disorders. While formal interpretation requires specialized training, every resident should understand the basic patterns that distinguish the major categories of neuromuscular disease.

Nerve Conduction Studies (NCS)

NCS involve stimulating a peripheral nerve and recording the electrical response. Two key parameters define the pattern:

Demyelinating Pattern
Slow Velocity, Prolonged Latency
Conduction velocity: Slow (myelin is damaged, so impulse propagation is impaired)
Distal latency: Prolonged
Amplitude: May be preserved early
Other features: Conduction block, temporal dispersion

Examples: GBS (AIDP), CIDP, Charcot-Marie-Tooth type 1
Axonal Pattern
Reduced Amplitude, Normal Velocity
Conduction velocity: Normal or near-normal (remaining axons conduct normally)
Distal latency: Normal
Amplitude: Reduced (fewer functioning axons = smaller response)
Other features: No conduction block

Examples: AMAN variant of GBS, toxic neuropathy, diabetic neuropathy

Electromyography (EMG)

EMG involves inserting a needle electrode into the muscle to assess electrical activity at rest and during voluntary contraction. Key patterns:

Finding Pattern Interpretation
Fibrillations & positive sharp waves Spontaneous activity at rest Denervation — muscle fibers have lost their nerve supply and are firing spontaneously (seen in neuropathies, anterior horn cell disease, radiculopathies)
Large, long-duration polyphasic MUAPs Voluntary contraction Chronic neurogenic process — surviving motor neurons are reinnervating orphaned muscle fibers, creating larger motor units
Small, short-duration polyphasic MUAPs Voluntary contraction Myopathic process — individual muscle fibers within motor units are damaged, producing smaller, briefer potentials
Decrement on repetitive stimulation Repetitive nerve stimulation at 2–3 Hz Neuromuscular junction disorder — progressive failure of transmission with repeated stimulation (myasthenia gravis)
Putting It Together — NCS/EMG Quick Reference
Neuropathy (demyelinating): Slow NCS velocity + prolonged latency → GBS, CIDP
Neuropathy (axonal): Reduced NCS amplitude + normal velocity → axonal GBS variant, toxic neuropathy
Anterior horn cell: Normal NCS velocity + fibrillations/fasciculations on EMG → SMA, ALS
NMJ disorder: Normal NCS + decrement on repetitive stimulation → myasthenia gravis
Myopathy: Normal NCS + small short MUAPs on EMG → muscular dystrophy, congenital myopathy

Case Vignettes

Case 1 — The Toddler Who Falls
Progressive Proximal Weakness in a 2-Year-Old Boy
A 2-year-old boy is brought by his parents for frequent falling and difficulty climbing stairs. He was walking by 15 months but has become increasingly clumsy. On examination, you note firm, enlarged calves and observe that he uses his hands on his thighs to push himself to standing from a seated position on the floor (Gowers sign). His CK returns at 25,000 IU/L. Reflexes are present but diminished at the knees. Sensation is normal.

Questions: (1) What is the most likely diagnosis? (2) What is the next diagnostic step? (3) What additional evaluations should be ordered at the time of diagnosis?

Diagnosis: Duchenne muscular dystrophy (DMD). The combination of a young boy with progressive proximal weakness, Gowers sign, calf pseudohypertrophy, and a markedly elevated CK (25,000 IU/L) is the classic presentation. The X-linked inheritance pattern means this overwhelmingly affects males.

Next step: Dystrophin gene testing (MLPA or sequencing). Muscle biopsy is reserved for cases where genetic testing is inconclusive. Do not simply repeat the CK — the diagnosis is clear and requires genetic confirmation.

Additional evaluations at diagnosis: Echocardiogram and ECG (baseline cardiac assessment — cardiomyopathy screening begins at diagnosis), baseline pulmonary function if the child can cooperate, developmental and cognitive screening, and referral to a multidisciplinary neuromuscular clinic for initiation of corticosteroid therapy, PT/OT, and comprehensive care planning.

Case 2 — Ascending Weakness After a Viral Illness
Acute Bilateral Leg Weakness in a 10-Year-Old
A 10-year-old girl presents with a 5-day history of progressive bilateral leg weakness that has now spread to involve her arms. She had a viral upper respiratory infection 2 weeks ago. On examination, she has symmetric weakness in all four extremities (legs worse than arms), areflexia, and complaints of tingling in her feet. Respiratory assessment shows a negative inspiratory force (NIF) of −25 cmH₂O and a forced vital capacity (FVC) of 35% predicted.

Questions: (1) What is the diagnosis? (2) What is the most urgent concern right now? (3) What treatment should be initiated?

Diagnosis: Guillain-Barré syndrome (GBS / AIDP). The presentation is classic: preceding viral illness, symmetric ascending weakness, areflexia, and paresthesias developing over days. The monophasic ascending pattern following a prodromal infection is pathognomonic.

Most urgent concern: Respiratory failure. Her NIF of −25 cmH₂O is approaching the intubation threshold (less negative than −30 indicates severe inspiratory muscle weakness). Her FVC of 35% predicted is critically low. This patient requires immediate ICU admission with continuous cardiopulmonary monitoring, serial NIF and FVC measurements every 4–6 hours, and preparation for intubation. Do not wait for arterial blood gas desaturation — by the time SpO₂ drops, the patient may be in extremis.

Treatment: Initiate IVIG (0.4 g/kg/day for 5 days) or plasma exchange — both have equivalent efficacy. IVIG is generally preferred in pediatrics. Do NOT give IV corticosteroids — they have no benefit in GBS. Supportive care includes pain management (neuropathic pain is common), DVT prophylaxis, and monitoring for autonomic dysfunction (arrhythmias, blood pressure lability).

Self-Assessment

Three questions to test your understanding of neuromuscular disorders. Click an answer to select it, then reveal the explanation.

Question 1 of 3
What is the hallmark cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) finding in Guillain-Barré syndrome?
  • A. Elevated protein with elevated white blood cell count (pleocytosis)
  • B. Elevated protein with normal cell count (albuminocytologic dissociation)
  • C. Normal protein with elevated white blood cell count
  • D. Low glucose with elevated protein
Correct Answer: B Albuminocytologic dissociation — elevated CSF protein with a normal cell count — is the hallmark CSF finding in GBS. It reflects breakdown of the blood-nerve barrier by the inflammatory process in the nerve roots without significant cellular infiltration into the CSF. This finding may be absent in the first week of illness, so a normal LP early in the course does not rule out GBS if clinical suspicion is high. If significant pleocytosis is present, consider alternative diagnoses such as infectious polyradiculitis (CMV, HIV, Lyme disease).
Question 2 of 3
Which treatment has NOT shown benefit in Guillain-Barré syndrome?
  • A. Intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG)
  • B. Plasma exchange (plasmapheresis)
  • C. Intravenous corticosteroids
  • D. Supportive respiratory care with mechanical ventilation
Correct Answer: C Intravenous corticosteroids have been studied in multiple randomized controlled trials and have consistently shown no benefit in GBS. Neither oral nor IV corticosteroids improve outcomes, speed recovery, or reduce the need for mechanical ventilation. This is a critical distinction from CIDP, where corticosteroids are a first-line treatment. IVIG and plasma exchange are the two proven disease-modifying treatments for GBS and have equivalent efficacy.
Question 3 of 3
A child with spinal muscular atrophy type 1 has a homozygous deletion of which gene?
  • A. Dystrophin
  • B. SMN1
  • C. SMN2
  • D. RYR1
Correct Answer: B SMA is caused by homozygous deletion or mutation of the SMN1 (survival motor neuron 1) gene on chromosome 5q13. The SMN1 gene produces the majority of full-length SMN protein needed for motor neuron survival. SMN2 is a paralogous gene that produces mostly truncated (non-functional) protein but a small amount of full-length protein — the copy number of SMN2 modifies disease severity. Dystrophin mutations cause Duchenne/Becker muscular dystrophy, and RYR1 mutations are associated with central core disease and malignant hyperthermia susceptibility.

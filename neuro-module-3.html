Learning Objectives — Module 3
By the end of this module, the learner will be able to:
  • Classify seizures using the ILAE 2017 framework (focal vs generalized vs unknown onset)
  • Recognize common pediatric epilepsy syndromes and their defining clinical features
  • Select appropriate antiseizure medications based on mechanism of action and seizure type
  • Manage status epilepticus using a timed, protocol-driven algorithm
  • Understand ketogenic diet indications, management during illness, and glucose monitoring protocols

Overview — Epilepsy in Children

Epilepsy is one of the most common neurological conditions in pediatrics, affecting approximately 0.5–1% of all children. It is defined as two or more unprovoked seizures occurring more than 24 hours apart, or one unprovoked seizure with a high probability of recurrence (≥60% over the next 10 years), or an epilepsy syndrome diagnosis.

The approach to pediatric epilepsy requires systematic classification of seizure type, identification of epilepsy syndrome where possible, selection of appropriate antiseizure medication (ASM), and a management plan that accounts for the child's developmental trajectory and quality of life.

Clinical Pearl
Not all paroxysmal events are seizures. The differential diagnosis includes syncope, breath-holding spells, parasomnias, tics, stereotypies, and psychogenic nonepileptic events (PNEE). A detailed history — including a video of the event when possible — is often more valuable than any test.

Seizure Classification — ILAE 2017

The International League Against Epilepsy (ILAE) revised its seizure classification in 2017 to improve clarity and clinical utility. Seizures are classified first by onset (focal, generalized, or unknown), then by awareness (for focal seizures), and finally by motor vs non-motor features.

Focal Onset Seizures

Focal seizures originate in networks limited to one hemisphere. They are further classified by level of awareness and predominant features:

Awareness Preserved
Focal Aware Seizure
Previously called "simple partial seizure." The patient is aware and responsive during the event. May have motor (clonic, tonic, automatisms) or non-motor (sensory, cognitive, emotional, autonomic) features.
Awareness Impaired
Focal Impaired Awareness Seizure
Previously called "complex partial seizure." Awareness is impaired or lost at any point during the seizure. Commonly arises from the temporal lobe and may include automatisms (lip smacking, fumbling).

Generalized Onset Seizures

Generalized seizures originate in and rapidly engage bilaterally distributed networks. They are classified as motor or non-motor:

Category Seizure Type Key Features
Motor Tonic-clonic Bilateral tonic stiffening → rhythmic clonic jerking; often with postictal state
Tonic Sustained bilateral stiffening without clonic phase
Clonic Bilateral rhythmic jerking without preceding tonic phase
Myoclonic Brief, shock-like bilateral jerks; often in clusters; no loss of consciousness
Atonic Sudden loss of muscle tone ("drop attacks"); high injury risk
Epileptic spasms Sudden flexion/extension of trunk and limbs; clusters typical; associated with West syndrome
Non-motor (Absence) Typical absence Brief staring (5–30 sec), abrupt onset/offset, 3 Hz spike-wave on EEG; hyperventilation provokes
Atypical absence Less abrupt onset/offset, often with tone changes; slower (<2.5 Hz) spike-wave; associated with LGS

Unknown Onset Seizures

When onset is not witnessed or cannot be determined, seizures are classified as unknown onset. This category includes:

  • Motor: Tonic-clonic seizures and epileptic spasms where onset type is unclear
  • Non-motor: Behavior arrest — the event is characterized primarily by cessation of activity
Classification Hierarchy
Step 1: Onset type (focal → generalized → unknown)  →  Step 2: Awareness level (focal only: aware vs impaired awareness)  →  Step 3: Motor vs non-motor features  →  Step 4: Focal to bilateral tonic-clonic (if applicable, replaces old term "secondary generalization")

Epilepsy Syndromes

Epilepsy syndromes are defined by a constellation of features including seizure types, EEG patterns, age of onset, and clinical course. Syndrome recognition is essential because it guides treatment selection, predicts prognosis, and may indicate specific genetic etiologies.

Dravet Syndrome (Severe Myoclonic Epilepsy of Infancy)

  • Presentation: First year of life with prolonged febrile seizures, often hemiclonic, in a child with previously normal development
  • Status epilepticus is common and may be the initial presenting event in approximately 50% of cases
  • Seizure evolution: Additional seizure types develop between ages 1–4 years, including myoclonic, focal, and atypical absence seizures
  • Prognosis: Associated with intractable epilepsy and progressive psychomotor decline
  • EEG: May be normal initially; becomes progressively abnormal with age
  • Etiology: 70–80% have pathogenic SCN1A mutations (voltage-gated sodium channel gene)
High-Yield Pearl — Dravet Syndrome
Treatment: Valproate, clobazam, topiramate, levetiracetam, stiripentol, ketogenic diet, and cannabidiol (Epidiolex — not first-line). CRITICAL — AVOID sodium channel blockers (carbamazepine, lamotrigine, phenytoin, oxcarbazepine) as they worsen seizure frequency due to the underlying SCN1A channelopathy.

Juvenile Myoclonic Epilepsy (JME / Janz Syndrome)

A generalized genetic epilepsy presenting in otherwise healthy teenagers, typically with one or more of three seizure types:

  • Myoclonic jerks (100%): The hallmark — isolated jerks of the arms, typically occurring in the morning within 1 hour of awakening. Patients may describe dropping objects or spilling coffee
  • Generalized tonic-clonic seizures (85–100%): May have focal features; often what brings the patient to medical attention
  • Absence seizures (20–40%): May precede the myoclonic jerks by up to 5 years
  • EEG: Interictal frontal-predominant 4–6 Hz polyspike-and-slow-wave complexes with normal background activity
  • Treatment: Valproate (first-line), levetiracetam (alternative first-line). Lamotrigine may be used but can worsen myoclonus
  • Prognosis: Lifelong treatment is usually required; high relapse rate if medications are withdrawn
Key Differentiator
JME is provoked by sleep deprivation, alcohol, and photic stimulation. Morning myoclonus in a teenager — especially with a history of "clumsiness" on awakening — should immediately raise suspicion for JME, even if the presenting complaint is a first GTC seizure.

Infantile Spasms (West Syndrome)

  • Classic triad: Epileptic spasms, hypsarrhythmia on EEG, and developmental regression/arrest
  • Age of onset: Typically 3–12 months (peak 4–7 months)
  • Spasm description: Sudden flexion or extension of the trunk and limbs, occurring in clusters — often upon awakening
  • EEG — Hypsarrhythmia: Chaotic, high-amplitude, disorganized slow-wave background with multifocal spikes
  • Etiology: Structural (e.g., tuberous sclerosis), genetic, metabolic, or unknown in ~40%
  • Treatment: ACTH (adrenocorticotropic hormone) or vigabatrin (first-line for tuberous sclerosis complex). Early treatment is critical to optimize neurodevelopmental outcomes
  • Prognosis: Many evolve to Lennox-Gastaut syndrome; developmental outcomes are often poor, especially if etiology is structural
Clinical Pearl — Infantile Spasms
Infantile spasms are a neurological emergency. The time from symptom onset to treatment initiation directly correlates with developmental outcome. Spasms may be subtle — parents often describe them as "startles" or "colic." A high index of suspicion and urgent EEG are essential.

Lennox-Gastaut Syndrome (LGS)

  • Onset: Typically 1–7 years of age; may evolve from West syndrome
  • Seizure types: Multiple types including tonic (especially during sleep), atypical absence, atonic ("drop attacks"), and myoclonic seizures
  • EEG: Slow spike-and-wave complexes (<2.5 Hz) during wakefulness; generalized paroxysmal fast activity (GPFA) during sleep
  • Cognitive: Progressive intellectual disability is the rule
  • Treatment: Valproate, clobazam, lamotrigine, rufinamide, cannabidiol, and ketogenic diet. Seizures are typically drug-resistant

Childhood Absence Epilepsy (CAE)

  • Onset: 4–10 years (peak 5–7 years); more common in girls
  • Seizures: Frequent typical absences (staring episodes, 5–30 seconds), abrupt onset and offset, dozens per day. May be accompanied by eyelid fluttering or subtle automatisms
  • EEG: Bilateral, synchronous, symmetric 3 Hz spike-and-wave discharges, easily provoked by hyperventilation
  • Treatment: Ethosuximide (first-line for absence-only), valproate (if GTC seizures are also present), lamotrigine (if ethosuximide and valproate are not tolerated)
  • Prognosis: ~65–70% remit by adolescence. Risk of GTC seizures in adulthood if EEG features are atypical

Self-Limited Epilepsy with Centrotemporal Spikes (SeLECTS / Benign Rolandic Epilepsy)

  • Onset: 3–13 years (peak 7–10 years); the most common focal epilepsy of childhood
  • Seizures: Brief focal motor seizures involving the face (hemifacial twitching, drooling, speech arrest), typically occurring during sleep or on awakening. May secondarily generalize
  • EEG: Centrotemporal (rolandic) spikes with a characteristic dipole morphology, activated by sleep. Normal background
  • Treatment: Many children do not require treatment if seizures are infrequent and nocturnal. If treated: levetiracetam, oxcarbazepine, or carbamazepine
  • Prognosis: Excellent — seizures virtually always remit by age 16. This is a self-limited epilepsy
Clinical Tip
Syndrome Recognition Matters for Prognosis
Correctly identifying the epilepsy syndrome is essential for setting realistic expectations. A child with SeLECTS can be reassured that seizures will almost certainly remit. A child with Dravet syndrome requires a very different conversation about lifelong management and developmental trajectory.

Antiseizure Medication Pharmacology

Antiseizure medications (ASMs) work through diverse mechanisms. Understanding these mechanisms is critical for rational drug selection, predicting drug interactions, and avoiding medications that may worsen specific seizure types.

Target Mechanism Medications
Voltage-Gated Na+ Channel Channel blocker Carbamazepine, Oxcarbazepine, Eslicarbazepine, Lamotrigine, Phenytoin, Valproate*, Topiramate*, Zonisamide*
Voltage-Gated K+ Channel (KCNQ) Potentiator Retigabine (Ezogabine)
Voltage-Gated Ca²+ Channel — α2δ1 Trafficking inhibition Pregabalin, Gabapentin
Voltage-Gated Ca²+ Channel — T-type T-type blocker Ethosuximide, Valproate*, Zonisamide*
Synaptic Vesicle SV2A Unknown (molecular affinity) Levetiracetam, Brivaracetam
NMDA Receptor Direct antagonist Ketamine
AMPA Receptor Direct antagonist Perampanel
GABA-A Receptor — BZD site Positive allosteric modulator (↑ frequency of Cl⁻ channel opening) Lorazepam, Diazepam, Clonazepam, Clobazam, Midazolam
GABA-A Receptor — Barbiturate site Positive allosteric modulator (↑ duration of Cl⁻ channel opening) Phenobarbital
GABA-A Receptor — Other Positive allosteric modulator Propofol
GABA Transporter (GAT-1) Reuptake inhibition Tiagabine
GABA Transaminase Irreversible enzyme inhibition Vigabatrin

* Denotes medications with multiple mechanisms of action

High-Yield Pearl — BZD vs Barbiturate Mechanism
Benzodiazepines increase the frequency of GABA-A chloride channel opening. Barbiturates increase the duration of channel opening. Both enhance GABAergic inhibition but through distinct allosteric mechanisms. This distinction is a classic board question.

Matching ASM to Seizure Type

Focal Seizures
First-Line ASMs
Oxcarbazepine, carbamazepine, levetiracetam, lamotrigine. Sodium channel blockers are particularly effective for focal-onset seizures.
Generalized Seizures
First-Line ASMs
Valproate (broad-spectrum), levetiracetam, lamotrigine (caution with myoclonus), ethosuximide (absence only). Avoid Na+ channel blockers in generalized epilepsies — they may worsen absence and myoclonic seizures.
Critical Safety Point
Sodium channel blockers can worsen seizures in certain epilepsy syndromes. Never use carbamazepine, oxcarbazepine, phenytoin, or lamotrigine (at higher doses) in Dravet syndrome or other sodium channelopathies. These agents may also exacerbate absence seizures in CAE and myoclonic seizures in JME.

Status Epilepticus — Timed Protocol

Status epilepticus (SE) is defined as a seizure lasting ≥5 minutes or two or more seizures without return to baseline between them. It is a medical emergency requiring rapid, protocol-driven management. The timed approach ensures escalation occurs without unnecessary delays.

Time Phase Intervention
0–5 min Stabilization ABCs, position of safety, check glucose, obtain IV access. First-line benzodiazepine: Lorazepam 0.1 mg/kg IV (max 4 mg) OR Midazolam 0.2 mg/kg IM (max 10 mg) if no IV access
5–10 min Repeat BZD If still seizing: repeat the same benzodiazepine dose once. Maximum of 2 doses total
10–20 min Second-line agent Choose ONE: Fosphenytoin 20 PE/kg IV (max rate 150 PE/min) OR Levetiracetam 60 mg/kg IV (max 4500 mg, over 15 min) OR Valproate 40 mg/kg IV (max 3000 mg, over 10 min)
20–40 min Ongoing SE If still seizing: consider a second, different second-line agent OR escalate to third-line therapy. Prepare for ICU transfer if not already there
>40 min Refractory SE Continuous infusion: Midazolam drip (0.1–0.4 mg/kg/hr), Pentobarbital, or Propofol. Requires continuous EEG monitoring, intubation, and ICU-level care
Clinical Pearl — Time is Brain
The most common error in status epilepticus management is delay — delay in giving the first benzodiazepine, delay in escalating to second-line agents, and delay in recognizing refractory SE. Each phase should be on a clock. Benzodiazepines become less effective as SE persists due to GABA-A receptor internalization.
Benzodiazepine Dosing Quick Reference
IV access available: Lorazepam 0.1 mg/kg IV (max 4 mg per dose)  |  No IV access: Midazolam 0.2 mg/kg IM (max 10 mg) OR Diazepam 0.3–0.5 mg/kg rectal (max 20 mg). IM midazolam has been shown to be non-inferior to IV lorazepam in the prehospital setting.

EEG Basics

The electroencephalogram (EEG) is the most important neurodiagnostic tool in epilepsy. It records electrical activity from the cerebral cortex via scalp electrodes placed according to the International 10-20 system. Understanding key EEG patterns is essential for syndrome identification and clinical decision-making.

Key EEG Patterns by Syndrome

Syndrome EEG Pattern Key Features
Childhood Absence Epilepsy 3 Hz spike-and-wave Bilateral, synchronous, symmetric; provoked by hyperventilation; normal background
JME 4–6 Hz polyspike-and-slow-wave Frontal predominant; interictal; normal background; photic sensitivity common
West Syndrome Hypsarrhythmia Chaotic, high-amplitude, disorganized slow waves with multifocal spikes; severely abnormal background
Lennox-Gastaut Slow spike-and-wave (<2.5 Hz) Generalized; with generalized paroxysmal fast activity (GPFA) during sleep; abnormal background
SeLECTS (Rolandic) Centrotemporal spikes Characteristic dipole morphology; activated by sleep; normal background
Dravet Syndrome Normal → progressive abnormalities EEG may be normal early; generalized and multifocal epileptiform discharges develop with age

EEG Terminology Essentials

  • Spike: Sharp transient lasting 20–70 ms; indicates cortical irritability
  • Sharp wave: Similar to spike but 70–200 ms duration
  • Spike-and-wave: Spike followed by a slow wave; hallmark of generalized epilepsies
  • Polyspike: Two or more spikes in rapid succession, followed by slow wave; seen in myoclonic epilepsies
  • Photoparoxysmal response: Epileptiform discharges triggered by photic stimulation; seen in JME and some generalized epilepsies
  • Electrodecremental response: Sudden diffuse attenuation of background; seen during epileptic spasms
Clinical Tip
A Normal EEG Does Not Exclude Epilepsy
A single routine EEG captures only 20–30 minutes of brain activity. Up to 50% of patients with epilepsy may have a normal initial EEG. If clinical suspicion is high, consider repeat EEG, prolonged EEG monitoring, or sleep-deprived EEG to increase diagnostic yield.

Ketogenic Diet

The ketogenic diet is a high-fat, low-carbohydrate, adequate-protein diet that shifts the brain's primary energy substrate from glucose to ketone bodies. It is an established, evidence-based treatment for drug-resistant epilepsy, with efficacy rates of 50–60% achieving ≥50% seizure reduction and approximately 10–15% achieving seizure freedom.

Indications

  • Drug-resistant epilepsy: Failure of two or more appropriate ASMs
  • Specific syndromes: Particularly effective in Dravet syndrome, infantile spasms, Lennox-Gastaut, myoclonic-astatic epilepsy (Doose syndrome), and tuberous sclerosis complex
  • Metabolic indications: First-line therapy for GLUT1 deficiency syndrome and pyruvate dehydrogenase deficiency (the diet bypasses the metabolic defect)

Management During Illness (Inpatient)

Children on the ketogenic diet require specialized management when hospitalized. Inadvertent carbohydrate administration can break ketosis and trigger breakthrough seizures.

High-Yield Pearl — Ketogenic Diet Inpatient Protocol
Fluids: Carbohydrate-free fluids only. Limit Pedialyte use. Dextrose-free IV fluids — use Normal Saline (NS) or Lactated Ringer's (LR). No D5, D10, or dextrose-containing fluids.

Labs: BMP every 12 hours for the first 24 hours of admission. BHB (beta-hydroxybutyrate) level on admission to confirm ketosis.

Glucose monitoring: Check blood glucose every 6 hours when NPO >4 hours.

Hypoglycemia Protocol for Ketogenic Diet Patients

If glucose <40 mg/dL and the patient is able to swallow:

Glucose Level Treatment
25–40 mg/dL 15 mL apple juice by mouth
20–25 mg/dL 30 mL apple juice by mouth
<20 mg/dL 30 mL apple juice by mouth + recheck glucose in 15 minutes

If glucose <40 mg/dL and the patient is unable to swallow:

Age Treatment
<2 years 30 mL D10% IV over 30 minutes
≥2 years 60 mL D10% IV over 30 minutes
Medication Considerations
Limit total carbohydrate intake to <1000 mg/day from all sources including medications. Avoid liquid medication preparations (they often contain sugar or sorbitol). IV antiseizure medications are carbohydrate-free. Important: "Sugar free" does NOT equal "carbohydrate free" — always check the total carbohydrate content of any medication.

VNS Therapy

The Vagus Nerve Stimulator (VNS) is a surgically implanted neuromodulation device used as adjunctive therapy for drug-resistant epilepsy. It delivers intermittent electrical impulses to the left vagus nerve via a lead connected to a pulse generator implanted in the chest wall.

Indications & General Overview

  • Indication: Adjunctive therapy for patients ≥4 years with focal or generalized epilepsy refractory to medication
  • Mechanism: Modulates brainstem nuclei (nucleus tractus solitarius → widespread cortical projections) to raise seizure threshold
  • Efficacy: Approximately 50% of patients experience ≥50% seizure reduction over time; efficacy tends to improve with longer duration of use
  • Magnet activation: Swiping a magnet over the generator delivers an on-demand stimulation — used by patients or caregivers at seizure onset to potentially abort or shorten seizures

Device Interrogation Procedure

  • Step 1: Turn on the programming tablet
  • Step 2: Select "Interrogate Device" from the menu
  • Step 3: Hold the programming wand over the VNS generator (left upper chest)
  • Step 4: Review current parameters (output current, signal frequency, pulse width, on/off times)

Programming the VNS

  • Select new parameters as determined by the epilepsy team
  • Hold the wand over the generator and click "Program"
  • Re-interrogate the device to confirm that new settings have been applied

System Diagnostics & Integrity Testing

  • Interrogate the device first to access the diagnostics menu
  • Select "System Diagnostics" → "Lead Test" to verify lead impedance
  • Check magnet registration to ensure the magnet-activation feature is functional
Critical Safety — MRI & VNS
The VNS must be turned OFF before the patient undergoes MRI. After the MRI is completed, the device must be reprogrammed to its prior settings. Failure to deactivate the VNS can result in tissue heating at the lead tip and potential vagus nerve injury. Always confirm VNS deactivation with interrogation before MRI clearance.

Case Vignettes

Work through each clinical scenario before revealing the teaching points. These cases integrate seizure classification, syndrome recognition, and pharmacotherapy decision-making.

Case Vignette 1
The Infant with a Prolonged Febrile Seizure
A 6-month-old male presents after a prolonged (20-minute) febrile seizure that was hemiclonic (involving the right arm and leg). He has previously been developing normally with no prior seizures. Initial EEG in the emergency department is normal. The attending physician is considering starting an antiseizure medication.

Question: What epilepsy syndrome should you consider in this child, and what class of medications should you specifically AVOID?

Diagnosis to consider: Dravet Syndrome.

This presentation — a prolonged, hemiclonic febrile seizure in the first year of life in a previously normally developing infant — is the classic initial presentation of Dravet syndrome. Approximately 50% of Dravet patients present with status epilepticus as their first event. The normal initial EEG does not exclude the diagnosis — the EEG often becomes progressively abnormal over time.

Medications to AVOID: Sodium channel blockers — specifically carbamazepine, oxcarbazepine, phenytoin, and lamotrigine. These agents worsen seizure frequency in Dravet syndrome due to the underlying SCN1A channelopathy (present in 70–80% of cases).

Appropriate first-line agents include valproate, clobazam, levetiracetam, or topiramate. Genetic testing for SCN1A should be sent. If Dravet is confirmed, stiripentol and cannabidiol (Epidiolex) may be considered as adjunctive therapies.

Case Vignette 2
The Teenager with Morning Clumsiness
A 16-year-old female presents to the emergency department after her first generalized tonic-clonic seizure at school. On further history, her parents note that for the past 6 months she has been "clumsy in the morning" — dropping her cereal spoon, knocking over her glass of juice — always within the first hour of waking. She is otherwise healthy and doing well in school. EEG shows interictal 4–6 Hz polyspike-and-slow-wave discharges with frontal predominance and a normal background.

Question: What is the most likely diagnosis and the first-line treatment?

Diagnosis: Juvenile Myoclonic Epilepsy (JME / Janz Syndrome).

This is a textbook presentation of JME. The "morning clumsiness" actually represents myoclonic jerks — the hallmark seizure type of JME — occurring within 1 hour of awakening. The GTC seizure that brought her to medical attention likely occurred after a period of sleep deprivation or other provocation. The EEG showing 4–6 Hz polyspike-and-slow-wave complexes with frontal predominance confirms the diagnosis.

First-line treatment: Valproate. Valproate remains the most effective ASM for JME, controlling all three seizure types (myoclonic, GTC, and absence). In women of childbearing age, the teratogenic risk of valproate must be carefully discussed; levetiracetam is an alternative first-line agent with a better safety profile in pregnancy.

Key counseling: JME is a lifelong condition. Seizures are provoked by sleep deprivation, alcohol, and photic stimulation. Medication adherence and lifestyle modification are essential. Relapse rates are high if medications are withdrawn — most patients require lifelong treatment.

Self-Assessment

Three questions to test your understanding of epilepsy and seizure management. Click an answer to select it, then reveal the explanation.

Question 1 of 3
Which antiseizure medication mechanism involves molecular affinity for the synaptic vesicle protein SV2A?
  • A. Ethosuximide and valproate — T-type calcium channel blockade
  • B. Levetiracetam and brivaracetam — SV2A binding
  • C. Gabapentin and pregabalin — α2δ1 subunit binding
  • D. Perampanel — AMPA receptor antagonism
Correct Answer: B Levetiracetam and brivaracetam are the two ASMs that exert their effects through binding to the synaptic vesicle glycoprotein SV2A. The exact downstream mechanism by which SV2A binding produces an antiseizure effect is not fully elucidated, but it is thought to modulate neurotransmitter release. Brivaracetam has approximately 15–30x higher affinity for SV2A than levetiracetam. The other answer choices describe real ASM mechanisms — but they involve different molecular targets.
Question 2 of 3
A child presents with ongoing seizure activity at 3 minutes. What is the correct first-line benzodiazepine and dose for status epilepticus if IV access is available?
  • A. Midazolam 0.2 mg/kg IV
  • B. Diazepam 0.1 mg/kg IV
  • C. Lorazepam 0.1 mg/kg IV (max 4 mg)
  • D. Phenobarbital 20 mg/kg IV
Correct Answer: C Lorazepam 0.1 mg/kg IV (maximum 4 mg per dose) is the first-line benzodiazepine when IV access is available. Lorazepam is preferred over diazepam for IV administration because it has a longer duration of action in the brain (despite a shorter half-life), reducing the risk of seizure recurrence. If IV access is NOT available, intramuscular midazolam 0.2 mg/kg (max 10 mg) is the preferred route. Phenobarbital is a second-line agent, not first-line, in status epilepticus protocols.
Question 3 of 3
A 2-year-old with genetically confirmed Dravet syndrome (SCN1A mutation) is having worsening seizures. The covering physician is considering adding carbamazepine. Why should this be avoided?
  • A. Carbamazepine has too many drug interactions with current ASMs
  • B. Carbamazepine is not approved for use in children under 6 years
  • C. Sodium channel blockers worsen seizure frequency in Dravet syndrome due to the underlying SCN1A channelopathy
  • D. Carbamazepine is only effective for absence seizures, not the seizure types seen in Dravet
Correct Answer: C Dravet syndrome is caused by loss-of-function mutations in SCN1A, which encodes the α1 subunit of the voltage-gated sodium channel (Nav1.1). This channel is predominantly expressed on GABAergic inhibitory interneurons. Sodium channel blockers (carbamazepine, oxcarbazepine, phenytoin, and at higher doses, lamotrigine) further reduce the function of these already-impaired inhibitory neurons, paradoxically worsening seizure frequency and potentially precipitating status epilepticus. This is one of the most important pharmacogenomic interactions in pediatric neurology.

Understanding the Basics

What Is a Tracheostomy?

A tracheostomy (or "trach") is a small opening made in the front of your child's neck so a breathing tube can be placed directly into the windpipe. You can manage this safely at home — thousands of families do.

01

Why It Was Placed

Your child may need a trach because of a blocked airway, prolonged need for a ventilator, a neurological condition, or a birth difference. The trach helps them breathe more comfortably and safely.

02

Parts of the Trach Tube

The outer cannula is the main tube. The inner cannula (on some tubes) can be removed for cleaning. The flange is the flat part that rests on the neck and holds it in place with ties.

03

Your Child Can Still Thrive

Most children with trachs attend school, play, and grow normally. With proper care and preparation, life at home in West Texas is very manageable — even with long distances to clinics.

Step-by-Step

Daily Tracheostomy Care

Always wash your hands for at least 20 seconds before any care. Have your emergency supplies nearby before you begin.

1

Gather Your Supplies

Before touching the trach, collect: saline, suction catheter, suction machine, extra trach tube (same size + one size smaller), trach ties, gloves, and gauze.

Before Every Care
2

Suctioning the Airway

Insert the catheter gently — no further than 1 cm past the tube tip. Apply suction only while withdrawing. Limit each pass to 10 seconds. Allow rest between attempts.

As Needed / Every 4–8 hrs
3

Stoma Skin Care

Use a damp gauze or pre-cut trach dressing around the stoma. Gently clean any crusting or secretions. Pat dry — never leave moisture under the flange as it can cause skin breakdown.

2–3× Daily
4

Changing the Trach Ties

Always have a second person present. New ties should be snug enough that only one finger fits underneath. Never remove old ties before new ones are secured.

Daily or When Wet/Soiled
5

Inner Cannula Cleaning

Remove the inner cannula (if your tube has one). Rinse with sterile saline or clean with a small brush. Let it dry or use a clean, lint-free cloth before reinserting.

Every 8–12 hrs
6

Full Tube Change

Your care team will train you on this. Changes are usually done every 1–4 weeks. Have your extra tube (same size) and a smaller backup tube ready. Deflate the cuff (if present) before removing.

Per Your Physician's Schedule
7

Humidification

The trach bypasses the nose and mouth which normally warm and humidify air. Use a heat-moisture exchanger (HME/trach collar) especially in dry West Texas air and air conditioning.

Continuous / As Directed
8

Document & Monitor

Keep a simple log: secretion color and amount, skin condition, how many times you suctioned, and any concerns. Bring this to every clinic visit at UMC or Texas Tech.

Daily

Supplies & Equipment

What to Keep at Home

Because many families travel 1–3 hours to reach Lubbock, keeping a well-stocked supply kit at home is essential. Talk to your care team about getting a 30-day supply buffer.

🧲

Suction Machine

Portable + backup. Keep charged. Know how to troubleshoot.

🔵

Suction Catheters

Correct size for your child's tube. Keep at least 10 on hand.

🩹

Extra Trach Tubes

Same size + one size smaller. Check expiry monthly.

💧

Sterile Saline

For cleaning and thinning secretions. Keep several vials.

🧤

Gloves

Non-latex if your child has latex sensitivity.

🩺

Trach Dressings

Pre-cut gauze pads. Never cut standard gauze (fraying is a hazard).

🎀

Trach Ties / Velcro Holders

Change daily or when soiled. Velcro holders are easy for caregivers.

💨

HME / Trach Collar

Humidity is critical in dry West Texas. Use as directed by your team.

Safety

Warning Signs to Know

Learn these signs. When in doubt — call or go in. Distance is not a reason to wait.

🚨 Call 911 Immediately

  • Trach tube has completely fallen out and you cannot replace it
  • Child is not breathing or lips/fingernails turning blue
  • Severe respiratory distress — pulling in of chest, neck
  • Child is unresponsive or unconscious
  • Massive bleeding from the stoma
  • Suction is not clearing blockage and child is struggling

⚠️ Call SPEC / Your Care Team Today

  • Secretions change color (yellow, green, brown, or bloody)
  • Increased secretion amount or thickness
  • Stoma skin is red, swollen, or has discharge
  • Child is more irritable, feverish, or breathing faster than normal
  • Difficulty passing the suction catheter
  • Trach tube appears to be moving or shifting
  • Granulation tissue (pink/red bumpy tissue) forming around stoma

⚡ If the Trach Tube Falls Out (Accidental Decannulation)

  1. Stay calm. Your child is watching you.
  2. Call for help — you need a second person if possible.
  3. Have your emergency trach tube ready (same size). Insert it gently into the stoma.
  4. If you cannot reinsert — use the one size smaller backup tube.
  5. If you still cannot reinsert — cover the stoma with a clean cloth and give rescue breaths through the mouth while calling 911.
  6. If stoma is less than 7 days old — do NOT attempt reinsertion. Call 911 immediately.

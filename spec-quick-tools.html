Diagnosis — ≥6 Years
Asthma Diagnosis Checklist

Step 1 — Symptom Pattern

  • Wheeze, shortness of breath, chest tightness, or cough
  • Symptoms vary over time and in intensity
  • Symptoms worse at night or on waking
  • Triggered by exercise, allergens, cold air, or viral URIs
  • Symptoms improve spontaneously or with bronchodilator

Step 2 — Objective Confirmation

  • FEV&sub1;/FVC reduced below lower limit of normal
  • Post-BD FEV&sub1; increase ≥12% of predicted (children)
  • PEF variability >13% over 2 weeks
  • Positive exercise challenge or methacholine (if available)
  • Significant improvement in FEV&sub1; after 4 weeks ICS trial

Supportive Features

  • Personal or family history of atopy (eczema, allergic rhinitis, food allergy)
  • Eosinophilia ≥4% or elevated total IgE
  • Elevated FeNO (≥25 ppb supports eosinophilic inflammation)
  • Symptoms improve with allergen avoidance

GINA 2025 rule: Diagnosis requires BOTH a compatible symptom history AND objective evidence of variable expiratory airflow limitation. Normal spirometry between episodes does not exclude asthma — repeat during symptoms.

When to Reconsider the Diagnosis

  • Normal spirometry with no reversibility during symptoms
  • Symptoms that do not respond to bronchodilators or ICS
  • Isolated cough without wheeze, dyspnea, or chest tightness
  • Onset before 6 months of age; features of alternative diagnosis
  • Digital clubbing, failure to thrive, persistent productive cough → refer urgently
Diagnosis — Under 6 Years
Preschool Diagnosis Framework

Key principle: Asthma cannot be confirmed by objective testing in routine practice under age 5–6. Use a probability-based approach based on symptom pattern, clinical features, and risk factors.

Features Favoring Asthma

  • Wheeze between viral illnesses (not only with colds)
  • Wheeze triggered by allergens, exercise, laughing
  • Personal history of eczema or food allergy
  • Parental history of asthma
  • Good response to bronchodilator during episodes
  • Good response to ICS trial over 2–3 months

Red Flags — Refer Urgently

  • Symptoms since birth or first weeks of life
  • Neonatal respiratory disease / prematurity + RDS
  • Persistent productive or moist cough
  • Digital clubbing
  • Failure to thrive or poor weight gain
  • Wheeze with feeds or recurrent vomiting
  • Focal abnormality on exam or CXR

Asthma Predictive Index (API) — Original

Positive API = ≥3 wheeze episodes in past year plus 1 major criterion or 2 minor criteria

Major CriteriaMinor Criteria
Parental asthma (physician-diagnosed)Allergic rhinitis (physician-diagnosed)
Physician-diagnosed eczema in childPeripheral eosinophilia ≥4%
Aeroallergen sensitizationWheezing unrelated to colds

Therapeutic Trial Decision

  • Trial of daily low-dose ICS for 2–3 months if: API positive, or SABA needed >2×/week, or ≥3 exacerbations/year requiring OCS
  • Assess response at 4–6 weeks: improvement in frequency/severity supports asthma diagnosis
  • Stop ICS after 3 months and reassess — if symptoms return, restart
  • If no response to ICS trial at adequate dose with confirmed adherence — re-evaluate diagnosis

Classification

ScoreClassificationAction
0 of 4Well ControlledMaintain; consider step-down if stable ≥3 months
1–2 of 4Partly ControlledReview technique, adherence, triggers; consider step-up
3–4 of 4UncontrolledCheck modifiable factors; step up therapy

Future Risk Factors (Check at Every Visit)

  • Uncontrolled symptoms (any of the 4 above)
  • One or more severe exacerbations in the past year
  • SABA overuse: ≥3 canisters (200-dose) per year; ≥12/year = high risk of death
  • Low FEV&sub1; — especially if <60% predicted
  • Confirmed sensitization + ongoing allergen exposure
  • Smoking exposure (patient or household)
  • Confirmed poor adherence or incorrect technique
  • Obesity, food allergy, eosinophilia, elevated FeNO

Two domains of control: Symptom control (how the patient feels now) and future risk (risk of exacerbations, loss of lung function, and medication side effects). Good symptom control does not guarantee low future risk — assess both at every visit.

pMDI + Spacer Steps

  • Remove cap; shake vigorously 5 seconds
  • Prime if new or unused >2 weeks (4 sprays)
  • Attach inhaler to spacer
  • Patient exhales gently away from spacer
  • Lips sealed around mouthpiece (or mask on face)
  • Press canister once as slow inhalation begins
  • Inhale slowly and steadily 3–5 seconds
  • Hold breath 5–10 seconds
  • Wait 30–60 sec before second puff
  • After ICS: rinse mouth, gargle, spit

DPI Steps (Diskus / Flexhaler)

  • Load device (slide lever / twist base until click)
  • Do NOT shake DPI after loading
  • Exhale fully — away from mouthpiece (moisture clumps powder)
  • Seal lips firmly around mouthpiece
  • Inhale fast and forcefully (opposite of pMDI)
  • Hold breath 10 seconds
  • After ICS: rinse mouth, gargle, spit
  • Flexhaler requires ≥60 L/min PIF — not ideal for young children

Always perform return demonstration: Watch the patient use their device, identify at least one error, correct it, then watch again. Document that technique was reviewed. GINA 2025 recommends checking technique at every visit.

SpO&sub2; Targets by Age

  • Children 1–5y: ≥95%
  • Children 6–12y: ≥94%
  • Adolescents ≥12y: 93–95%
  • Avoid uncontrolled high-flow O&sub2; — titrate to target

Bronchodilator Doses

  • Mild: Albuterol 2–4 puffs pMDI+spacer q4h prn
  • Moderate: 4–10 puffs q20min ×3 in first hour
  • Severe: 10 puffs q20min or continuous neb
  • Add ipratropium for moderate–severe: 250 mcg neb q20min ×3

Systemic Corticosteroids

  • Start early for moderate–severe — do not wait for BD response
  • Prednisolone oral: 1–2 mg/kg/day (max 40 mg/day children; 50 mg adults)
  • Duration: 3–5 days; no taper needed for short courses
  • Dexamethasone 0.6 mg/kg (max 16 mg) ×1–2 doses: acceptable alternative
  • IV methylprednisolone 1 mg/kg q6h for severe / cannot take orals
  • IV magnesium sulfate 25–75 mg/kg (max 2.5g) over 20 min: for severe with inadequate BD response
Discharge & Follow-Up
Discharge Checklist

GINA 2025: Six elements are mandatory before every asthma discharge. Missing any two = significantly increased risk of recurrence and return ED visit within 2 weeks.

The 6-Point Discharge Checklist

  • 1. Controller prescribed or escalated. Patient must leave with an ICS-containing controller. If not previously on one, start now. If on one, assess whether to step up. Document the change.
  • 2. Oral corticosteroid course in hand. Prednisolone 1–2 mg/kg/day (max 40 mg) for 3–5 days total. Confirm prescription filled or supplied. Teach: complete the full course even if feeling better.
  • 3. Reliever prescription confirmed. SABA available for acute use. Counsel on overuse threshold (>2×/week = inadequate control).
  • 4. Inhaler technique checked. Watch patient demonstrate device. Correct at least the most critical error. Document technique reviewed in chart.
  • 5. Written asthma action plan provided. Review green/yellow/red zones explicitly. Confirm family can identify when to call 911. Do not assume prior plan is remembered or accessible.
  • 6. Follow-up within 1–2 working days. Not 2 weeks. Not next available appointment. The post-exacerbation period is the highest-risk time for recurrence. Confirm appointment time before patient leaves.

Disposition Criteria After 1 Hour of Treatment

ResponseDisposition

High-Risk Features Requiring Admission or Extended Observation

  • Prior intubation or ICU admission for asthma
  • Near-fatal asthma event in past
  • No transportation for follow-up or no phone to call for help
  • Medication cost barriers not resolved before discharge
  • Caregiver unable to repeat back key action plan instructions
Referral Guidance
When to Refer Pocket Card

Refer to Pediatric Pulmonology

  • Asthma uncontrolled at Step 3+ despite confirmed adherence and correct technique
  • Diagnosis uncertain or alternative condition suspected (CF, PCD, tracheomalacia, ILO/VCD)
  • ≥2 systemic corticosteroid courses in past year, or OCS dependency
  • Prior intubation or ICU admission for asthma
  • Significant exercise limitation despite treatment
  • Spirometry pattern inconsistent with typical asthma (fixed obstruction, restrictive pattern)
  • Any preschool child with red flag features (see Preschool Framework card)

Refer to Allergy / Immunology

  • Suspected allergic triggers with confirmed sensitization + ongoing exposure
  • Consideration of allergen immunotherapy (SCIT/SLIT)
  • Concurrent severe eczema, food allergy, or CRSwNP requiring coordinated T2 management
  • Evaluation for biologic therapy (Step 5)
  • Suspected NSAID-exacerbated respiratory disease in adolescents
  • Unexplained elevated IgE or eosinophilia

Before Referring as “Severe Asthma” — Check These First

  • Inhaler technique: watch the patient use every device
  • Adherence: pharmacy dispensing records; non-judgmental direct question
  • Ongoing triggers: allergens, smoke, NSAIDs, occupational exposures
  • Comorbidities: rhinitis, obesity, GERD, anxiety/ILO
  • Diagnosis confirmed: is there objective evidence of variable airflow limitation?

GINA 2025 reminder: Most patients referred as “severe asthma” have difficult-to-treat asthma — uncontrolled because of modifiable factors, not because of true refractory disease. Address modifiable factors systematically before escalating to biologics or subspecialty referral for “severe” label.

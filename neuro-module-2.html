Learning Objectives — Module 2
By the end of this module, the learner will be able to:
  • Recognize signs and symptoms of arterial ischemic stroke in children
  • Activate a stroke alert protocol and initiate the appropriate workup
  • Perform and score the Pediatric NIH Stroke Scale (PedNIHSS)
  • Manage blood pressure in the setting of acute pediatric stroke
  • Understand tPA eligibility criteria, contraindications, and dosing in the pediatric population

Why Pediatric Stroke Matters

Pediatric stroke is rare compared to adult stroke, but the consequences are devastating. Delays in recognition are common because stroke is not on the differential for most clinicians evaluating a child. The median time from symptom onset to diagnosis in children can exceed 24 hours — a window during which interventions are most effective.

Unlike adult stroke, pediatric stroke has a broad differential of underlying etiologies — cardiac disease, sickle cell disease, arteriopathies, thrombophilias, and autoimmune conditions. Recognition and rapid activation of a structured protocol is the single most important step a general pediatrician or emergency physician can take.

Key Principle
Time is brain. Every minute of delayed treatment in acute ischemic stroke results in further neuronal loss. The goal of this module is to equip you to recognize stroke presentations in children, activate the correct pathway, and initiate stabilization before the neurology team arrives.

Stroke Recognition

The key to recognizing pediatric stroke is maintaining a high index of suspicion. Unlike adults, children may present atypically, and the diagnosis is frequently delayed because stroke mimics (seizure, migraine, conversion disorder) are more common in the pediatric population.

Focal Motor Deficits

  • Monoparesis — weakness of a single limb
  • Hemiparesis — weakness of one side of the body (face, arm, and/or leg)

Focal Neurologic Deficits

  • Vision changes — visual field cut, diplopia
  • Dysarthria — slurred or difficult speech
  • Aphasia — difficulty producing or comprehending language
  • Numbness — unilateral sensory loss

Temporal Pattern

Symptoms may be active (ongoing at presentation) or resolved (transient). Document the exact time of onset or last seen well — this is critical for treatment eligibility decisions.

High-Risk Patients

Certain populations carry substantially elevated stroke risk and should prompt immediate evaluation even with subtle findings:

  • Cardiac disease — congenital heart disease, post-cardiac surgery, cardiomyopathy
  • Thrombophilia — known or suspected prothrombotic conditions
  • Previous stroke or TIA — history of prior cerebrovascular events
  • Sickle cell disease — one of the most significant pediatric stroke risk factors

Other Symptoms to Consider

  • Focal seizure — new-onset focal seizure can be the presenting sign of stroke
  • Altered mental status — unexplained change in consciousness or behavior
  • Dizziness / ataxia — may indicate posterior circulation stroke
Clinical Pearl — Think Stroke First
In a child with acute-onset focal neurologic deficit, assume it is a stroke until proven otherwise. "Probable seizure" and "probable migraine" are diagnoses of exclusion that should only be made after stroke has been evaluated with appropriate imaging. The cost of a missed stroke far exceeds the cost of a negative workup.

tPA Contraindications
History: >4.5 hours from symptom onset, unknown time of onset, recent stroke or major surgery, prior intracranial hemorrhage, known AVM or aneurysm, GI/GU bleeding within 21 days, significant bleeding disorder
  • Patient Factors: Would decline blood transfusion, acute MI, arterial puncture at non-compressible site within 7 days
  • Etiology: Subacute bacterial endocarditis (SBE), sickle cell disease, meningitis, marrow/air/fat embolism, moyamoya
  • Exam: SBP >15% above 95th percentile for age, mild deficit (PedNIHSS <6), severe deficit suggesting large territory infarction
  • Imaging: Subarachnoid hemorrhage, >⅓ hypodensity or effacement in MCA territory, arterial dissection
  • Lab: Glucose <50 or >400 mg/dL, platelets <100,000, INR >1.4, elevated PTT
tPA Dosing — Pediatric
Total dose: 0.9 mg/kg IV
Maximum dose: 90 mg (regardless of weight)
Bolus: 10% of total dose over 5 minutes
Infusion: Remaining 90% IV over 1 hour

Safety check: Nurse and physician must double-check dose calculation with pharmacy before administration.

Pediatric NIH Stroke Scale

The PedNIHSS is a validated modification of the adult NIHSS designed for children ages 2 and older. It quantifies neurological deficit severity and is used for tPA eligibility decisions, serial monitoring, and communication between care teams. The scale ranges from 0 (no deficit) to 42 (maximum deficit).

Scoring Interpretation
PedNIHSS < 6: Mild deficit — tPA generally not recommended (risk may outweigh benefit)
PedNIHSS 6–24: Moderate to severe — candidate for thrombolytic therapy if criteria met
PedNIHSS > 24: Very severe — raises concern for large territory infarction; weigh tPA risks carefully

Clinical Tip
Performing PedNIHSS in Young or Uncooperative Children
The PedNIHSS requires adaptation for developmental level. For children who cannot follow verbal commands reliably, use observation-based assessments: watch for spontaneous limb movement, facial symmetry during crying, visual tracking of a toy or parent's face, and withdrawal from noxious stimuli. Document the child's developmental level and any modifications used. Serial exams are more valuable than any single assessment — trends reveal what snapshots may miss.

Imaging in Acute Pediatric Stroke

Neuroimaging is essential to confirm the diagnosis, differentiate ischemic from hemorrhagic stroke, identify the vascular territory, and guide treatment decisions. The choice of initial modality depends on availability and clinical urgency.

Preferred — When Available
MRI Stroke Protocol
DWI (Diffusion-Weighted Imaging) — most sensitive for acute ischemia; detects changes within minutes of onset

FLAIR — identifies subacute changes and edema

MRA (MR Angiography) — evaluates intracranial vasculature

SWI (Susceptibility-Weighted Imaging) — detects hemorrhage and microbleeds

If MRI confirms ischemic stroke → obtain CTA head and neck to evaluate cervicocephalic vessels
Alternative — When MRI Unavailable
CT + CTA
CT Head without contrast — rapid, widely available; rules out hemorrhage but may miss early ischemia

CTA Head and Neck — evaluates for large vessel occlusion, dissection, or other vascular pathology

Obtain MRI when available — CT alone is insufficient for definitive characterization of ischemic stroke in children
Clinical Pearl — MRI Over CT When Possible
MRI with DWI is far more sensitive than CT for detecting acute ischemic stroke, especially in the first 6–12 hours. However, do not delay treatment to obtain MRI if it is not immediately available. A normal CT does not rule out ischemic stroke. If CT is negative but clinical suspicion remains high, continue with the stroke protocol and obtain MRI as soon as feasible.

Acute Management

Once a stroke alert is activated, the following interventions should be initiated immediately and in parallel with imaging. The goal is neuroprotection — minimizing secondary injury while definitive treatment decisions are being made.

Immediate Checklist

  • ABCs — secure airway, ensure adequate breathing and circulation
  • Frequent vital signs & neuro checks — every 15 minutes initially
  • Initial labs: glucose, CBC, PT/PTT, INR, fibrinogen, Chem 10, type & screen, hemoglobin profile if sickle cell disease suspected
  • Blood pressure management per stroke protocol (see BP Management section)
  • 15-lead EKG — evaluate for cardiac source of embolism
  • Continuous cardiac monitoring & pulse oximetry
  • Maintain O₂ saturation ≥ 95% — supplement as needed
  • Place peripheral IV
  • Glucose management: keep glucose >80 and <140 mg/dL
  • IV fluids: <1 year: D5NS; ≥1 year: 0.9% NS — consider renal status when selecting fluids
  • Temperature management: acetaminophen if temperature >100°F
  • NPO status — nothing by mouth until swallowing assessment
  • Strict bedrest, HOB flat, neck midline — optimize cerebral perfusion
  • Seizure management: if seizures occur, administer a nonsedating anticonvulsant IV (avoid agents that may confound the neurological exam)

Aspirin Therapy

Once ischemic stroke is confirmed on imaging and hemorrhage has been excluded:

Clinical Scenario Aspirin Recommendation
Not currently on antiplatelet therapy Aspirin 3–5 mg/kg orally or rectally once ischemic stroke is confirmed
Maximum: 325 mg/dose
Already on aspirin Evaluate current dose and increase to 3–5 mg/kg if below therapeutic range
Aspirin not tolerated or contraindicated Consider alternatives: dipyridamole, clopidogrel, or anticoagulation based on clinical context
Important — Timing of Aspirin After tPA
If the patient receives IV tPA, do not administer aspirin for 24 hours following thrombolytic therapy. Obtain a follow-up CT head at 24 hours to rule out hemorrhagic transformation before initiating antiplatelet therapy.

Blood Pressure Management

Blood pressure management in acute pediatric stroke is nuanced. Permissive hypertension is generally preferred in the acute phase to maintain cerebral perfusion to the ischemic penumbra. However, excessively elevated blood pressure increases the risk of hemorrhagic transformation, especially if thrombolytics are being considered.

BP Target Rules
Target range: Maintain BP between the 50th percentile and 15% above the 95th percentile for age and sex.

Treat to lower if:
• BP >15% above the 95th percentile sustained for >1 hour
• BP >20% above the 95th percentile at any time (treat immediately)

Clinical Pearl — Why Permissive Hypertension?
In acute ischemic stroke, the penumbra — brain tissue surrounding the infarct core — depends on collateral blood flow for survival. Aggressive blood pressure lowering can reduce perfusion to this vulnerable zone and extend the infarct. The goal is to keep BP high enough to perfuse the penumbra but low enough to avoid hemorrhagic complications. Use the tables above to identify your patient's age- and sex-appropriate targets.

Anticoagulation in Pediatric Stroke

The decision to anticoagulate in acute pediatric ischemic stroke is complex and depends on the underlying etiology, infarct size, and hemorrhagic risk. Anticoagulation is not routine for all pediatric stroke patients — it is reserved for specific high-risk clinical scenarios.

Key Principle
Early anticoagulation within the first 48 hours carries an increased risk of hemorrhagic transformation. The decision must be weighed against the risk of recurrent thromboembolism.

Indications for Anticoagulation

Anticoagulation is generally recommended when the following comorbid conditions or very high-risk lesions are present:

  • Intracranial arterial dissection
  • Extracranial arterial dissection
  • Intracardiac thrombus
  • Catastrophic antiphospholipid syndrome (CAPS)
  • Other comorbid conditions associated with very high recurrence risk

Agent Selection

Timing Agent Rationale
First 48 hours Unfractionated heparin (UFH) Short half-life, easily reversible with protamine. Preferred during the highest-risk window for hemorrhagic transformation. Allows dose titration to PTT targets.
After 48 hours Transition to LMWH or warfarin Once hemorrhagic risk stabilizes, transition to longer-acting anticoagulation as indicated by the underlying etiology.

Monitoring Requirements

  • Close neurological monitoring — any decline should prompt immediate reassessment and imaging
  • Consider repeat CT or MRI at 24–48 hours to assess for hemorrhagic transformation before continuing or escalating anticoagulation
  • Monitor PTT every 6 hours while on unfractionated heparin; adjust infusion rate per nomogram
  • If anti-Xa monitoring is available, use for LMWH dose verification
Clinical Pearl — Aspirin vs. Anticoagulation
Most pediatric ischemic strokes are managed with aspirin alone as the initial antithrombotic. Anticoagulation is reserved for specific high-risk etiologies (dissection, cardiac thrombus, CAPS). When in doubt, start aspirin and consult neurology for guidance on whether anticoagulation is indicated. The two are generally not used simultaneously in the acute phase.

Case Vignettes

Case 1 — Acute Presentation Within tPA Window
The 7-Year-Old with Sudden Hemiparesis
A 7-year-old previously healthy boy is brought to the emergency department by his parents. Two hours ago, while playing soccer, he suddenly developed left-sided weakness and began slurring his words. On examination, he has left hemiparesis (face, arm, and leg), dysarthria, and a leftward gaze preference. His BP is 128/84 mmHg. Heart rate 92, SpO₂ 98% on room air. He is alert and following commands but appears frightened. What are your next steps?

Step 1 — Activate stroke alert: This child has acute-onset focal neurological deficit (left hemiparesis + dysarthria) that began 2 hours ago. This is a stroke until proven otherwise. Immediately activate the STAT stroke alert and consult the neurology/stroke team.

Step 2 — ABCs and stabilization: Secure IV access, continuous cardiac monitoring, pulse oximetry. Obtain stat labs (glucose, CBC, PT/PTT/INR, fibrinogen, Chem 10, type & screen). Obtain 15-lead EKG. Keep NPO, HOB flat, neck midline.

Step 3 — Perform PedNIHSS: Score the examination systematically. With left hemiparesis involving face/arm/leg, dysarthria, and gaze preference, expect a score in the moderate-to-severe range (likely >6), supporting tPA candidacy.

Step 4 — Imaging: If MRI is immediately available, obtain MRI stroke protocol (DWI, FLAIR, MRA, SWI). If not, obtain CT head without contrast + CTA head and neck. Do not delay imaging.

Step 5 — tPA eligibility: This patient is within the 4.5-hour window, is older than 2, and has no immediately apparent contraindications. BP of 128/84 should be evaluated against 95th percentile for a 7-year-old male (119 systolic) — BP is elevated. Check if >15% above 95th percentile (137). At 128, he is below this threshold, so BP does not contraindicate tPA. If imaging confirms ischemic stroke and PedNIHSS ≥ 6, IV tPA should be considered. Simultaneously consult Neuro IR for potential endovascular intervention.

Case 2 — Outside tPA Window with High-Risk Etiology
The 12-Year-Old with Sickle Cell Disease
A 12-year-old girl with known sickle cell disease (HbSS) presents to the emergency department with right arm weakness and difficulty speaking. Her mother reports she was last seen well 6 hours ago when she went to her room after dinner. The mother found her 30 minutes ago with right arm weakness and "talking funny." On exam, she has right upper extremity weakness (3/5 strength), expressive aphasia, and is otherwise neurologically intact. BP 118/72 mmHg. Is this patient eligible for tPA?

tPA eligibility — No. This patient is not a candidate for IV tPA for two reasons:

1. Beyond the 4.5-hour window: Last seen well 6 hours ago. The time of symptom onset is unknown but is at minimum 30 minutes ago and could be up to 6 hours ago. The relevant time is "last seen well," which is 6 hours — this exceeds the 4.5-hour tPA eligibility window.

2. Sickle cell disease etiology: Sickle cell disease is listed as a relative contraindication to tPA in pediatric stroke protocols. The pathophysiology of stroke in SCD is often related to vasculopathy (moyamoya-like disease) rather than thromboembolic occlusion, which affects the expected benefit of thrombolysis.

Next steps: Activate stroke alert, obtain urgent imaging (MRI preferred or CT + CTA), perform PedNIHSS, and consult Neuro Interventional Radiology for potential endovascular intervention, which may be available beyond the tPA window. Also obtain hemoglobin profile and coordinate with hematology — exchange transfusion to reduce HbS to <30% is a critical intervention in acute stroke related to sickle cell disease. Start supportive management per the acute management checklist.

Self-Assessment

Three questions to test your understanding of pediatric stroke management. Click an answer to select it, then reveal the explanation.

Question 1 of 3
What is the maximum tPA dose regardless of patient weight?
  • A. 50 mg
  • B. 90 mg
  • C. 100 mg
  • D. There is no maximum — dose is strictly weight-based
Correct Answer: B — 90 mg The tPA dose for pediatric stroke is 0.9 mg/kg IV, with an absolute maximum of 90 mg regardless of patient weight. Ten percent of the total dose is administered as a bolus over 5 minutes, with the remaining 90% infused over 1 hour. This dosing mirrors the adult protocol established in landmark stroke trials.
Question 2 of 3
At what PedNIHSS score should you reconsider tPA administration due to a mild deficit?
  • A. < 2
  • B. < 4
  • C. < 6
  • D. < 10
Correct Answer: C — PedNIHSS < 6 A PedNIHSS score below 6 indicates a mild neurological deficit. In this range, the bleeding risk of tPA may outweigh the potential benefit, and thrombolytic therapy is generally not recommended. However, clinical judgment should be applied — a PedNIHSS of 5 with a large vessel occlusion on imaging may still warrant intervention. The score is one input into the decision, not the sole determinant.
Question 3 of 3
What percentage of the total tPA dose is given as the initial bolus?
  • A. 5%
  • B. 10%
  • C. 25%
  • D. 50%
Correct Answer: B — 10% The tPA bolus is 10% of the calculated total dose (0.9 mg/kg, max 90 mg), administered IV over 5 minutes. The remaining 90% is then infused continuously over 1 hour. This bolus-plus-infusion approach ensures rapid achievement of therapeutic drug levels while maintaining sustained thrombolytic activity. Both the nurse and physician must verify the dose calculation with pharmacy prior to administration.

Overview — Pediatric Headache

Headache is one of the most common neurological complaints in pediatrics. By age 15, approximately 75% of children will have experienced a significant headache. Migraine alone affects 8–12% of the pediatric population and is a leading cause of school absence and impaired quality of life in children and adolescents.

The evaluation and management of headache in children requires a systematic approach. The clinician must first determine whether the headache is primary (migraine, tension-type, cluster) or secondary (caused by an underlying pathology). This distinction drives every subsequent decision — from imaging to treatment to disposition.

Resident Pearl — History Is Your Best Diagnostic Tool
In pediatric headache, the history is more valuable than any imaging study. Temporal pattern (acute, acute-recurrent, chronic progressive, chronic non-progressive), associated features (aura, nausea, photophobia), triggers, family history of migraine, and impact on function will correctly classify >90% of headaches. Imaging should be reserved for red flag scenarios, not used as a substitute for a thorough history.

Headache Classification

The International Classification of Headache Disorders, 3rd edition (ICHD-3) provides the standard classification framework for headache disorders. Primary headaches account for the vast majority of pediatric headache presentations.

Primary Headache Disorders

Most Common
Migraine
Without aura (most common in children): ≥5 attacks lasting 2–72 hours (shorter duration accepted in pediatrics vs. adults), with at least 2 of: unilateral location (often bilateral in children), pulsating quality, moderate-severe intensity, aggravation by routine physical activity. Plus at least 1 of: nausea/vomiting, photophobia and phonophobia.

With aura: ≥2 attacks with fully reversible visual, sensory, or speech symptoms. At least 3 of: ≥1 aura symptom spreads gradually ≥5 min, 2+ aura symptoms in succession, each symptom lasts 5–60 min, at least 1 aura is unilateral, headache accompanies or follows aura within 60 min.
Also Common
Tension-Type Headache
Episodic: ≥10 episodes occurring <15 days/month, lasting 30 min to 7 days, with at least 2 of: bilateral, pressing/tightening (non-pulsating), mild-moderate intensity, not aggravated by physical activity. No nausea or vomiting; no more than one of photophobia or phonophobia.

Chronic: ≥15 days/month for >3 months. May overlap with chronic migraine — differentiation requires careful diary tracking of features.

Temporal Headache Patterns

Pattern Description Common Causes
Acute Single episode, no prior history Viral illness, sinusitis, meningitis, subarachnoid hemorrhage, first migraine
Acute Recurrent Episodic attacks with symptom-free intervals Migraine (most common), cluster headache, trigeminal autonomic cephalalgias
Chronic Progressive Increasing frequency and/or severity over time Intracranial mass, hydrocephalus, IIH, subdural hematoma — requires urgent evaluation
Chronic Non-Progressive Daily or near-daily headache, stable severity Chronic migraine, chronic tension-type headache, medication overuse headache, new daily persistent headache
Key Pediatric Differences from Adults
In children, migraine is often bilateral (not classically unilateral), of shorter duration (as brief as 2 hours vs. 4 hours in adults per ICHD-3), and may present predominantly with abdominal symptoms (nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain) rather than head pain. Young children may not verbalize photophobia or phonophobia — instead, look for behavioral cues such as seeking dark rooms, covering eyes, or refusing to eat.

Red Flags for Secondary Headache

The primary goal of the initial headache evaluation is to identify features suggesting a secondary cause that may require urgent imaging, lumbar puncture, or subspecialty consultation. The following features should raise concern:

⚠ Red Flags — Evaluate Urgently
  • Fever with headache — consider meningitis, encephalitis, abscess
  • Papilledema on fundoscopic exam — suggests elevated intracranial pressure
  • Positional quality — worse lying down, improved upright (or vice versa)
  • Morning headaches or headaches that wake the child from sleep
  • Abnormal neurological exam — focal deficits, gait abnormality, ataxia, cranial nerve palsies
  • Posterior fossa symptoms — vomiting without nausea, truncal ataxia, nystagmus
  • Sudden "worst headache of life" — thunderclap headache (consider SAH, RCVS)
  • Progressive worsening over weeks with no symptom-free intervals
  • Age <5 years — primary headache disorders are less common; secondary causes more likely
  • Change in headache pattern in a patient with known migraine — new features warrant reassessment
  • Signs of neurocutaneous disorder — café-au-lait spots, ash leaf macules (increased tumor risk)
  • Recent head trauma or VP shunt history
Resident Pearl — When to Image
A child with a classic migraine pattern (episodic, throbbing, with nausea and photophobia, normal neurological exam, family history of migraine) does not need neuroimaging. Reflexive MRI ordering for every pediatric headache adds cost, requires sedation in young children, and incidentally identifies benign findings that generate unnecessary anxiety. Image when there are red flags — not to reassure yourself that you haven't missed something.

Indications for Neuroimaging

  • Any red flag feature listed above
  • Abnormal neurological examination (focal deficits, papilledema, cranial neuropathy)
  • Chronic progressive headache pattern
  • New-onset severe headache (thunderclap presentation)
  • Headache with signs of elevated ICP (morning vomiting, 6th nerve palsy, papilledema)
  • Change in established headache pattern that is unexplained

MRI with and without contrast is preferred over CT for most non-emergent indications. CT is appropriate for acute emergency situations (suspected hemorrhage, trauma). Add MRV if concern for cerebral venous sinus thrombosis.

* Definition of Significant Pain Reduction
Episodic migraine: At least 50% pain reduction from baseline.
Chronic migraine with acute exacerbation: Return to the patient's baseline chronic headache level.

At each "Yes" decision point → Discharge with consideration for abortive therapy prescription, headache diary, and neurology follow-up as appropriate.

Notes on Substitutions

  • Prochlorperazine alternatives: Metoclopramide PO/IV, or ondansetron 4 mg IV/ODT (less effective as anti-migraine agent but useful if prochlorperazine contraindicated)
  • If extrapyramidal side effects occur: Diphenhydramine 1–2 mg/kg IV (MAX 50 mg)
  • Valproate contraindications: Pregnancy, known hepatic disease, urea cycle disorder, mitochondrial disease (POLG mutations)
Clinical Tip
Premedicate Before Prochlorperazine
Extrapyramidal symptoms (acute dystonia, akathisia) from dopamine antagonists like prochlorperazine are distressing to children and families and can mimic other neurological emergencies. Routine premedication with diphenhydramine 1 mg/kg IV (MAX 50 mg) given 15–20 minutes before prochlorperazine significantly reduces this risk and is recommended practice.

Abortive Therapy

Abortive (acute) therapy aims to terminate a migraine attack once it begins. Treatment is most effective when administered early — ideally within 60 minutes of headache onset. Patient education about early treatment is essential for outpatient migraine management.

Principles of Abortive Therapy

  • Treat early: Migraine-specific medications are most effective when used at the onset of symptoms, before central sensitization develops
  • Use an adequate dose: Underdosing is a common cause of treatment failure
  • Avoid medication overuse: Using abortive medications >2–3 days per week can lead to medication overuse headache (MOH)
  • Ensure the patient has rescue medication at home and school: Delays in treatment reduce efficacy
Medication Dose Route Notes
Ibuprofen 10 mg/kg (MAX 600 mg) PO First-line for mild-moderate migraine. Best evidence in pediatrics. Administer with fluids.
Acetaminophen 15 mg/kg (MAX 1000 mg) PO Alternative to ibuprofen. Less effective but may be preferred if NSAID contraindicated.
Sumatriptan nasal spray 5–20 mg (age ≥12) Intranasal FDA-approved for adolescents. Nasal route avoids oral route issues with nausea/vomiting.
Rizatriptan 5 mg (20–39 kg), 10 mg (≥40 kg) PO/ODT FDA-approved ≥6 years. ODT dissolves on tongue — useful when vomiting. Adjust if on propranolol (use 5 mg).
Almotriptan 6.25–12.5 mg PO FDA-approved ≥12 years. Favorable side effect profile among triptans.
Zolmitriptan nasal spray 2.5–5 mg Intranasal FDA-approved ≥12 years. Good option when oral route unavailable.

Resident Pearl — Triptans in Children
Many practitioners remain hesitant to prescribe triptans in children, but evidence supports their safety and efficacy in pediatric patients. Rizatriptan is FDA-approved for ages ≥6, and sumatriptan nasal spray, almotriptan, and zolmitriptan nasal spray are FDA-approved for ages ≥12. Triptans should not be withheld from children with moderate-severe migraine who do not respond to simple analgesics. Remember: triptans are contraindicated in hemiplegic migraine and migraine with brainstem aura.

Prophylactic Therapy

Prophylactic (preventive) therapy should be considered when migraines occur with significant frequency or disability. Common indications include ≥4 migraines per month, migraines significantly impacting school attendance or quality of life, failure or overuse of abortive therapy, or certain migraine subtypes (hemiplegic migraine, migraine with brainstem aura).

General Principles

  • The goal is to reduce headache frequency by ≥50% — complete elimination is rarely achievable and should not be the expectation
  • Allow 8–12 weeks at adequate dose before judging efficacy
  • Start low, titrate slowly to minimize side effects and improve adherence
  • Once effective, continue for 6–12 months, then consider a slow taper
  • Non-pharmacological measures (sleep hygiene, hydration, regular meals, exercise, stress management) are first-line and should accompany all pharmacotherapy
  • Maintain a headache diary to track response objectively

The CHAMP Trial — Context for Practice
The Childhood and Adolescent Migraine Prevention (CHAMP) trial (2017) found that neither amitriptyline nor topiramate was superior to placebo for migraine prevention in children ages 8–17, and both had higher rates of adverse effects than placebo. This landmark trial has been widely discussed but should be interpreted carefully: it does not mean prophylactic medication never works — it means that the placebo response in pediatric migraine is substantial (~60%), lifestyle interventions matter profoundly, and medication should be selected thoughtfully after non-pharmacological strategies are in place.

Idiopathic Intracranial Hypertension

Idiopathic intracranial hypertension (IIH), formerly known as pseudotumor cerebri, is characterized by elevated intracranial pressure without an identifiable intracranial mass, infection, or other structural cause. In children, IIH accounts for 53–77% of cases of raised ICP without a secondary structural cause and requires prompt recognition to prevent permanent vision loss.

Clinical Characteristics

  • Headache — no specific pattern; may be constant or intermittent, mild or severe
  • Nausea and emesis
  • Pulsatile tinnitus — a distinctive symptom that should raise suspicion
  • Diplopia — typically from 6th cranial nerve palsy (false localizing sign of elevated ICP)
  • Blurred vision and transient visual obscurations (brief episodes of visual dimming)
  • Neck, shoulder, and arm pain
  • Papilledema — present in majority but may be absent (especially if chronic or in very young children)
  • Normal mental status and no focal neurologic findings (CN palsies acceptable if no other etiology identified and deficits improve with ICP reduction)

Diagnostic Evaluation

Study Purpose Key Details
Ophthalmology exam Document papilledema grade, visual acuity, visual fields Fundoscopic exam and formal visual field testing are essential at diagnosis and for monitoring
Lumbar puncture with opening pressure Confirm elevated ICP ≥25 cm H₂O abnormal in non-sedated pediatric patients; ≥28 cm H₂O if sedated or obese. CSF composition should be normal.
MRI brain with & without contrast Rule out hydrocephalus, mass lesion May show empty sella, optic nerve sheath distension, flattening of posterior sclera, tortuosity of optic nerve
MRV without contrast Evaluate for cerebral sinus venous thrombosis Sinus venous thrombosis is a key secondary cause that mimics IIH and requires anticoagulation

Treatment of IIH

Category Intervention Details
First-line medical Acetazolamide Start 25 mg/kg/day divided BID-TID; titrate up to 100 mg/kg/day (MAX 2–4 g/day). Monitor BMP for metabolic acidosis. Supplemental bicarbonate may be needed.
Alternative / adjunct Topiramate Has both carbonic anhydrase inhibition and weight-loss properties. Useful when weight management is a treatment goal. Dose as per migraine prophylaxis.

Resident Pearl — IIH Is a Visual Emergency
The headache in IIH is distressing, but the real danger is vision loss. Papilledema left untreated can lead to progressive optic atrophy and permanent blindness. Every patient diagnosed with IIH needs ophthalmology involvement from the start — with formal visual field testing at diagnosis and at regular intervals thereafter. Do not wait for visual complaints to involve ophthalmology; visual field loss can be asymptomatic until advanced.

Case Vignettes

Case 1 — Idiopathic Intracranial Hypertension
The Adolescent with Daily Headaches and Blurred Vision
A 15-year-old female with a BMI of 34 presents with daily headaches for the past 3 months. The headaches are diffuse, worse in the morning, and occasionally associated with brief episodes of vision "going dark" for a few seconds. She also reports a "whooshing" sound in her right ear. On examination, she is alert and oriented with normal mental status. Visual acuity is 20/30 bilaterally. Fundoscopic exam reveals bilateral papilledema. Neurological exam is otherwise unremarkable. MRI brain is normal. MRV shows no evidence of sinus thrombosis. LP is performed with an opening pressure of 32 cm H₂O and normal CSF composition.

Questions: (1) What is the most likely diagnosis? (2) What is the significance of the opening pressure? (3) What are the transient visual episodes called? (4) What is your initial management plan?

Diagnosis: Idiopathic intracranial hypertension (IIH). This patient meets modified Dandy criteria: symptoms and signs of elevated ICP (headache, papilledema, transient visual obscurations, pulsatile tinnitus), elevated opening pressure on LP (≥25 cm H₂O in a non-sedated pediatric patient), normal CSF composition, and normal neuroimaging without a secondary structural cause.

Opening pressure: 32 cm H₂O is significantly elevated (normal <25 cm H₂O non-sedated; <28 cm H₂O if sedated or obese). This confirms the diagnosis of intracranial hypertension.

Transient visual obscurations: The brief episodes of vision "going dark" are transient visual obscurations — a hallmark symptom of papilledema caused by transient ischemia of the optic nerve head from elevated ICP. They are a warning sign of threatened vision.

Initial management: (1) Start acetazolamide 25 mg/kg/day divided BID, titrate as tolerated. (2) Urgent ophthalmology referral for formal visual field testing and serial monitoring. (3) Counseling on weight management — even 5–10% weight loss can significantly improve IIH in obese patients. (4) Medication review to exclude offending agents. (5) Labs: BMP (baseline before acetazolamide), CBC, iron panel, vitamin A and D levels, TSH, free T4. (6) Close follow-up with neurology and ophthalmology.

Case 2 — Acute Migraine in the Emergency Center
The Child with Refractory Headache
A 10-year-old male presents to the emergency center with a severe, throbbing headache for 8 hours, worse on the right side. He has photophobia, nausea, and has vomited twice. His mother gave ibuprofen 6 hours ago with minimal relief. He has a history of similar episodic headaches every 2–3 weeks for the past year. His mother has migraines. Neurological examination is completely normal. Vital signs show mild tachycardia (HR 102) but are otherwise normal.

Questions: (1) Are there any red flags requiring imaging? (2) What is your stepwise treatment approach using the emergency center migraine treatment algorithm? (3) What do you do if he does not respond to the headache cocktail?

Red flag assessment: No red flags are present. The headache is episodic (acute-recurrent pattern), throbbing, unilateral, with nausea/vomiting and photophobia — classic migraine features. The neurological exam is normal, and there is a positive family history. The mild tachycardia is likely secondary to pain and dehydration. Neuroimaging is not indicated.

Stepwise treatment per the algorithm:

(1) Baseline assessment: Confirm last abortive was ibuprofen 6 hours ago, check for allergies, obtain urine pregnancy test if indicated.

(2) Headache cocktail: Start normal saline bolus 20 mL/kg (this child is likely dehydrated from vomiting). During the infusion, give ketorolac 0.5 mg/kg IV (MAX 30 mg) over 10 minutes. Then give prochlorperazine 0.15 mg/kg IV (MAX 10 mg) over 5 minutes. Consider premedication with diphenhydramine 1 mg/kg IV (MAX 50 mg) to prevent EPS.

(3) Dexamethasone: If no steroids in past 7 days, give dexamethasone 0.6 mg/kg IV (MAX 16 mg) to prevent rebound headache.

(4) Reassess at 60 minutes. If ≥50% pain reduction → discharge with outpatient follow-up, headache diary, and consideration for prophylaxis given frequency (every 2–3 weeks). Prescribe a triptan (e.g., rizatriptan 10 mg ODT if ≥40 kg) for future attacks.

(5) If no significant relief: Administer sodium valproate (Depacon) 20 mg/kg IV (MAX 1000 mg) over 30 minutes. Reassess after 60 minutes.

(6) If still no relief: Consider DHE protocol or magnesium sulfate 1000 mg IV over 30 minutes. If continued refractory → consider admission for ongoing IV therapy.

Self-Assessment

Three questions to test your understanding of headache evaluation and management in children.

Question 1 of 3
What opening pressure on lumbar puncture is considered abnormal in a non-sedated pediatric patient?
  • A. ≥15 cm H₂O
  • B. ≥20 cm H₂O
  • C. ≥25 cm H₂O
  • D. ≥30 cm H₂O
Correct Answer: C An opening pressure of ≥25 cm H₂O is considered abnormal in a non-sedated pediatric patient. The threshold is higher (≥28 cm H₂O) if the patient is sedated or obese, as both conditions can elevate baseline opening pressure. Normal CSF pressure in children is typically <20 cm H₂O, with 20–25 cm H₂O considered a borderline range. The opening pressure must be measured with the patient in the lateral decubitus position with legs extended for accurate readings.
Question 2 of 3
What is the maximum dose of ketorolac in the emergency center headache cocktail?
  • A. 15 mg
  • B. 20 mg
  • C. 30 mg
  • D. 60 mg
Correct Answer: C Ketorolac is dosed at 0.5 mg/kg IV with a maximum dose of 30 mg, administered over 10 minutes during the normal saline infusion. It is given as part of the initial "headache cocktail" alongside IV fluids and before prochlorperazine. The 30 mg maximum applies to the single emergency center dose. Ketorolac is a potent NSAID and should be avoided in patients with renal impairment, active bleeding, or a history of peptic ulcer disease.
Question 3 of 3
Name two medications that can cause secondary intracranial hypertension in children.
  • A. Ibuprofen and acetaminophen
  • B. Doxycycline and oral contraceptives
  • C. Amoxicillin and montelukast
  • D. Albuterol and fluticasone
Correct Answer: B Several medications are known to cause secondary intracranial hypertension. The most important to recognize include: doxycycline (and other tetracyclines), oral contraceptives, vitamin A toxicity (including isotretinoin), corticosteroid withdrawal, and growth hormone. When evaluating a child with suspected IIH, a thorough medication history is essential — identifying and discontinuing the offending agent may be the most important intervention. This is why the condition should only be considered "idiopathic" after secondary causes, including drug-induced, have been excluded.

📋 Mission Brief

A 7-year-old child has arrived in the West Texas pediatric ED with fever, cough, and a spreading rash. The team suspects measles. Your mission: identify the outbreak, protect the unit, and save the patient. Before you enter the escape room, you need to know everything in this module. Study it. Know it. Be ready.

Context

Why This Matters in West Texas

Measles was declared eliminated in the United States in 2000, but outbreaks continue — and West Texas is particularly vulnerable.

🚨
Team Alert
West Texas Outbreak Risk Is Real

West Texas has experienced significant measles outbreaks driven by pockets of vaccine hesitancy, large unvaccinated communities, and geographic barriers to healthcare access. In 2025, Texas reported its largest measles outbreak in decades, centered in the panhandle region. Your preparation today is a direct response to this reality.

🗩
Transmission
Airborne & Highly Contagious
Measles virus is one of the most contagious pathogens known. Viral particles remain suspended in air for up to 2 hours after the patient leaves the room. R&sub0;: 12–18 — one of the highest of any infectious disease.
💉
Vaccination Gap
Undervaccination Drives Outbreaks
Two doses of MMR vaccine are >97% effective. Communities with vaccination rates below 95% lose herd immunity. Rural West Texas counties have reported MMR coverage significantly below this threshold.
🏠
Clinical Impact
Serious in Children Under 5
Pneumonia occurs in 1 in 20 cases. Encephalitis in 1 in 1,000. Death in 1–2 per 1,000 in developed nations. Risk is highest in children under 5, immunocompromised patients, and malnourished children.

Core Knowledge

Measles Essentials

🧠
Pathogen
Measles Virus (Rubeola)
Single-stranded RNA paramyxovirus. Spreads via respiratory droplets and airborne particles. Highly stable in air. Enters via respiratory epithelium; replicates in regional lymph nodes before systemic viremia.
🤭
Contagion
Contagious Period
4 days before rash to 4 days after rash onset. The prodromal phase is the most dangerous — highly contagious and often undiagnosed. Virus survives in air up to 2 hours.
🔥
The 3 C’s + 1 K
Classic Prodrome
Cough • Coryza • Conjunctivitis
Koplik spots — pathognomonic; appear 1–2 days before the rash on buccal mucosa
🔆
Rash Pattern
Cephalocaudal Spread
Starts at hairline and face, spreads downward over 2–3 days: neck → trunk → extremities → feet. Maculopapular, erythematous. May become confluent. Lasts 5–6 days.
💉
Vaccination
MMR Vaccine
Dose 1 at 12–15 months, dose 2 at 4–6 years. Efficacy >97% with two doses. Live attenuated — contraindicated in pregnancy and severe immunocompromise.
💊
Treatment
Supportive Care + Vitamin A
No specific antiviral. Supportive care: fever management, hydration, nutrition. Vitamin A recommended by WHO and CDC for all children with measles — reduces morbidity and mortality.
Clinical Recognition

Recognizing Measles: What to Look For

Measles has a distinctive clinical progression. Study these findings carefully before your simulation.

🚫
Clinical rule: Do not wait for a confirmed measles PCR to initiate airborne isolation. If the clinical picture is consistent with measles, isolate immediately. Every minute of delay is additional exposure for patients, families, and staff.

Clinical Progression Timeline

🫨
Days 1–4 (Prodrome)
High Fever, 3 C’s — Already Contagious
Abrupt onset high fever (101–105°F). Cough, coryza, conjunctivitis appear. Koplik spots appear in this phase — small blue-white spots on buccal mucosa opposite the second molar. Most contagious period; least likely to be diagnosed.
🔴
Day 3–5 (Rash Onset)
Maculopapular Rash Begins at Hairline
Erythematous maculopapular rash erupts at the hairline and behind the ears, spreads to face. Fever may worsen. Koplik spots fade. Patient remains contagious. Rash is not pruritic (unlike varicella).
📈
Days 5–7 (Rash Spread)
Cephalocaudal Progression to Trunk and Extremities
Rash spreads: neck → trunk → arms → legs → feet over 2–3 days. May become confluent on face and trunk. Contagious through day 4 after rash onset. Watch for complications: respiratory worsening, neurological changes.
✅
Days 7–10 (Recovery)
Rash Fades; No Longer Contagious After Day 4 Post-Rash
Rash fades in the same order it appeared (face first), leaving temporary brownish discoloration. Fever resolves. Post-measles immune suppression can last weeks to months.

🔍 Koplik Spots — Pathognomonic Finding

⭐
Koplik spots are pathognomonic for measles. They appear 1–2 days before the rash on the buccal mucosa, opposite the second molar. They look like tiny grains of salt on a red base — “grains of sand on a red sea.” They fade as the rash appears. Always look inside the mouth.
👀
Koplik Spots — Buccal Mucosa
  • Small blue-white spots with red halo on buccal mucosa
  • Located opposite the second molar
  • Appear 1–2 days before the rash erupts
  • Fade as maculopapular rash appears
  • Pathognomonic: present only in measles
View Koplik spots on CDC.gov ↗
👀
How to Find Koplik Spots
  • Use a penlight or headlamp — good lighting is essential
  • Ask patient to open wide; use a tongue depressor
  • Look at buccal mucosa bilaterally, opposite the second molar
  • Spots are tiny (1–2 mm), white with a red base
  • May be subtle — look carefully before dismissing
  • Disappear rapidly after rash onset — check early

Critical Action

Infection Control & Immediate Actions

🚨
Measles is airborne. One patient can infect up to 18 others in an unprotected space. Viral particles remain suspended in air for up to 2 hours. Isolation is not optional — it is the first intervention. Before anything else: isolate, mask, notify.

Immediate Action Sequence

1
🏠 ISOLATE — Airborne Precautions Immediately
Move to a negative-pressure AIIR on clinical suspicion. Do not wait for lab confirmation. Keep door closed. If AIIR unavailable, private room with door closed — notify infection control immediately.
2
🦯 PPE — N95 or Higher Before Entering
All personnel must wear N95 respirator (or PAPR), eye protection, gown, and gloves. Don PPE before entering. Perform the N95 seal check every time. Surgical masks do NOT protect against airborne measles.
3
📞 NOTIFY — Infection Control and Team
Notify infection control immediately. Notify the charge nurse and attending. Inform the entire team that airborne precautions are in effect. Begin compiling the list of all exposed staff, patients, and visitors.
4
📋 DOCUMENT — Exposure Timeline
Record arrival time, waiting room location, all patient-staff contacts, and procedures performed. Ask: who was in the waiting room? Who examined the patient before isolation? What areas did the patient pass through?
5
🌎 REPORT — Public Health Notification
Measles is a nationally notifiable disease. Report to local and state health department immediately — do not wait for confirmation. In Texas, report to DSHS and your local health authority.

Clinical Management

Management Pearls

💊
Vitamin A in measles: WHO and CDC recommend vitamin A for all children with measles in the US. Give to children 6 months–2 years hospitalized with measles, and any child with immunodeficiency, malabsorption, or clinical vitamin A deficiency.
Vitamin A Dosing
<6 months: 50,000 IU ×2 days
6–11 months: 100,000 IU ×2 days
≥12 months: 200,000 IU ×2 days
Third dose if signs of vitamin A deficiency
Supportive Care Priorities
Antipyretics for fever (acetaminophen or ibuprofen). Adequate hydration — oral if tolerated, IV if not. Monitor for superimposed bacterial infections. Nutritional support. No aspirin in children.
IVIG Consideration
Immune globulin within 6 days of exposure can prevent or modify measles in susceptible contacts: unvaccinated infants <12 months, pregnant women, severely immunocompromised persons. Consult ID or public health.

⚠️ Recognize Complications Early

🫀
Measles Pneumonia High Risk
Most common cause of measles death. ~1 in 20 cases. Watch for: worsening cough, increased work of breathing, hypoxia. Escalate early.
🧠
Acute Encephalitis 1 in 1,000
Altered mental status, seizures, or focal neurological signs 2–6 days after rash onset. Urgent neurology and ICU involvement required.
🔊
Otitis Media 1 in 10
Most common complication. Secondary bacterial superinfection. Watch for ear pain, irritability, fever returning after initial improvement.
💡
SSPE (Late Complication)
Rare but fatal progressive neurological disease occurring 7–10 years later. No treatment. Risk highest when measles occurs in infancy.
🚨
Escalate immediately if: SpO&sub2; <94% on room air • Increased respiratory rate for age • Retractions or accessory muscle use • Altered mental status • Seizure • Unable to maintain hydration.
Beyond the Bedside

The Public Health Mindset

When measles walks through your door, you are not just treating one patient. You are the first line of defense against a community outbreak.

🔍
Step 1
Identify
Clinical suspicion based on prodrome + rash + exposure history
🚫
Step 2
Isolate
Airborne isolation immediately; N95 PPE for all entering
📞
Step 3
Report
Notify infection control + local/state public health now
👥
Step 4
Trace
Identify all contacts; determine immune status of each
🍌
Step 5
Contain
PEP with MMR within 72h or IVIG within 6 days for eligible contacts
📋
Measles is a nationally notifiable disease. In Texas, report to DSHS within 24 hours of suspected diagnosis. Do not wait for laboratory confirmation. Public health will guide contact tracing, PEP, and community communication.
Contact Tracing Window
Identify all contacts from 4 days before rash onset. Includes the waiting room, hallways, triage area. Susceptible contacts: MMR within 72h or immune globulin within 6 days.
Staff Immunity
All HCWs should have documented immunity: 2 MMR doses, positive measles IgG, or born before 1957. Exposed unvaccinated staff excluded from duty days 5–21 post-exposure.
Post-Exposure Prophylaxis
MMR within 72h prevents measles in susceptible contacts. IVIG within 6 days for those who cannot receive live vaccines. Coordinate with public health immediately.
Interprofessional Education

Teamwork in the Escape Room

Know your role — and know theirs. Effective interprofessional teams communicate early, share information, and support each other under pressure.

🩺
Physician / Resident
Clinical Lead
  • Clinical recognition and diagnosis
  • Orders airborne isolation
  • Vitamin A dosing decision
  • Escalation decision-making
  • Public health notification
  • SBAR to senior team
🩹
Nurse / Nursing Student
Safety & Monitoring
  • First to don PPE and enter room
  • Vital signs and monitoring
  • Medication administration
  • IV access and hydration
  • Family communication
  • Exposure list documentation
🩸
Respiratory Therapy
Airway & Breathing
  • Leads PPE training for team
  • N95 seal check verification
  • Oxygen delivery optimization
  • Respiratory assessment
  • Nebulizer setup in isolation
  • Escalation to HFNC support
👥
All Team Members
Shared Responsibilities
  • Speak up if something is missed
  • Call out errors respectfully
  • Use closed-loop communication
  • Support patient and family
  • Document your role in exposure
  • Debrief honestly after sim
💬
Closed-loop communication rule: When a task is assigned, the receiver repeats it back. When it is completed, the receiver reports back. “I’m putting the patient in airborne isolation now” → “Confirmed, airborne isolation initiated, door closed, team notified.”

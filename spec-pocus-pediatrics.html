Pediatric Point-of-Care Ultrasound

A practical teaching page for learners and frontline pediatric clinicians, focused on cardiac POCUS and lung ultrasound in children. This page includes real open-license ultrasound examples together with bedside teaching pearls for pediatric critical care education.

What learners should leave with

  • How to obtain a focused pediatric cardiac scan using 5 core views
  • How to recognize pericardial effusion, ventricular function patterns, and right-heart strain clues
  • How to interpret pediatric lung ultrasound across common respiratory conditions

Educational note: Pediatric POCUS answers focused bedside questions. It does not replace comprehensive pediatric echocardiography or formal radiology imaging when a full diagnostic study is needed.

Overview

In children, point-of-care ultrasound is most useful when it is tied to a clinical question. This page teaches a structured approach to pediatric cardiac and lung ultrasound so that learners can recognize high-yield patterns, avoid common mistakes, and integrate imaging into real bedside decision making.

Why it matters

Fast bedside information

POCUS can rapidly answer whether a child has pericardial fluid, gross ventricular dysfunction, pleural effusion, consolidation, pneumothorax, or diffuse interstitial edema.

Best use

Focused clinical questions

Pediatric POCUS works best when learners know exactly what they are looking for: fluid, function, strain, sliding, B-lines, consolidation, or effusion.

Teaching goal

Pattern recognition first

Before advanced measurements, learners should master normal views, clear abnormal patterns, and the language to describe what they see with confidence and humility.

Part I. Pediatric Cardiac POCUS

Teach this as focused cardiac ultrasound, not as a complete pediatric echocardiogram. The purpose is to help learners answer urgent bedside questions in shock, respiratory failure, cardiac arrest, or hemodynamic instability.

Useful for

What focused cardiac POCUS can answer well

  • Is there a pericardial effusion?
  • Does the LV look globally hyperdynamic, normal, or depressed?
  • Does the RV look enlarged or pressure-loaded?
  • Does the heart look empty, full, or failing?
  • Is there cardiac activity during arrest?
Not for

What cardiac POCUS should not replace

  • Formal congenital heart disease evaluation
  • Detailed valve assessment
  • Formal pulmonary hypertension workup
  • Shunt quantification or gradient measurement
  • Comprehensive pediatric echocardiography

The 5 core beginner views

These five views are enough for a high-yield focused pediatric cardiac teaching exam.

Below are real open-license examples. A few are classic adult teaching files because openly reusable pediatric cardiac images are limited.

View 1

Parasternal Long-Axis

Look for pericardial fluid, global LV squeeze, and gross chamber relationships.

View 2

Parasternal Short-Axis

Assess LV contraction, septal shape, and whether the RV looks oversized or pressure-loaded.

View 3

Apical 4-Chamber

Compare RV and LV size, check global contractility, and look for gross right-heart enlargement.

View 4

Subcostal 4-Chamber

Often an excellent pediatric window for pericardial effusion and a fast global function check.

View 5

IVC / Subcostal Sagittal

Use as one preload clue only. Teach learners not to overinterpret the IVC in isolation.

Pearl

Subcostal views are high-yield

In many younger children, subcostal windows are among the fastest and most useful views at the bedside.

The 4-question interpretation framework

This simple framework keeps the bedside exam clinically useful and teachable.

1. Is there pericardial fluid?

Teach learners to identify anechoic fluid around the heart and to connect fluid with the clinical picture rather than calling tamponade from imaging alone.

2. Is the LV squeezing well?

Teach broad categories: hyperdynamic, normal, or depressed. Learners do not need complex formulas before they can recognize a clearly weak ventricle.

3. Is the RV enlarged or pressure-loaded?

Focus on gross RV size, septal flattening, and right-sided chamber prominence rather than trying to make a formal pulmonary hypertension diagnosis from one view.

4. Does the heart look empty, full, or failing?

Use chamber size, squeeze, venous clues, and the overall clinical picture to frame shock physiology at the bedside.

Clinical syndrome Common cardiac POCUS pattern Teaching point
Hypovolemia Small chambers, vigorous squeeze, low-preload appearance Teach that this is a pattern, not a diagnosis in isolation.
Cardiogenic shock Depressed global function, larger chambers, pulmonary edema may coexist Pair cardiac and lung POCUS for stronger bedside reasoning.
Obstructive shock Pericardial effusion or marked right-heart strain Look for fluid, RV enlargement, and septal flattening.
Septic shock Hyperdynamic early or depressed later; often mixed physiology Teach learners that septic shock can evolve over time.

Teaching RV function and pulmonary hypertension clues

For pediatric learners, keep this practical. Teach gross RV enlargement, septal flattening, decreased RV shortening, and right atrial enlargement as clues that should raise concern.

A measurable tricuspid regurgitation jet can be helpful when visible, but not every child will have one, and pediatric pulmonary hypertension is not diagnosed by a single quick POCUS number.

Common mistake

Overcalling effusion

Epicardial fat and off-axis images can mimic fluid. Teach learners to look in more than one view.

Common mistake

Using IVC alone

In children, the IVC is one clue, not a stand-alone dehydration test.

Common mistake

Trying to do too much

Focused cardiac ultrasound should answer a narrow bedside question, not become a rushed incomplete echo.

Part II. Pediatric Lung POCUS

Lung ultrasound is one of the highest-yield pediatric POCUS skills because many important abnormalities reach the pleural surface. It is especially useful in respiratory distress, pneumonia, atelectasis, pleural effusion, pulmonary edema, trauma, and postoperative care.

Normal

Lung sliding

Visible shimmering movement of the pleural line with respiration.

Normal

A-lines

Horizontal reverberation artifacts consistent with aerated lung.

Abnormal

B-lines

Vertical comet-tail artifacts that reach the bottom of the screen and move with the pleura.

Abnormal

Consolidation

Tissue-like lung with possible air bronchograms and surrounding pleural irregularity.

High-yield lung teaching pearl

Start by teaching normal lung before teaching disease. Learners become much stronger when they can confidently identify the pleural line, sliding, and A-lines before moving on to B-lines and consolidation.

In younger children, do not forget the posterior lung fields. Important dependent atelectasis and effusions are easy to miss if only the anterior chest is scanned.

Pitfall

Scanning too few zones

A fast scan should still be systematic. Missing posterior zones means missing common pediatric findings.

Pitfall

Overcalling isolated B-lines

A single B-line is not the same as diffuse interstitial syndrome. Teach distribution and density, not just presence.

Pitfall

Assuming normal excludes disease

A normal or near-normal scan does not exclude all pathology, but it can help narrow the bedside differential meaningfully.

Part III. Integrating Heart and Lung POCUS

The most useful bedside ultrasound reasoning in pediatrics often comes from combining focused heart and lung findings rather than interpreting either in isolation.

Shock

Ask four things

  • Is the LV weak, normal, or hyperdynamic?
  • Is the RV enlarged or pressure-loaded?
  • Is there pericardial fluid?
  • Are the lungs wet, dry, consolidated, or collapsed?
Respiratory failure

Frame the differential

  • Normal-appearing lungs with wheeze may support asthma while you exclude other causes.
  • Diffuse B-lines plus poor LV function suggest cardiogenic physiology.
  • Patchy B-lines with irregular pleura suggest inflammatory or noncardiogenic disease.
Pulmonary vascular disease

Clues, not a full diagnosis

  • RV enlargement
  • Septal flattening
  • Right atrial prominence
  • Lung findings that help explain oxygenation and pressure load

Part IV. Suggested Teaching Plan

This structure works well for residents, fellows, APPs, nurses, respiratory therapists, and interprofessional PICU teaching sessions.

Begin with the question, not the probe

Teach learners to start with a bedside question: fluid, function, strain, edema, effusion, pneumothorax, consolidation, or collapse.

Teach normal before abnormal

Show multiple normal pediatric clips first. Confidence with normal anatomy and normal artifacts prevents overcalling disease later.

Use a limited high-yield protocol

Cardiac: 5 views. Lung: anterior, lateral, and posterior zones. This keeps teaching reproducible and practical.

Move from pattern to syndrome

After teaching what a pattern looks like, connect it to common pediatric syndromes: pneumonia, bronchiolitis, asthma, effusion, edema, tamponade, myocarditis, and shock.

Finish with integrated cases

Use short cases like septic shock, myocarditis, bronchiolitis, postoperative respiratory distress, tamponade, or pulmonary hypertension to help learners apply both heart and lung POCUS together.

Sample cardiac cases
  • Pericardial effusion with hemodynamic instability
  • Myocarditis with depressed LV function
  • Septic shock with evolving myocardial dysfunction
  • RV strain clues in severe pulmonary vascular disease
Sample lung cases
  • Lobar pneumonia with dynamic air bronchograms
  • Postoperative atelectasis with lung pulse
  • Bronchiolitis with patchy B-lines and small subpleural changes
  • Pulmonary edema versus ARDS pattern comparison

Key Take-Home Messages

Focused is better

The most reliable pediatric POCUS exams answer a narrow bedside question clearly.

Pattern recognition first

Before advanced measurements, learners should master normal views and unmistakable abnormal findings.

Lung POCUS is high yield

In children, lung ultrasound often offers rapid, bedside answers with immediate clinical relevance.

Know the limits

POCUS is a powerful adjunct, not a replacement for formal pediatric imaging when a complete study is needed.

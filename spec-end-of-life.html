When Your Child Is Dying Naturally

Choosing comfort for your child is not giving up. This page is here to help families understand what they may see, how comfort is protected, and how the hospital team, chaplain, and community can walk with them through an impossibly hard time.

Choosing comfort is not choosing less love.

When more machines, procedures, or medicines are no longer helping in the way your child needs, some families choose care focused on comfort, peace, dignity, and closeness. Your child will still receive careful medical care. The focus becomes relief of suffering and support for your family.

Many parents feel guilt, fear, doubt, anger, sadness, or confusion. These feelings are common. They do not mean you are making the wrong decision.

Who can help

  • Bedside nurses
  • PICU doctors and advanced practice providers
  • Palliative care team
  • Chaplain or spiritual care
  • Child life specialists
  • Social workers
  • Bereavement and grief support
  • Your own pastor, priest, minister, deacon, or faith leader

A gentle guide for families

This page is meant to help parents, grandparents, siblings, and loved ones prepare for what may happen when a child is dying naturally in the PICU. It does not replace bedside conversations. Instead, it gives families a steady place to return to when they need simple words, honest guidance, and reassurance.

What choosing comfort means

Choosing comfort means the team focuses on keeping your child as peaceful and comfortable as possible. This is still active care. It is not abandonment. It is not “doing nothing.” It is care shaped around your child’s comfort and your family’s goals.

What the team still does

  • Treats pain, breathing discomfort, anxiety, agitation, and distress
  • Adjusts medications and the room environment to reduce suffering
  • Makes room for touch, prayer, music, quiet, and family presence
  • Continues honest communication and bedside support

What families often call this

Some say comfort-focused care. Some say allowing a natural death. Some say, “We want peace for our child.” There is no one perfect phrase. What matters most is that the care matches your child and your values.

Words many parents need to hear

You are not causing your child’s death by making a comfort-focused plan.
Guilt is common. Guilt does not mean you made the wrong decision.
2

How children and siblings may understand death

Children understand death differently depending on age, development, personality, and past experience. Honest, simple words are often kinder than silence. Siblings also need support and reassurance that they did not cause this.

Helpful words

  • “Your body is getting weaker.”
  • “We are going to focus on keeping you comfortable.”
  • “When a person dies, their body stops working.”
  • “You did not cause this.”
  • “We will stay with you.”

Words to use carefully

Very young children may misunderstand phrases such as “going to sleep,” “passing away,” or “we lost him.” A child life specialist, chaplain, social worker, or bedside team can help you find words that fit your child and family.

What you may see as your child is dying naturally

Some changes can be hard to see, but many are part of the body’s natural process of slowing down. The team will help you understand what you are seeing and will treat anything that looks uncomfortable.

Common physical changes

  • Sleeping more and waking less
  • Less eating and drinking
  • Cooler hands and feet
  • Less urine output
  • Changes in skin color

Breathing and awareness

  • Different breathing patterns, including pauses
  • Noisy breathing as swallowing and coughing become weaker
  • Restlessness or confusion at times
  • Becoming less responsive near the end
Some of these changes can be upsetting to watch. Many are normal near the end of life.

Please tell us anytime something worries you. Families should never feel they have to silently carry fear or uncertainty at the bedside.

4

How we keep your child comfortable

Comfort care means doing the things that matter most now: relieving suffering, protecting dignity, and making room for love.

Medical comfort support

  • Medicines for pain
  • Medicines to ease shortness of breath or air hunger
  • Medicines to reduce anxiety, agitation, or distress
  • Careful reassessment if families feel their child looks uncomfortable

Gentle bedside care

  • Repositioning, mouth care, and skin care
  • A quieter room when possible
  • Fewer interruptions when appropriate
  • Music, blankets, familiar items, touch, prayer, or silence

What parents can say

  • “I want my child comfortable.”
  • “I’m afraid my child is suffering.”
  • “Please tell me what you are seeing.”
  • “Can you stay with us?”
5

What may happen at the time of death

Near the very end, breathing may become irregular, with longer pauses between breaths. Your child may not respond, even though you can still talk, pray, sing, and hold them. Sometimes death comes quietly. Sometimes there are several pauses before a final breath.

What families may choose

  • Who they want in the room
  • Whether they want prayer or spiritual support
  • Whether they want music or silence
  • Whether they want to hold their child
  • Whether siblings should be present, with preparation

What the bedside team does

  • Stays nearby and explains what is happening
  • Protects privacy and calm when possible
  • Continues comfort support
  • Helps families move at their own pace
6

Faith, family, and community support

Families in West Texas draw strength in many different ways. Some want prayer at the bedside. Some want a pastor, priest, chaplain, or church family present. Some want quiet. Some want a room full of loved ones. Some want only a few people nearby. There is no one right way.

We will honor your family’s way

We will respect your family’s culture, traditions, faith, and values. Some families want scripture, blessing, anointing, song, or silence. Some want grandparents, godparents, church members, or chosen family nearby. Some want private time. All of these are valid.

Support to ask for

  • Chaplain or spiritual care
  • Your own faith leader
  • Social work support
  • Child life for siblings
  • Palliative care consultation
  • Help contacting loved ones
7

Memory making, guilt, and after-death support

Love does not stop because time is short. Many families find comfort in creating memories and in having gentle support after death.

Ideas for memory making

  • Holding your child
  • Family photos
  • Handprints or footprints
  • A lock of hair
  • Favorite blanket or outfit
  • Reading a letter aloud
  • Prayer, blessing, or favorite song

What parents often feel

Many parents wonder whether they are giving up or making the wrong choice. These thoughts are common. Choosing comfort does not mean you failed your child. It often means you are loving your child in one of the hardest and most protective ways a parent can.

After your child dies

You will not be left alone after your child dies. The hospital team can help with time at the bedside, keepsakes, spiritual support, help for siblings and family members, practical next steps, and grief resources. Some families want support right away. Some need it later. Both are normal.

Optional video support

If you need words right now

Families in crisis often need simple phrases more than long explanations. These are okay to say at the bedside:

  • “I want my child comfortable.”
  • “Please tell me what to expect.”
  • “Can someone explain this to our other children?”
  • “Can we have a chaplain or our own faith leader here?”
  • “Can we have help making memories?”
  • “Can you stay with us?”

Learning Objectives — Module 1
By the end of this module, the learner will be able to:
  • Perform a systematic pediatric neurological examination including mental status, cranial nerves, motor, sensory, reflexes, and cerebellar function
  • Identify age-appropriate modifications for infants and young children at each stage of the neurological exam
  • Localize neurological lesions based on examination findings using anatomical reasoning
  • Recognize key patterns in the neurological exam: upper motor neuron (UMN) vs lower motor neuron (LMN), anterior vs posterior circulation syndromes

The Mental Status Examination

The mental status examination is the first component of the neurological exam and provides critical information about cortical and subcortical function. In pediatric patients, it must be adapted to the child's developmental stage. The exam assesses level of consciousness, orientation, attention, memory, language, and higher cortical function.

Mini Mental State Exam (MMSE)

The MMSE is a 30-point standardized screening tool (adapted from Rovner and Folstein, 1987) widely used to assess cognitive function. While originally designed for adults, components can be adapted for older children and adolescents. The total score of 30 is divided across the following domains:

Domain Max Points Components
Orientation 10 Time orientation (5 pts: year, season, date, day, month) and place orientation (5 pts: state, county, town, hospital/building, floor)
Registration 3 Name 3 objects; patient repeats all 3 (1 pt each)
Attention & Calculation 5 Serial 7s (subtract from 100) or spell "WORLD" backwards (1 pt per correct response)
Recall 3 Recall 3 objects named during Registration (1 pt each)
Language 8 Naming (2 pts), repetition (1 pt), 3-step command (3 pts), reading (1 pt), writing (1 pt)
Visual Construction 1 Copy intersecting pentagons
Pediatric Adaptations for Mental Status
Level of consciousness: For young children, ask "How old are you?" instead of month/year orientation questions.

Following commands: Use "Show me your nose" or "Touch your nose" instead of complex hand grip commands for toddlers and preschoolers.

Language assessment: Observe spontaneous speech, naming of objects and pictures, and ability to follow simple vs. multi-step commands appropriate to developmental level.

General observation: In infants, assess alertness, visual tracking, social smile, and response to voice and stimuli rather than formal MMSE components.

Clinical Pearl — Mental Status in Children
The most important part of the pediatric mental status exam happens before you formally examine the child. Watch them in the waiting room. Are they interacting with their parent? Making eye contact? Playing? An infant who is alert, visually tracking, and socially engaged has a fundamentally different mental status than one who is lethargic and unresponsive to the environment — and you can determine this in the first 30 seconds of the encounter.

Cranial Nerves I–XII

A systematic cranial nerve examination is essential for localizing lesions within the brainstem, cranial nerve pathways, and supranuclear structures. Each of the twelve cranial nerves has specific functions and testing methods.

Clinical Pearl — UMN vs LMN Facial Weakness
This is one of the most important localizing distinctions in clinical neurology. In an UMN lesion (e.g., stroke affecting the motor cortex or internal capsule), the forehead is spared because it receives bilateral cortical innervation — only the lower face is weak contralaterally. In an LMN lesion (e.g., Bell's palsy), the entire ipsilateral face is weak — both upper and lower — because the facial nerve nucleus or peripheral nerve itself is damaged. A child who cannot close their eye on the affected side has an LMN pattern.
Cranial Nerve Exam — Pediatric Tips
Infants: Use a bright toy or light for visual tracking (CN II, III, IV, VI). Observe for facial symmetry during crying (CN VII). Assess suck and swallow (CN V, VII, IX, X, XII). Startle response to sound (CN VIII).

Toddlers: Have the child imitate facial expressions (big smile, blow out cheeks). Use toys to test extraocular movements. Observe chewing during a snack (CN V motor).

School-age: Full formal cranial nerve exam is usually achievable with cooperation. Make it a game — "Can you stick out your tongue and wiggle it?"

The Motor Examination

The motor examination assesses muscle bulk, tone, strength, and involuntary movements. It is critical for distinguishing upper motor neuron (UMN) from lower motor neuron (LMN) lesions, as well as for localizing lesions to specific nerve roots, peripheral nerves, or muscle.

Clinical Pearl — Erb's Palsy
Erb's palsy results from upper trunk (C5–C6) damage, typically from birth trauma with lateral traction on the neck during delivery. The infant presents in the classic "waiter's tip" position: the arm is adducted and internally rotated at the shoulder, the elbow is extended, the forearm is pronated, and the wrist is flexed. The affected muscles (deltoid, biceps, brachioradialis, supraspinatus, infraspinatus) all receive C5–C6 innervation. Biceps reflex is absent.
Clinical Pearl — Klumpke's Palsy
Klumpke's palsy results from C8–T1 (lower trunk) damage, often from upward traction on the arm during delivery or from a grabbing reflex mechanism. It produces a "claw hand" with weakness of the intrinsic hand muscles (interossei, lumbricals, thenar, hypothenar). When T1 is involved, the sympathetic chain may be disrupted, producing an ipsilateral Horner syndrome (ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis). This is much less common than Erb's palsy.
Clinical Pearl — Wrist Drop (Radial Nerve Palsy)
Radial nerve damage — classically from compression against the humerus during prolonged pressure ("Saturday night palsy") — produces wrist drop because the wrist extensors (ECRL, ECRB, ECU) are paralyzed. The patient cannot extend the wrist or fingers at the MCP joints. Importantly, finger extension at the IP joints (mediated by lumbricals/interossei via ulnar and median nerves) is preserved. Sensation is lost over the dorsal first web space (superficial branch of radial nerve).
Clinical Pearl — Carpal Tunnel Syndrome
Carpal tunnel syndrome is the most common peripheral nerve entrapment. The median nerve is compressed within the carpal tunnel at the wrist. Key findings include thenar eminence atrophy (abductor pollicis brevis, opponens pollicis), weakness of thumb opposition and abduction, and sensory loss over the palmar aspect of the thumb, index, middle, and radial half of the ring finger. Tinel sign (tapping over the carpal tunnel) and Phalen sign (sustained wrist flexion) may reproduce symptoms. In children, it is rare but can occur with mucopolysaccharidoses, juvenile idiopathic arthritis, or after distal radius fractures.

The Sensory Examination

The sensory exam tests the integrity of peripheral nerves, nerve roots, the spinal cord, and sensory pathways in the brainstem and cortex. It is the most subjective component of the neurological examination and requires patient cooperation — which makes it particularly challenging in young children.

Modalities and Pathways

Dorsal Columns / Medial Lemniscus
Fine Touch, Vibration, Proprioception
Tests: Light touch (cotton wisp), vibration (128 Hz tuning fork), joint position sense (proprioception), two-point discrimination

Pathway: Ipsilateral dorsal columns → decussation at medulla → medial lemniscus → contralateral thalamus → cortex

Key point: Lesion below the decussation → ipsilateral loss
Spinothalamic Tract
Pain, Temperature, Crude Touch
Tests: Pin prick (disposable safety pin), temperature (cold tuning fork or test tube)

Pathway: Enters spinal cord → crosses within 1–2 levels → ascends in contralateral spinothalamic tract → thalamus → cortex

Key point: Lesion in the cord → contralateral loss below the level (crossed pathway)

In the pediatric sensory exam, reliability depends on the child's age and cooperation. For infants, observe withdrawal responses to light touch and pin prick. For toddlers, make it a game: "Close your eyes — tell me where I'm touching you!" For older children, a systematic dermatomal survey can be performed.

Clinical Pearl — Brown-Séquard Syndrome
A hemisection of the spinal cord (e.g., penetrating trauma, tumor) produces the classic Brown-Séquard pattern: ipsilateral UMN weakness and loss of dorsal column function (proprioception, vibration, fine touch) below the lesion, combined with contralateral loss of spinothalamic function (pain, temperature) beginning 1–2 levels below the lesion. This dissociated pattern is one of the most elegant lesion localizations in clinical neurology.

Reflexes

Deep tendon reflexes (DTRs) are stretch reflexes that test the integrity of the reflex arc: sensory afferent → spinal cord segment → motor efferent → muscle. They are graded on a standard scale from 0 (absent) to 4+ (clonus). The reflex examination is one of the most reliable and objective components of the neurological exam, even in young children.

Grading Scale

Grade Description
0Absent
1+Diminished (present only with reinforcement)
2+Normal
3+Brisk (may or may not be pathological)
4+Clonus (sustained or unsustained) — pathological

Plantar Response (Babinski Sign)

The plantar response is elicited by stroking the lateral sole of the foot from the heel toward the toes. A normal response in individuals older than approximately 12–24 months is downgoing (flexion of the great toe). An upgoing response (extension of the great toe with fanning of the other toes) — the Babinski sign — is pathological and indicates UMN dysfunction in individuals beyond infancy. In neonates and young infants, an upgoing toe is a normal finding because the corticospinal tract is not yet fully myelinated.

Clinical Tip
Getting Reliable Reflexes in Children
Children are often anxious about the reflex hammer. Let them hold it first. Demonstrate on yourself or on the parent. For the very anxious child, use a finger tap instead of the hammer. Always compare sides — the most important finding is asymmetry, not the absolute grade. Use Jendrassik maneuver (have the patient hook fingers and pull) if reflexes seem globally diminished — this differentiates truly absent reflexes from those suppressed by anxiety.

Cerebellar Function

The cerebellum coordinates voluntary movement, maintains balance, and regulates muscle tone. Cerebellar dysfunction produces a characteristic set of findings: ataxia, dysmetria, dysdiadochokinesia, intention tremor, and impaired balance. The cerebellar exam is particularly important in pediatric neurology because acute cerebellar ataxia is a common presentation in children.

Components of the Cerebellar Exam

Test What It Assesses Abnormal Finding
Finger-to-nose Limb coordination, dysmetria Past-pointing (overshooting the target), intention tremor that worsens as finger approaches target
Heel-to-shin Lower limb coordination Inability to smoothly run heel down opposite shin; side-to-side deviation
Rapid alternating movements Dysdiadochokinesia Irregular rhythm and amplitude when rapidly alternating pronation/supination of hands
Romberg test Sensory ataxia vs cerebellar ataxia Positive Romberg (falls with eyes closed) = sensory/proprioceptive ataxia, NOT cerebellar. Cerebellar ataxia is present with eyes open and closed
Gait assessment Truncal ataxia, balance Wide-based, staggering gait; inability to tandem walk (heel-to-toe); veering to one side suggests ipsilateral cerebellar lesion
Speech Cerebellar dysarthria Scanning, staccato speech with irregular rhythm and volume (explosive dysarthria)
Clinical Pearl — Midline vs Lateral Cerebellar Lesions
Midline (vermis) cerebellar lesions primarily produce truncal ataxia and gait instability — the child cannot sit or stand steadily but may perform finger-to-nose relatively well. This pattern is classically seen in medulloblastoma (midline posterior fossa tumor) in children. Lateral (hemispheric) cerebellar lesions produce ipsilateral limb ataxia — dysmetria, intention tremor, dysdiadochokinesia — on the same side as the lesion. Remember: the cerebellum is ipsilateral — it controls the same side of the body.
Cerebellar Exam in Young Children
Formal finger-to-nose testing requires cooperation that toddlers may not provide. Instead, observe the child reaching for toys — does the reach overshoot or undershoot? Watch them stack blocks — is there dysmetria? Have them kick a ball — is there lower limb coordination? Observe their gait for wide-based walking, frequent falls, or truncal sway. These functional assessments provide cerebellar information without requiring formal testing.

Pediatric Adaptations & Reference Values

The neurological examination of children must account for the ongoing maturation of the nervous system. Many findings that would be pathological in adults are normal in infants (e.g., upgoing plantar response, brisk reflexes, inconsistent tone patterns). Conversely, developmental milestones provide neurological information that is unavailable in adult examinations.

Clinical Pearl — Neonatal CSF Interpretation
A CSF WBC count of 15 cells/μL in a term neonate is within the normal range — do not reflexively call this meningitis. Conversely, normal neonatal CSF protein can be as high as 170 mg/dL; an elevated protein that would be alarming in an older child may be physiologic in a term newborn. Always interpret CSF values using age-appropriate reference ranges. The CSF-to-serum glucose ratio (normally >0.6 in neonates, >0.5 in older children) is often more informative than the absolute CSF glucose value.

Clinical Tip
The Observation-First Approach in Pediatric Neuro Exam
In infants and toddlers, the most valuable neurological information often comes from observation before you touch the child. Watch the infant's spontaneous movements (are they symmetric?), posture (frog-legged = hypotonia), visual tracking (does the child follow you across the room?), and interaction with the environment. Once you begin handling the baby, you can assess tone (pull-to-sit maneuver for head lag, ventral suspension for truncal tone), primitive reflexes, and deep tendon reflexes. Save the most intrusive parts of the exam (fundoscopy, plantar response) for last.

Case Vignettes

Case 1 — Cranial Nerve Localization
Acute Facial Weakness in a 4-Year-Old
A 4-year-old girl presents with acute onset right facial droop noticed by her parents this morning. On examination, she cannot close her right eye completely, cannot raise her right eyebrow, and has drooling from the right corner of her mouth. Her smile is asymmetric with flattening of the right nasolabial fold. The left side of her face moves normally. She is afebrile, alert, and otherwise neurologically intact.

Question: Is this an upper motor neuron (UMN) or lower motor neuron (LMN) pattern of facial weakness? What is the most likely diagnosis?

Localization: This is a lower motor neuron (LMN) pattern of facial weakness. The key evidence is that both the upper and lower face are involved on the right — she cannot close her eye (orbicularis oculi, upper face) AND cannot smile properly (lower face). In an UMN lesion (e.g., cortical stroke), the forehead is spared because it receives bilateral cortical innervation — only the contralateral lower face is weak.

Diagnosis: The most likely diagnosis is Bell's palsy (idiopathic acute peripheral facial nerve palsy, CN VII). It is the most common cause of acute unilateral facial weakness in children. The presentation is typically sudden onset, reaching maximal weakness within 48–72 hours.

Key teaching point: Always test the forehead — it is the single most important maneuver for distinguishing UMN from LMN facial weakness. An UMN pattern (forehead spared) in a child warrants urgent neuroimaging to evaluate for stroke or mass lesion.

Case 2 — Motor Localization
Progressive Proximal Weakness in an 8-Year-Old
An 8-year-old boy is referred for progressive bilateral leg weakness over the past year. His parents note he has difficulty climbing stairs, frequently falls, and has trouble keeping up with peers during physical activities. On examination, he uses his hands on his thighs to push himself up from a seated position on the floor (Gowers sign positive). He has bilateral proximal lower extremity weakness (hip flexors 3/5, quadriceps 4/5) with relatively preserved distal strength. His calves appear enlarged bilaterally. Reflexes are diminished at the knees. Sensation is intact throughout.

Question: Where is the lesion? What investigation should be ordered first?

Localization: The pattern of progressive proximal weakness, pseudohypertrophy of the calves, positive Gowers sign, and diminished reflexes with intact sensation localizes to the muscle itself — this is a myopathy, not a neuropathy or UMN lesion.

Diagnosis: The clinical picture is classic for Duchenne muscular dystrophy (DMD). DMD is an X-linked recessive dystrophinopathy that typically presents in boys aged 3–5 years with progressive proximal weakness, calf pseudohypertrophy, and Gowers sign. It is caused by mutations in the dystrophin gene on Xp21.

First investigation: Order a serum creatine kinase (CK) level. In DMD, CK is dramatically elevated — typically 10,000–50,000+ IU/L (normal <200 IU/L). This is the most sensitive initial screening test. Confirmatory diagnosis is via genetic testing for dystrophin gene mutations.

Key teaching point: Gowers sign indicates proximal hip girdle weakness and is not specific to DMD — it can be seen in any cause of proximal myopathy. However, in a young boy with calf pseudohypertrophy, it should immediately raise suspicion for DMD. The preserved sensation and the proximal-greater-than-distal pattern distinguish myopathy from neuropathy.

Self-Assessment

Three questions to test your understanding of neurological examination fundamentals. Click an answer to select it, then reveal the explanation.

Question 1 of 3
Which cranial nerve innervates the lateral rectus muscle, responsible for abduction of the eye?
  • A. CN III — Oculomotor
  • B. CN IV — Trochlear
  • C. CN VI — Abducens
  • D. CN V — Trigeminal
Correct Answer: C — CN VI (Abducens) The abducens nerve (CN VI) innervates the lateral rectus muscle, which abducts the eye (turns it laterally). The name "abducens" literally refers to abduction. A CN VI palsy produces impaired lateral gaze with esotropia (eye turns inward) and horizontal diplopia that worsens when looking toward the affected side. CN VI has the longest intracranial course of any cranial nerve, making it vulnerable to increased intracranial pressure — a "false localizing sign" in raised ICP.
Question 2 of 3
A newborn is found to have the arm adducted and internally rotated at the shoulder, the elbow extended, the forearm pronated, and the wrist flexed — the "waiter's tip" position. Which nerve roots are most likely injured?
  • A. C5–C6 (upper trunk) — Erb's palsy
  • B. C8–T1 (lower trunk) — Klumpke's palsy
  • C. C6–C7 (middle trunk)
  • D. C3–C5 (phrenic nerve)
Correct Answer: A — C5–C6 Upper Trunk (Erb's Palsy) Erb's palsy results from damage to the upper trunk of the brachial plexus (C5–C6), typically from lateral traction on the neck during difficult delivery. The "waiter's tip" position reflects loss of C5–C6 innervated muscles: the deltoid (shoulder abduction), biceps (elbow flexion), and brachioradialis/supinator (forearm supination) are paralyzed, while the C7–T1 innervated antagonists (adductors, extensors, pronators) pull the arm into the characteristic posture. Klumpke's palsy (option B) affects C8–T1 and produces a "claw hand" with intrinsic hand muscle weakness ± Horner syndrome.
Question 3 of 3
What is the normal cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) white blood cell count in a child older than 1 month of age?
  • A. <25 cells/μL
  • B. <15 cells/μL
  • C. <5 cells/μL
  • D. <50 cells/μL
Correct Answer: C — <5 cells/μL In children older than 1 month, the normal CSF WBC count is <5 cells/μL with 0% PMNs (mononuclear cells only). This is the standard reference value used across pediatric practice. In contrast, neonates have higher normal CSF WBC counts: up to 22 cells/μL in term neonates and up to 25 cells/μL in preterm neonates. Applying the <5 threshold to neonatal CSF would lead to overdiagnosis of meningitis, while applying neonatal norms to older children would lead to missed infections.
